## Argument

This section introduces the paper's central conceptual contribution: the distinction between capacity-building enhancement (interventions that strengthen durable agency, judgment, and self-regulation) and pseudo-enhancement (interventions that feel empowering but primarily increase pleasure, compliance, productivity, or dependency while narrowing autonomy). It then identifies a specific civilizational risk — that AI-accelerated markets will produce sophisticated pseudo-enhancement at scale — and proposes three evaluative criteria for telling the difference. This distinction structures the rest of the paper: Sections VIII–X evaluate interventions through this lens, and Section XIV confronts what happens when the line blurs.

## Evidence Bridge

1. The bioethics literature has long debated the therapy/enhancement distinction, but the standard framing (restoring normal function vs exceeding it) is increasingly recognized as insufficient. Earp et al. (2014) argue that enhancement should be defined by whether it increases the chances of leading a good life, not by whether it exceeds a species-typical baseline — context matters more than functional level.
2. This paper takes a different cut. The relevant question for AI governance is not therapy vs enhancement, but agency-building vs dependency-building. An intervention can be "enhancing" by any standard definition and still make people less capable of self-governance.
3. AI-accelerated drug discovery (Serrano et al. 2024) makes this distinction urgent. The same ecosystem that could produce genuine cognitive enhancement tools could also produce highly effective forms of sedation disguised as empowerment.
4. Greely et al. (2008) already noted the need for responsible use frameworks for cognitive-enhancing drugs — but wrote before AI dramatically accelerated the drug-discovery pipeline. The scale of what's coming requires sharper evaluative criteria than "responsible use."
5. Nyholm (2024) flags "quasi-enhancement" — AI that appears to extend cognition but may produce cognitive atrophy. Yadav (2025) formalizes this as "competency erosion." Both support the claim that not all apparent improvement is genuine.
6. The paper's three evaluative criteria (net effect on autonomous functioning, effect on metacognition/self-regulation, effect on underlying competence and resilience) give the distinction enough analytic teeth to be actionable without claiming to be a formal decision procedure. Crucially, they do not penalize ongoing external support — they ask whether such support expands or erodes functioning.

## Gaps

- A dedicated paper on pseudo-enhancement or dependency-producing interventions would strengthen P2. The concept is discussed across several literatures (addiction medicine, digital wellness, enhancement ethics) but no single canonical source.
- Could use a concrete historical example of technology marketed as empowering but producing dependency (e.g., opioid crisis, social media engagement optimization).

## Evidence Log

### Source: Earp et al. 2014 — When is diminishment a form of enhancement?
- **Core argument:** The treatment-enhancement distinction should be replaced with a welfarist framework. Enhancement is "any change in the biology or psychology of a person which increases the chances of leading a good life in a given set of circumstances."
- **Key insight:** "What it is that enhances well-being is not species-general, but rather context-specific" (Fine-Tuning section).
- **Relevance:** Supports the claim that the standard therapy/enhancement boundary is insufficient. But their welfarist framework doesn't address the specific governance question this paper raises: whether interventions build or erode the capacity for autonomous self-governance.

### Source: Serrano et al. 2024 — AI drug discovery and delivery
- **Core contribution:** AI is accelerating drug discovery, biomarker identification, and personalized drug delivery at unprecedented speed. This creates both opportunity (faster development of genuinely useful compounds) and risk (faster development of dependency-producing ones).
- **Relevance:** Makes the "chemical opium" risk concrete and timely rather than speculative.

### Source: Greely et al. 2008 — Responsible use of cognitive-enhancing drugs
- **Core argument:** Cognitive enhancement through pharmacology is not inherently unethical but requires responsible-use frameworks.
- **Relevance:** Establishes that the enhancement ethics conversation is mature enough to move beyond "should we?" to "which kinds, and how do we tell?"

### Source: Nyholm 2024 — AI and human enhancement
- **Key concept:** Distinguishes "genuine enhancement" from "quasi-enhancement" — AI that appears to extend cognition but may produce cognitive atrophy. Draws on extended mind theory.
- **Relevance:** Supports the claim that not all apparent cognitive improvement is genuine. His quasi-enhancement concept is close to our pseudo-enhancement, but framed philosophically rather than as a governance criterion.

### Source: Yadav 2025 — Cognitive sustainability in the age of AI
- **Key concept:** "Competency erosion" — AI systems substitute for core cognitive processes rather than merely extending them. "AI systems don't merely extend human cognition but potentially substitute for core cognitive processes."
- **Relevance:** Provides the diagnostic complement to our prescriptive distinction. His erosion model formalizes what happens when pseudo-enhancement goes unchecked.

## Draft (~800 words, 4 paragraphs)

The bioethics literature has long debated where therapy ends and enhancement begins, but the standard framing — restoring normal function versus exceeding it — is increasingly recognized as insufficient for the questions this paper raises. Enhancement is not a single category. As Earp et al. have argued, whether an intervention constitutes genuine improvement depends on context and consequences, not on whether it exceeds a species-typical baseline [@earp2014]. This paper proposes a different cut, one suited to the governance problem at hand. The relevant question is not whether an intervention crosses the therapy-enhancement boundary, but whether it builds durable human agency or deepens dependency. This cuts across the usual therapy-enhancement boundary: some therapeutic interventions may be strongly agency-building, while some nominal enhancements may function primarily as dependency-producing substitutes. Capacity-building enhancement, as used here, refers to interventions that strengthen judgment, self-regulation, metacognition, empathy, or resilience in ways that expand a person's capacity for autonomous action over time, whether through durable internal change, supportive scaffolding, or both. Pseudo-enhancement refers to interventions that feel empowering — improving mood, productivity, social confidence, or cognitive speed — while primarily increasing reliance on external systems, narrowing the range of autonomous functioning, or substituting transient performance for durable competence. The distinction is not between feeling better and being better in some moralistic sense; it is between interventions that leave people more capable of governing themselves and those that leave them less so. Not every externally supported intervention is pseudo-enhancement; assistive tools may expand agency when they scaffold functioning without degrading the underlying capacity for judgment, reflection, or self-direction.

This distinction is not merely conceptual. It identifies a concrete civilizational risk. As AI accelerates drug discovery, biomarker identification, and pharmacological personalization [@serrano2024], the market will have strong incentives to produce both genuine capacity-building tools and highly effective forms of what might be called chemical opium — dependency-producing substitutes optimized for engagement, compliance, pleasure, or productivity rather than for the hard-won gains in judgment, patience, and self-awareness that genuine stewardship requires. The same logic applies beyond pharmacology. AI-mediated systems that optimize for user engagement, behavioral compliance, and frictionless experience may produce populations that feel more empowered, more informed, and more connected while becoming progressively less capable of independent thought, sustained attention, or meaningful self-direction. Nyholm has flagged this dynamic in the context of AI and cognition, distinguishing genuine cognitive enhancement from "quasi-enhancement" that may produce cognitive atrophy rather than growth [@nyholm2024]. Yadav has formalized it further, arguing that AI systems increasingly substitute for core cognitive processes rather than merely extending them, producing "competency erosion" in individuals and organizations [@yadav2025]. The civilizational question, then, is not whether enhancement will exist, but whether societies can distinguish capacity-building from dependency-producing substitutes as both become more widely available.

This paper proposes three evaluative criteria for making that distinction, offered not as a formal decision procedure but as analytic tools with enough specificity to guide both research and policy. First, what is the net effect on autonomous functioning over time? Does the intervention expand the person's capacity for judgment and self-direction, or merely provide temporary substitution? A stimulant that improves focus for eight hours raises different questions depending on whether it scaffolds the development of attentional skill or simply substitutes for it. The key question is not whether continued support is needed — many genuine capacity-building interventions involve ongoing use — but whether autonomous functioning expands or contracts over the course of engagement. Second, what is the effect on metacognition and self-regulation? Does the intervention increase reflective control and understanding — the ability to monitor one's own cognition, recognize biases, manage emotional reactivity, and adjust behavior in light of reflection — or does it mainly reduce discomfort or increase compliance? An anxiolytic that suppresses distress may relieve suffering and in some cases restore baseline functioning, without necessarily, on its own, building the higher-order capacities relevant to stewardship. Third, what is the effect on underlying competence and resilience? Does the intervention scaffold growth, or create fragility, withdrawal vulnerability, or erosion of baseline capacity? The distinction here is not between dependent and independent functioning — many effective interventions involve continued external support — but between supportive scaffolding that expands what a person can do and substitution that erodes what they can do without it. These criteria are not bright lines. Many interventions will fall in ambiguous territory, and reasonable people will disagree about specific cases. But they give the distinction enough analytic teeth to move beyond vague appeals to "responsible enhancement" toward evaluable claims about what specific interventions actually do to human agency.

This distinction matters for every section that follows. Not all enhancement is stewardship enhancement. A system that makes people faster, more productive, or more emotionally comfortable is not necessarily making them wiser, more reflective, or more capable of governing powerful technologies. The paths forward examined in Sections VIII through X — therapeutic intervention, adult enhancement, and early education — are evaluated partly through this lens: do they build the capacities identified in Section VII, and do they do so in ways that satisfy the criteria proposed here? Section XIV confronts the dark side: what happens when the tools of capacity-building are repurposed for optimization, compliance, or control, and the line between agency-building and dependency-building becomes dangerously thin. The paper's claim is that this distinction — between interventions that expand human autonomy and those that narrow it while appearing to do the opposite — is not a secondary concern. It is the central evaluative question for any serious proposal to strengthen human capacity in the age of advanced AI.
