## Summary
Earp, Sandberg, Kahane, and Savulescu argue that the enhancement debate in biomedical ethics is overly focused on augmenting capacities (memory, attention, cognition), neglecting cases where *diminishing* a capacity could enhance overall well-being. They defend a "welfarist" conception of enhancement -- any change in biology or psychology that increases a person's chances of leading a good life -- and show it resolves definitional problems that plague augmentative and "not-medicine" approaches to the enhancement concept.

## Key Findings
- The dominant "functional-augmentative" approach to enhancement (more of a capacity = enhancement) fails to capture cases where reducing a capacity promotes well-being (e.g., blunting traumatic memories with propranolol, dampening attachment to an abuser, appetite suppression for obesity).
- The "not-medicine" (treatment vs. enhancement) distinction is similarly inadequate: it cannot cleanly classify interventions that diminish a function beyond the species-typical baseline but still promote flourishing.
- The welfarist definition resolves both problems: "Enhancement should be defined to mean any change in the biology or psychology of a person which increases the chances of leading a good life in a given set of circumstances."
- Diminishment is especially likely to be enhancing when evolutionary trade-offs have shifted (e.g., fight-or-flight responses maladaptive in modern life, immune overreaction causing allergies, hunger drives causing obesity).
- Even "obviously beneficial" capacities like empathy, intelligence, and memory can have maladaptive consequences when excessive (empathy fatigue, hyperthymesia, social costs of very high IQ).
- The welfarist approach ties enhancement to well-being while remaining neutral on substantive theories of the good life, and can accommodate novel capacities (e.g., magnetic implants) and body modifications that are neither augmentative nor diminishing.

## Methodology
Conceptual/philosophical analysis. The paper is a hypothesis-and-theory article in Frontiers in Systems Neuroscience. It draws on examples from neuropharmacology (propranolol, beta-blockers, Ritalin), neurotechnology (TMS), and clinical ethics (the Ashley case) to test and motivate the welfarist framework. No empirical data collection.

## Relevance to This Work
This paper is central to Section V (therapy/enhancement distinction). It provides the philosophical apparatus for arguing that cognitive augmentation and capacity-building need not be limited to "adding more" -- sometimes the most enhancing intervention involves rebalancing or reducing. This directly supports the manuscript's broader thesis that human capacity-building (including via contemplative practices, psychotherapy, and neurotechnology) should be understood through a welfarist lens focused on flourishing rather than raw capacity augmentation. The paper also provides precedent for discussing the blurriness of the therapy/enhancement boundary in ways that favor a governance strategy centered on well-being rather than categorical prohibitions.
