## Argument

This section addresses the more speculative tier of adult capacity-building: interventions that aim to expand healthy cognition rather than restore impaired function. It argues that while current enhancement tools are modest, AI is accelerating the trajectories that may produce more powerful ones — creating a paradox in which the same technology that threatens human capacity may also accelerate tools to strengthen it. The governance question is not only what works today, but whether governance attention is needed now to shape the direction of emerging tools.

## Evidence Bridge

1. Current standalone cognitive enhancement in healthy adults has a thin and contested evidence base — modest effects, high individual variability.
2. AI is accelerating drug discovery, neuroscience analysis, biomarker identification, and trial design (Serrano 2024), compressing timelines and enabling novel interventions.
3. This creates a paradox: AI may erode human capacity AND accelerate tools to strengthen it.
4. BCI illustrates the speed of this dynamic — moving from lab to clinical application rapidly (Cinel 2019).
5. The governance question (agency vs control) is already live and cannot wait for the technology to mature.
6. The agency-vs-dependency distinction from Section V applies with equal force to augmentative technologies.

## Draft (~700 words, 3 paragraphs)

Some interventions relevant to AI governance aim not merely to restore impaired function but to expand specific dimensions of healthy cognition. These include tools aimed at enhancing sustained attention, modulating emotional regulation, and, more speculatively, supporting metacognitive monitoring. Further on the horizon are attempts to augment cognition through direct brain-computer interfaces. This is the more speculative tier of the paper's argument, and it should be handled accordingly. The current evidence base for genuine cognitive augmentation in healthy adults is thinner and more contested than the therapeutic evidence reviewed in the preceding section. Most pharmacological cognitive enhancers show modest effects that vary significantly across individuals and tasks; most neurotechnological approaches remain early-stage. The honest assessment is that standalone cognitive enhancement, as currently available, does not yet offer reliable tools for strengthening stewardship capacities at scale. What it offers instead is a set of trajectories worth monitoring — and, crucially, worth shaping.

Here the central paradox of the paper becomes sharpest: AI may simultaneously undermine human capacity through dependency and accelerate the tools that might strengthen it. AI is transforming drug discovery by accelerating target identification, compound screening, and aspects of trial design, in some cases substantially compressing development timelines [@serrano2024]. AI is also being applied to neuroscience analysis, biomarker identification, and aspects of trial design in ways that may eventually support more precise and individualized interventions. The strategic question for this paper is therefore not only what enhancement tools work today, but what trajectories are opening and whether governance attention is needed now to shape their direction. Current tools remain limited, but AI may accelerate the trajectories along which more capable interventions emerge. This does not license speculation about future capabilities as though they were established facts. But it does mean that governance frameworks designed only around today's tools risk being overtaken by tomorrow's.

Brain-computer interfaces illustrate the speed and stakes of this dynamic. At present, the strongest BCI applications remain therapeutic and assistive rather than augmentative — primarily restoring lost function in motor control and communication [@cinel2019]. But the field is moving from laboratory demonstration toward a growing set of clinical applications, and the conceptual and technical boundary between therapeutic restoration and cognitive augmentation may narrow over time. The governance question is already live: will brain-computer interfaces be developed primarily toward agency, understanding, and autonomous self-regulation — or toward entertainment, productivity optimization, and behavioral control? This is not a question that can wait until the technology matures. The values embedded in early design choices, funding priorities, and regulatory frameworks will shape whether these tools become instruments of capacity-building or instruments of dependency. The same agency-versus-dependency distinction that structures this paper's evaluation of therapeutic intervention applies with equal force to augmentative technologies, where early design choices may shape long-term patterns of agency, dependence, and control.
