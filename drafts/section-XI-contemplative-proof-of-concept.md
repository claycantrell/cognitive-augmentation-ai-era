## Argument

Contemplative practice demonstrates that the capacities this paper identifies as critical for AI stewardship — metacognition, emotional regulation, attentional stability, compassion — are trainable rather than fixed. But traditional cultivation is slow, making it strategically insufficient under compressed timelines. Early work in contemplative neuroscience and targeted neuromodulation suggests some of the same neural systems may be technologically scaffolded, though the key unresolved question is whether transient modulation of networks associated with contemplative states can produce durable changes in judgment and behavior, rather than merely state-level phenomenological effects.

## Draft (~500 words, 3 paragraphs)

Contemplative practice provides a proof of concept that several of the capacities most relevant to AI stewardship can be deliberately cultivated rather than merely assumed. Decades of contemplative neuroscience have documented that experienced meditators exhibit increased prefrontal activity supporting executive function and planning, enhanced empathy and compassion, stronger metacognitive awareness, and reduced amygdala reactivity associated with less fear-driven decision-making [NEED: Fox et al. 2014 meta-analysis; Davidson & Lutz 2008]. These findings matter for the paper's argument because they suggest that the cluster of capacities identified in Section VII — long-term thinking, emotional regulation, metacognition, resistance to attentional capture — are not hardwired constraints but developmentally responsive traits. The strategic problem is that such changes typically unfold over years or decades of disciplined practice. Galante, Sanguinetti et al. documented that mindfulness is a developmental process whose advanced stages remain poorly understood scientifically, and that "limited evidence for very-long-term benefits comes from cross-sectional comparisons of mindfulness meditation beginners with either Buddhist monks or lay long-term Buddhist meditators" — populations with "very different lifestyles and personal histories than the average mindfulness meditator" [@galante2023, p. 1056]. Traditional contemplative training, while demonstrating the destination, cannot plausibly serve as a standalone response to compressed AI timelines.

Early work in contemplative neuroscience and targeted neuromodulation suggests that some of the neural systems associated with contemplative development may be technologically scaffolded. Sanguinetti et al. demonstrated that brief transcranial focused ultrasound to the right inferior frontal gyrus — a hub for inhibition and cognitive control — significantly improved mood and altered functional connectivity in healthy volunteers in a randomized, double-blind design [@sanguinetti2020]. Lord, Sanguinetti et al. subsequently showed that ultrasound targeting the posterior cingulate cortex, a central node of the default mode network, produced significant reductions in DMN connectivity and significant increases in state mindfulness on the Toronto Mindfulness Scale [@lord2024]. Wright, Sanguinetti, Young, and Sacchet have also developed a framework for translating contemplative states into testable neuroscientific hypotheses, suggesting that the target states of contemplative practice can be specified with enough precision to guide technological intervention [@wright2023]. These findings remain preliminary — the studies involve small samples, single sessions, and short-term outcomes — but they indicate that the relevant neural systems are not inaccessible to technological modulation.

The key unresolved question is whether transient modulation of networks associated with contemplative states can produce durable changes in judgment, self-regulation, and prosocial behavior, rather than merely state-level phenomenological effects. Neural correlates are not the same as lived wisdom; state changes are not the same as trait changes; and short-term modulation is not the same as durable character formation. The paper does not claim equivalence. What contemplative practice provides is evidence that the capacities this paper cares about are real and trainable. What early neuromodulation research provides is evidence that the timeline for accessing some of those capacities may eventually be compressible. Whether the shortcut produces the same quality of change remains an open and important question.
