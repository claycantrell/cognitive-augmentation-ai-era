## Draft (~700 words, 3 paragraphs)

A serious response to the human-capacity bottleneck cannot begin only in adulthood. Many of the capacities most relevant to AI stewardship — including metacognition, emotional regulation, empathy, and civic reasoning — are developmental achievements shaped substantially by early experience, schooling, and culture. They are not fixed at birth, but neither are they equally plastic across the lifespan; childhood and adolescence represent periods of particular opportunity for cultivation. This makes educational and developmental intervention potentially among the least coercive, most scalable, and institutionally legitimate forms of capacity-building available. Unlike pharmacological or neurotechnological approaches, which raise immediate questions about safety, access, and consent, educational programs operate within institutions that already exist, enjoy broad public support, and can in principle reach entire populations. The question is whether these programs can be designed to cultivate the specific capacities that matter for governance — not merely academic performance — and whether the evidence shows that they can.

On that question, the evidence is encouraging if uneven. A 2023 meta-analysis of social and emotional learning programs encompassing 424 studies across 53 countries and more than 575,000 students found that well-implemented SEL programs are associated with gains in social-emotional competence, prosocial behavior, and academic outcomes, with evidence that some benefits persist beyond the intervention period. Executive function — including working memory, inhibitory control, and cognitive flexibility — is widely understood as foundational to later self-regulation, planning, and adaptive behavior. Early interventions targeting executive function have shown promising effects, especially for children in high-stress environments where these capacities are most vulnerable. Mindfulness-based programs for children have shown effects on attentional control and emotional regulation, though the evidence base remains smaller and more variable than for SEL. What connects these approaches to this paper's argument is not any single program's effect size but the underlying principle: that capacities often treated as fixed personality traits — patience, self-regulation, reflective thinking, empathy — are in fact trainable, and trainable early. Immordino-Yang's work on civic reasoning and transcendent thinking offers a particularly direct bridge: she argues that the capacity to connect immediate experience to broader ethical and civic meaning is itself a developmental achievement, one that educational design can either support or neglect. If that capacity is governance-relevant — and this paper argues it is — then educational systems that cultivate it are doing governance work, whether or not they use that language. In this sense, educational intervention is not merely preparatory or preventive; it is a governance-relevant form of developmental infrastructure.

The strategic logic of educational intervention differs from adult approaches in ways that matter. Education is slower. A generation-scale investment in metacognitive development, social-emotional learning, and civic reasoning will not produce results in time to address near-term AI governance pressures. But what education offers in return is durability, equity, and political legitimacy that adult intervention struggles to match. Educational programs do not raise the same consent and bodily-intrusion issues as pharmacological or neurotechnological modification, even though they introduce their own questions about curriculum, authority, and influence; and they can be embedded in public institutions rather than distributed through markets that may favor access for the privileged. A serious capacity-building strategy therefore needs both layers: near-term adult intervention for the populations already making governance decisions, and longer-term developmental reform for the populations who will inherit the consequences. Neither alone is sufficient. The adult tier addresses urgency; the educational tier addresses depth. Together they constitute something closer to a complete response to the human-capacity bottleneck — provided both are evaluated by the same standard: not whether they improve performance, but whether they build the kind of agency that wise governance of advanced AI requires.
