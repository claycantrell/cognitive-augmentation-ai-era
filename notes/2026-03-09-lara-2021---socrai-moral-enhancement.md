## Summary

Francisco Lara argues that AI can be more effective than human instruction for moral enhancement, but *only* if the technology is designed to increase the individual's capacity for autonomous moral reasoning rather than directly influencing behavior. He critiques three existing AI-based moral enhancement proposals -- ethics machines (which replace human judgment), nudge-based systems (which manipulate via heuristics), and value-based advisors (which produce deference rather than reflection) -- finding that all three undermine personal autonomy to varying degrees. As an alternative, Lara proposes "SocrAI," a virtual assistant that uses Socratic dialogue, procedural neutrality, and virtual reality technologies to teach users to make better moral decisions on their own. SocrAI is an Artificial Moral Agent at Moor's Level 3 ("explicit ethical agent") that employs a hybrid architecture combining top-down procedural guidelines with bottom-up machine learning, designed to function as a conversational bot without a robotic body.

## Key Findings

- **Autonomy as the central criterion:** Moral enhancement is only genuine if it respects and promotes personal autonomy, defined as having two elements: (1) reflective identification with one's values through rational consideration, and (2) authenticity/self-control -- confidence to act on those values without identity discontinuities.

- **Three rejected approaches:**
  1. **Ethics machines:** Replace or override human decision-making. Users remain passive; "our role will always be largely passive and the reasons to behave morally will always come from the outside."
  2. **Nudge-based systems:** Technically preserve choice but manipulate through heuristic triggers. Risk of "hypernudging" -- AI creating "subtle, unobtrusive and tremendously powerful" influence by learning user preferences.
  3. **Value-based advisors** (e.g., Savulescu and Maslen): Users input values and receive recommendations, but without reflection on how values connect to conclusions, this produces "deference" rather than "deep reflection."

- **SocrAI's three differentiating features:**
  1. *Educational rather than behavioral:* "The aim of this assistant is not to directly and immediately alter the behaviour of the person." Instead, it develops independent critical moral decision-making capacity.
  2. *Full user participation:* Users play "a dominant role in the decision-making and learning process" through continuous dialogical interaction.
  3. *Value neutrality:* Operates through "procedural values of minimal and open rationality" -- logical competence, conceptual precision, empirical accuracy, cognitive empathy -- not substantive ethical doctrines.

- **Design safeguards against manipulation:** No humanoid/animal form (avoids emotional attachment), no "affective computing" techniques, transparent data sourcing, and use of cognitive empathy (perspective-taking) rather than compassionate empathy (emotional manipulation).

- **Virtual reality for perspective-taking:** Users embody different perspectives through VR avatars, but designed to represent *roles* rather than specific personalities, maintaining cognitive over emotional empathy.

- **SocrAI vs. human Socratic teacher:** SocrAI potentially superior in information processing speed, monitoring user states, neutrality (lacks evolutionarily shaped biases), availability (24/7, multilingual), and motivation (perceived reliability due to predictability).

## Methodology

Conceptual/philosophical analysis in applied AI ethics. The paper proceeds by: (1) establishing autonomy as the central criterion for evaluating moral enhancement proposals, (2) systematically critiquing three categories of existing AI moral enhancement approaches against this criterion, (3) designing SocrAI as an alternative that satisfies the autonomy requirement, (4) comparing SocrAI's capabilities to those of a hypothetical human Socratic instructor along three dimensions (teaching skill, neutrality, motivation), and (5) acknowledging limitations including user willingness, technology access, and dialogue quality.

## Relevance to This Work

**Directly relevant to how we frame AI's role in cognitive/moral enhancement for governance.**

1. **The autonomy vs. control framing is central to our paper:** Lara's taxonomy of approaches (ethics machines, nudges, value-advisors, SocrAI) provides a useful conceptual map for discussing how AI could enhance human governance capacity. Our paper should cite this taxonomy when arguing that the *right kind* of AI-assisted enhancement preserves and strengthens human agency rather than replacing it.

2. **SocrAI as a concrete enhancement modality:** The SocrAI concept exemplifies one pathway in our multi-modal portfolio -- AI-assisted moral and cognitive training that improves human reasoning capacity rather than substituting for it. This fits our argument that enhancement should make humans *better governors*, not remove them from the governance loop.

3. **Strengthens the anti-automation argument:** Lara's critique of ethics machines and nudge systems directly supports our claim that simply automating governance decisions (or nudging humans toward "correct" oversight actions) is insufficient. Genuine enhancement of human capacity is needed.

4. **Limitation relevant to our argument:** Lara acknowledges SocrAI depends on users *valuing* "the examined life" and willingly engaging in intellectual effort. This maps onto our paper's challenge of ensuring widespread uptake of cognitive enhancement -- a governance strategy requires population-level adoption, not just willing participants.

5. **The neutrality problem:** Lara's insistence on procedural neutrality (no substantive ethical doctrines) is relevant to our discussion of which values cognitive enhancement should promote. For AI governance, we may need to be more directive than SocrAI -- some capacities (e.g., risk assessment, long-term thinking) may be objectively needed for effective oversight.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- How does SocrAI handle pluralism in practice? If users arrive with deeply entrenched biases, does Socratic questioning alone suffice, or does it require some substantive moral content?
- The VR perspective-taking component could be particularly relevant for AI governance -- helping policymakers embody the perspectives of communities affected by AI deployment.
- Lara and Rodriguez-Lopez published a 2023 follow-up on "Socratic nudges" that may address some of these limitations.
- How does Lara's approach compare with Volkman and Gabriels's (2023) modular system of multiple AI interlocutors? Both emphasize dialogue but differ on neutrality vs. pluralism.

## Key Quote

> "An individual chooses their values autonomously when they are the result of a balance between capacities of both an affective and rational nature reached personally by the individual."
