## Argument

The introduction does three jobs: (1) names the problem — AI governance has a human-capacity bottleneck that current discourse underweights; (2) proposes a response — adult intervention, enhancement, and early education as complementary governance strategies; (3) states the paper's deepest claim — these approaches must be designed to build agency rather than dependency. The literature positioning paragraph establishes novelty by differentiating from six neighboring works.

## Evidence Bridge

1. Current AI governance discourse focuses on alignment, regulation, and institutional oversight. These are necessary but incomplete: they assume competent human operators without asking whether the capacities for competent operation are themselves adequate or improvable.
2. This gap is not historically unprecedented — nuclear weapons illustrate how transformative technologies outpace governance — but AI may intensify it through generality, speed, opacity, and the possibility of deep civilizational dependence.
3. Six neighboring literatures have illuminated parts of this terrain (Nyholm, Lara, Volkman & Gabriels, Laux, Gordon & Seth, Yadav), but none has proposed a governance framework organized around human capacity-building as a strategic priority, evaluated through the lens of agency-building vs dependency-building.
4. The paper's contribution is that framework, spanning three complementary paths: adult therapeutic intervention, adult enhancement, and early educational development.

## Draft (~600 words, 3 paragraphs)

Advanced AI raises not only a technical alignment problem but a problem of human adequacy. Current AI governance discourse focuses primarily on model alignment, regulation, and institutional oversight — necessary priorities, but ones that share an unexamined assumption: that the humans operating inside these systems possess the cognitive, emotional, and moral capacities needed to govern technologies of unprecedented power. This paper argues that the assumption deserves scrutiny. Humanity is building systems more powerful and more general than any it has previously governed, using institutions and cognitive habits that have repeatedly struggled to manage transformative technologies. This gap is not historically unprecedented. Transformative technologies have often outpaced the ethical and institutional capacities needed to govern them, a dynamic captured by the Collingridge dilemma [@collingridge1980]. Nuclear weapons offered the starkest modern demonstration: catastrophic stakes, narrow margins for error, and governance that lagged dangerously behind capability. What makes AI distinct is the combination of generality, speed, opacity, scale, and the possibility of deep civilizational dependence — a combination that may amplify the consequences of ordinary human cognitive weakness [@russell2019].

This paper proposes that human capacity-building is a neglected but potentially important dimension of AI governance strategy. It examines three complementary paths: adult therapeutic intervention, combining pharmacological or neurotechnological tools with psychotherapy and behavioral support; adult cognitive enhancement, aimed at expanding relevant capacities beyond restoration alone; and early educational development, cultivating metacognition, self-regulation, empathy, and civic reasoning from childhood onward. These are not proposed as alternatives to alignment research, compute governance, or regulatory oversight. They are proposed as a complementary governance layer focused on the quality of human judgment operating inside every other governance structure. The paper's contribution is not a defense of enhancement in general, but a framework for evaluating which forms of human capacity-building support self-governance in the age of advanced AI. If the bottleneck is partly cognitive, then cognitive capacity is a governance variable.

Existing scholarship has illuminated important parts of this terrain, but not the argument advanced here. Work on AI and human enhancement has framed AI as both a potential cognitive enhancer and a source of cognitive atrophy or "quasi-enhancement" [@nyholm2024]. Work on AI moral enhancement has emphasized the importance of autonomy and the risk of moral deskilling, proposing that AI should strengthen human deliberation rather than replace it [@lara2021; @volkman2023]. Work on AI oversight has highlighted the systematic fallibility of human overseers, arguing that institutional design should anticipate human cognitive limitations rather than assume competence [@laux2023]. And work on enhancement neurotechnology and cognitive sustainability has raised urgent questions about selfhood, privacy, cognitive diversity, and the erosion of human expertise in AI-dependent systems [@gordon2024; @yadav2025]. What remains underdeveloped is a governance framework that treats human cognitive and moral capacity as a strategic bottleneck — one that identifies capacity-building as a governance priority and asks not merely whether interventions improve performance, but whether they build agency or deepen dependency. This paper contributes such a framework. Its central distinction — between interventions that expand human autonomy and those that narrow it while appearing to do the opposite — structures the analysis that follows. The sections that follow define the human-capacity gap, develop the agency-building versus dependency-building distinction as the paper's core evaluative contribution, examine three intervention pathways, situate them within the broader governance portfolio, and conclude with a research and policy agenda.
