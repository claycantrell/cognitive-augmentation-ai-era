## Argument

This section explains why human capacity-building has not already become central to AI governance discourse, despite the case made in the preceding sections. It identifies both evidentiary obstacles (regulatory lag, stigma, safety uncertainty) and structural obstacles (market incentives favoring pseudo-enhancement, political fragmentation, coordination failure). The deepest obstacle is circular: better judgment is needed to develop better tools for judgment.

6. Political fragmentation creates paralysis across ideological lines.

## Draft (~600 words, 3 paragraphs)

A serious case for human capacity-building must also explain why this agenda has not already become central to AI governance discourse. Several obstacles stand out. Regulatory frameworks for enhancement and neurotechnology remain incomplete and slow-moving; approval pipelines designed for conventional therapeutics are poorly suited to combined interventions, emerging neuromodulatory tools, or governance-relevant aims that do not map neatly onto standard clinical endpoints. Stigma poses a different kind of barrier: cognitive enhancement is frequently associated with transhumanist excess, competitive cheating, or reckless self-experimentation, making serious academic and policy discussion harder even when the underlying questions are legitimate. The FDA's August 2024 rejection of MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD illustrates how regulatory caution and reputational sensitivity can slow even promising combined-intervention models. And the evidence base itself remains early, mixed, and unevenly distributed across the intervention categories this paper has reviewed: strong in some domains (social-emotional learning, rTMS for depression), promising but unsettled in others (psychedelic-assisted therapy, executive function training), and largely speculative in still others (cognitive augmentation via BCI, AI-accelerated enhancement tools). Honest uncertainty about what works, for whom, and at what cost is a legitimate reason for caution — though not, this paper argues, a reason for inattention.

Structural obstacles may be more formidable than evidentiary ones. Market incentives in pharmacology and neurotechnology tend to favor interventions that maximize pleasure, productivity, compliance, and engagement — precisely the pseudo-enhancement dynamics this paper identified in Section V. A drug that makes workers more focused and less anxious may be more commercially viable than one that makes citizens more reflective and harder to manipulate, even if the latter matters more for governance. Political fragmentation compounds the problem: different ideological constituencies resist enhancement for different reasons — often centered on inequality and corporate capture on one side, and technocratic overreach or human dignity on the other — creating paralysis even where selective, cautious progress might be justified. And the deepest structural obstacle may be the coordination failure that runs through the entire paper: the capacities needed to design and implement a wise capacity-building agenda are themselves the capacities currently most at risk. Better judgment is needed to develop better tools for judgment. If the agenda has remained marginal, it is not because the underlying questions are trivial. It is because the institutions, incentives, and capacities required to pursue them wisely are themselves fragile. These obstacles are reasons for caution, sequencing, and institutional humility — not reasons to ignore a potentially important dimension of AI governance.
