# I. Introduction

Advanced AI raises not only a technical alignment problem but a problem of human adequacy. Current AI governance discourse focuses on model alignment, regulation, and institutional oversight, necessary priorities that share an unexamined assumption: that the humans inside these systems possess the capacities needed to govern technologies of unprecedented power. This paper argues that the assumption deserves scrutiny. Humanity is building systems more powerful and more general than any it has previously governed, using institutions and cognitive habits that have repeatedly struggled to manage transformative technologies. This gap is not historically unprecedented; the Collingridge dilemma describes how transformative technologies have repeatedly outpaced the capacities needed to govern them [@collingridge1980]. Nuclear weapons offered the starkest modern demonstration: catastrophic stakes, narrow margins for error, and governance that lagged dangerously behind capability. What makes AI distinct is its combination of generality, speed, opacity, and the possibility of deep civilizational dependence [@russell2019].

This paper proposes that human capacity-building is a neglected dimension of AI governance strategy. It examines three complementary paths (assisted adult therapeutic intervention, adult cognitive enhancement, and early educational development) focused on the minds inside the system, not only the system itself. The paper's contribution is not a defense of enhancement in general, but a framework for evaluating which forms of capacity-building support self-governance in the age of advanced AI. If the bottleneck is partly cognitive, then cognitive capacity is a governance variable.

Existing scholarship has illuminated important parts of this terrain, but not the argument advanced here. Work on AI and human enhancement has framed AI as both a potential cognitive enhancer and a source of "human downgrading" through deskilling and reduced opportunities to develop intellectual potential [@nyholm2024]. Work on AI moral enhancement has emphasized the importance of autonomy and the risk of moral deskilling, proposing that AI should strengthen human deliberation rather than replace it [@lara2021; @volkman2023]. Work on AI oversight has highlighted the systematic fallibility of human overseers, arguing that institutional design should anticipate human cognitive limitations rather than assume competence [@laux2023]. Work on enhancement neurotechnology has raised urgent questions about selfhood, privacy, and cognitive diversity [@gordon2024], while work on cognitive sustainability has documented the erosion of human expertise in AI-dependent systems [@yadav2025]. What remains underdeveloped is a governance framework that treats human capacity as a strategic bottleneck and asks not merely whether interventions improve performance, but whether they build agency or deepen dependency. This paper contributes such a framework. Its central distinction (between agency-building and dependency-building interventions) structures the analysis that follows across three intervention pathways, situates them within the broader governance portfolio, and proposes a research and policy agenda.

# II. The Governance Gap: Human Limits and Technological Power

Human beings are not ideal stewards of high-stakes technologies. Several cognitive limitations are especially relevant to governance: short-term bias, attentional fragility under overload, emotionally reactive judgment under threat, weak metacognitive awareness, difficulty reasoning about complex systems, and poor coordination under competitive pressure [@tversky1974]. These limitations are manageable in many contexts. They become dangerous when paired with technologies that are fast-moving, scalable, opaque, and capable of catastrophic misuse — precisely the conditions under which careful, reflective, long-horizon judgment matters most and is hardest to sustain. The coordination failures matter as much as the individual ones: governing AI well requires sustained international cooperation, and cooperation depends on the capacity to resist short-term advantage, maintain trust under uncertainty, and reason about shared long-term consequences.

The historical pattern is familiar. Transformative technologies have repeatedly outpaced the ethical and institutional capacities needed to govern them. Nuclear weapons remain the clearest parallel: a technology of civilizational consequence, developed under competitive pressure, with governance that lagged years behind capability. The record includes repeated near-failures: Stanislav Petrov's 1983 decision not to report a false alarm that could have triggered nuclear war, the Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962 in which miscalculation nearly produced catastrophe [@lewis2014]. In each case, the margin between survival and disaster depended not only on institutional safeguards but on the judgment of individual human beings under extreme pressure: misperception, groupthink, emotional escalation, and inadequate awareness of how badly the situation was being read. The analogy to AI is structural: catastrophic stakes, governance lag, and extreme dependence on fallible human judgment under pressure. Laux has documented this pattern in AI oversight specifically, arguing that human overseers are systematically unreliable and that institutional design should anticipate this fallibility rather than assume competence [@laux2023]. The implication is uncomfortable: governance frameworks that depend on the quality of human judgment are only as strong as that judgment actually is.

AI may intensify this pattern in ways that go beyond historical precedent. Unlike nuclear weapons, which concentrated catastrophic risk in a narrow set of actors and decisions, AI distributes risk across every domain it enters: labor markets, information ecosystems, financial systems, military strategy, scientific research, interpersonal relationships. Its generality means that human cognitive weakness is no longer relevant only at moments of peak crisis; it matters continuously, across every system where AI-mediated decisions shape outcomes. Its opacity means that the humans nominally overseeing these systems may not understand what the systems are doing or why. Its speed means that the window for reflective judgment shrinks. And its capacity to optimize for engagement and convenience may actively reinforce the very cognitive weaknesses most relevant to governance: short-termism, attentional capture, preference for comfort over effort.

The bottleneck, in short, is not only technical. It is cognitive. And it is not merely one governance variable among many; it is a background condition for all the others. Every major governance mechanism (alignment, regulation, coordination) assumes a quality of human judgment that is neither guaranteed nor, under current conditions, being systematically cultivated. This is the gap the present paper addresses.

# III. The Timeline Problem: Why Capacity Matters Now

If the problem were only that human judgment is fallible, institutional design might be enough. The strategic difficulty is that AI may compress the time available to compensate for that fallibility. Expert surveys suggest that compressed timelines are at least plausible. Grace et al. found that AI researchers assigned a 50% probability to high-level machine intelligence being achieved within 45 years, though estimates vary widely across surveys, methods, and respondent pools [@grace2018]. Even if such predictions carry wide uncertainty, they matter for strategy: under plausible compressed-timeline scenarios, governance institutions that typically take decades to mature (regulatory agencies, international agreements, educational systems, professional norms) may face demands for which they are structurally unprepared. The mismatch is not merely between what AI can do and what law permits. It is between the speed at which AI reshapes cognitive environments and the speed at which human institutions, habits, and capacities can adapt.

This mismatch has a direct implication for the strategy this paper proposes. If the governance gap is partly a capacity gap, and if the timeline for AI-driven disruption is measured in years rather than generations, then interventions that operate on generational timescales (childhood education, cultural norm change, institutional reform) are necessary but may not be sufficient on their own. Adult-focused interventions deserve serious attention precisely because they may operate on shorter timescales: months to years rather than decades. This is not an argument against educational and developmental approaches, which may ultimately be more durable, more equitable, and less vulnerable to coercive misuse. It is an argument for a layered strategy in which near-term adult interventions and long-term developmental reform proceed in parallel. Race dynamics among firms and states (in which competitive pressure to deploy AI capabilities may outstrip incentives for caution) make the case for such a layered strategy more urgent, not less.

# IV. AI Dependence, Cognitive Offloading, and Civilizational Atrophy

One risk from advanced AI is not catastrophe but comfortable dependency. This concern is not purely speculative. Earlier digital technologies have already shown that systems optimized for convenience and engagement can, under some conditions, degrade the capacities they appear to support. The cognitive offloading literature suggests a pattern: when external tools reliably handle cognitive tasks (navigation, memory, arithmetic, information retrieval), the internal capacities for those tasks may weaken rather than develop. The question is not whether offloading occurs (it clearly does, and it can be efficient) but whether it produces net gains in autonomous functioning or net losses. Smartphones and social media offered a first large-scale demonstration: platforms engineered to capture and hold attention have been associated with declining attentional control, reduced tolerance for boredom and difficulty, and weakened self-regulation, particularly among populations whose capacities are still developing [see @haidt2024 for a recent review of the evidence and debate]. The debate about effect sizes continues, but there is now substantial reason to take seriously the possibility that systems designed to reduce cognitive effort can, under some conditions, weaken the capacities they replace.

Advanced AI may deepen these dynamics in ways that go well beyond what earlier technologies produced. If smartphones created frictionless access to information, advanced AI creates frictionless access to judgment. The shift matters because the capacities most relevant to governance (sustained reasoning, metacognitive monitoring, emotional self-regulation, tolerance for ambiguity, motivation to engage with difficult problems) are precisely the capacities that frictionless outsourcing is most likely to erode. Yadav (2025, preprint) has described this as "competency erosion," arguing that AI systems increasingly substitute for core cognitive processes rather than merely extending them, producing skill decay at both individual and organizational levels [@yadav2025]. The potential dynamics are broad and increasingly plausible: reduced motivation to learn when answers are instantly available, diminished tolerance for effort and ambiguity when AI handles complexity, increasing dependence on machine judgment as human judgment is exercised less often and less confidently, and shrinking public comprehension of the systems that increasingly govern social life. The result may be what this paper calls civilizational atrophy: not annihilation but a gradual erosion of the capacities needed for meaningful self-government, in which AI makes agency less necessary and therefore less developed.

The question is therefore not whether AI assists humanity (it will, and in many domains it should) but whether the form of assistance preserves or erodes agency. An AI system that frees a person for higher-order judgment functions as supportive scaffolding; one that handles so much that higher-order judgment never develops functions as a dependency-producing substitute.

# V. Good Enhancement, Bad Enhancement, and False Expansion

The bioethics literature has long debated where therapy ends and enhancement begins, but the standard framing (restoring normal function versus exceeding it) is increasingly recognized as insufficient for the questions this paper raises. Enhancement is not a single category. As Earp et al. have argued, whether an intervention constitutes genuine improvement depends on context and consequences, not on whether it exceeds a species-typical baseline [@earp2014]. This paper proposes a different cut, one suited to the governance problem at hand. The relevant question is not whether an intervention crosses the therapy-enhancement boundary, but whether it builds durable human agency or deepens dependency. This cuts across the usual therapy-enhancement boundary: some therapeutic interventions may be strongly agency-building, while some nominal enhancements may function primarily as dependency-producing substitutes. Capacity-building enhancement, as used here, refers to interventions that strengthen judgment, self-regulation, metacognition, empathy, or resilience in ways that expand a person's capacity for autonomous action over time, whether through durable internal change, supportive scaffolding, or both. Pseudo-enhancement refers to interventions that feel empowering (improving mood, productivity, social confidence, or cognitive speed) while primarily increasing reliance on external systems, narrowing the range of autonomous functioning, or substituting transient performance for durable competence. The distinction is not between feeling better and being better in some moralistic sense; it is between interventions that leave people more capable of governing themselves and those that leave them less so. Not every externally supported intervention is pseudo-enhancement; assistive tools may expand agency when they scaffold functioning without degrading the underlying capacity for judgment, reflection, or self-direction.

This distinction identifies a concrete civilizational risk. As AI accelerates drug discovery, biomarker identification, and pharmacological personalization [@serrano2024], the market will have incentives to produce both genuine capacity-building tools and highly effective forms of what might be called chemical opium: dependency-producing substitutes optimized for engagement, compliance, pleasure, or productivity rather than for the hard-won gains in judgment, patience, and self-awareness that genuine stewardship requires. AI-mediated systems that optimize for engagement, compliance, and frictionless experience may produce populations that feel more empowered, more informed, and more connected while becoming progressively less capable of independent thought, sustained attention, or meaningful self-direction. Consider two illustrative cases. A writing assistant that generates fluent prose may feel like cognitive enhancement while gradually eroding the user's capacity for independent composition. A decision-support system that reliably produces good recommendations may feel empowering while weakening the judgment it was meant to support. Neither outcome is inevitable, but both illustrate how the framework applies: the question is whether the tool builds capacity or substitutes for it. One line of pharmacological evidence suggests a similar gap: studies of cognitive enhancers in healthy adults have found that users perceive significant cognitive benefit even when objective performance gains are minimal or absent [@ilieva2013]. Nyholm has flagged this dynamic in the context of AI and cognition, distinguishing AI that genuinely extends human minds from AI that merely produces intelligent-seeming behavior without building underlying capacity, a form of "human downgrading" rather than genuine enhancement [@nyholm2024]. The civilizational question, then, is not whether enhancement will exist, but whether societies can distinguish capacity-building from dependency-producing substitutes as both become more widely available.

This paper proposes three evaluative criteria for making that distinction, offered not as a formal decision procedure but as analytic tools with enough specificity to guide both research and policy. First, what is the net effect on autonomous functioning over time? Does the intervention expand the person's capacity for judgment and self-direction, or merely provide temporary substitution? The key question is not whether continued support is needed (many genuine interventions involve ongoing use) but whether autonomous functioning expands or contracts over the course of engagement. Second, what is the effect on metacognition and self-regulation? Does the intervention increase reflective control and understanding (the ability to monitor one's own cognition, recognize biases, manage emotional reactivity, and adjust behavior in light of reflection) or does it mainly reduce discomfort or increase compliance? An anxiolytic that suppresses distress may relieve suffering without necessarily building the higher-order capacities relevant to stewardship. Third, what is the effect on underlying competence and resilience? Does the intervention scaffold growth, or create fragility, withdrawal vulnerability, or erosion of baseline capacity? The distinction is not between dependent and independent functioning but between scaffolding that expands capacity and substitution that erodes it. These criteria are not bright lines. Many interventions will fall in ambiguous territory, and reasonable people will disagree about specific cases. But they give the distinction enough analytic teeth to move beyond vague appeals to "responsible enhancement" toward evaluable claims about what specific interventions actually do to human agency.

This distinction (between interventions that expand human autonomy and those that narrow it while appearing to do the opposite) is the central evaluative question for any serious proposal to strengthen human capacity in the age of advanced AI. Not all enhancement is stewardship enhancement, and the intervention pathways in Sections VIII through X are evaluated through this lens.

# VI. Defining Human Capacity-Building

This paper uses "human capacity-building" as an umbrella term for interventions aimed at improving capacities relevant to judgment, self-regulation, cooperation, understanding, or resilience. The interventions most relevant to this argument fall into five overlapping categories. *Therapeutic and behavioral* interventions include psychotherapy, cognitive-behavioral approaches, trauma-focused treatment, coaching, and structured integration protocols: the scaffolding that often determines whether other tools produce durable change. *Pharmacological* interventions include substances that alter mood, cognition, or motivation in ways that may affect governance-relevant capacities, including stimulants, nootropics, psychedelics, and targeted neurochemical treatments. *Neurotechnological* interventions include devices that modulate neural activity directly rather than through chemistry, such as neurofeedback, noninvasive brain stimulation, transcranial focused ultrasound, and brain-computer interfaces. *Contemplative and reflective* interventions include meditation, attentional training, compassion practice, and metacognitive exercises, practices that cultivate awareness and self-regulation through sustained discipline. *Developmental and educational* interventions include childhood mindfulness programs, executive-function training, social and emotional learning, reflective pedagogy, and civic reasoning education, approaches that shape capacities during developmental periods when these capacities are especially plastic. These categories are not mutually exclusive, and many of the most promising approaches involve combinations across them.

Because these categories overlap, several distinctions become important for the analysis that follows. The *therapy/enhancement* distinction matters conceptually, even if it is often blurred in practice; an antidepressant that restores executive function may also improve it beyond baseline. The *adult/developmental* distinction matters strategically: adult tools can respond to near-term governance pressure, while developmental approaches are slower but potentially more durable and more equitable. The *standalone/combined* distinction matters empirically: as Section VIII will argue, pharmacological and neurotechnological tools often produce their most durable effects when embedded in therapeutic, behavioral, or contemplative scaffolding rather than administered in isolation. And the *self-directed/imposed* distinction matters ethically: an individual choosing a meditation practice or seeking therapy occupies a fundamentally different position from one required to undergo cognitive modification by an employer, a military, or a state. These distinctions become especially important in Section XIV's analysis of coercion and misuse.

# VII. Capacities for AI Stewardship

The capacities most relevant to AI stewardship are not reducible to raw intelligence. Processing speed, memory, and analytical power matter, but the governance challenges posed by advanced AI demand a broader cluster of cognitive, affective, and social capacities, what this paper calls *stewardship capacities*: the cluster of abilities most relevant to governing powerful systems under uncertainty. The list is not intended as exhaustive; it identifies the capacities most directly implicated by the governance failures, timeline pressures, and dependency risks outlined in the preceding sections. Taken together, these capacities describe the kind of mind advanced AI governance requires, even if no individual or institution will embody them perfectly. Several features of this list deserve emphasis. First, many of these capacities are relational and affective rather than narrowly cognitive. Empathy, emotional regulation, and civic reasoning are not usually classified as "intelligence," yet they may matter as much for AI governance as any form of analytical skill. Second, the capacities overlap and reinforce one another: metacognition supports resistance to manipulation, emotional regulation supports long-term thinking, and epistemic humility supports cooperation. Third, the list is oriented toward *judgment* rather than *performance*. The goal is not to produce faster or more productive minds, but wiser, more reflective, and more cooperative ones. A governance system populated by highly intelligent actors who lack empathy, epistemic humility, or resistance to manipulation would not be safer; it might be more dangerous. The normative core of this paper is that stewardship requires not maximal cognitive power but a particular *quality* of cognition, and that this quality of cognition can, at least in principle, be cultivated.

# VIII. Intervention Pathways I: Assisted Therapeutic Intervention in Adults

A major near-term path is *assisted adult intervention*: combined approaches in which a pharmacological or neurotechnological tool acts as a catalyst within a therapeutic framework. In several relevant therapeutic domains, standalone interventions produce weaker or less durable effects than combined ones [@cuijpers2020]. The principle (that the tool matters less than the scaffolding around it) structures the three cases that follow.

Psychedelic-assisted therapy illustrates the model with unusual clarity. The intervention is not the drug but the process: altered state, guided support, and meaning-making combined. Mitchell et al. reported that MDMA-assisted therapy produced significant reductions in PTSD severity, with 67% of participants no longer meeting diagnostic criteria at follow-up [@mitchell2021], though the FDA's 2024 rejection on methodological grounds underscores that the path from promising results to approved treatment is neither fast nor guaranteed. Noninvasive brain stimulation offers a parallel case: rTMS has received FDA clearance for major depressive disorder [@somani2019] and additional indications, and a meta-analysis of brain stimulation paired with psychotherapy found enhanced outcomes across over 600 participants [@he2022], reinforcing the pattern that neuromodulatory tools may be most valuable when used to support therapeutic learning rather than replace it.

Restorative psychopharmacology represents a related dimension. Severe depression, chronic dysregulation, and pathological anger directly erode governance-relevant capacities. Ketamine and esketamine represent the most advanced case: rapid-acting antidepressants that produce meaningful symptom relief within hours rather than weeks [@krystal2023]. Depression is the strongest case for pharmacological optimism; anger and aggression present a more mixed picture, with psychological treatments often matching or outperforming medication [@liu2025].

The near-term value of assisted adult intervention lies less in transformation than in resilience: even modest, widely distributed improvements in self-regulation and emotional stability may matter under compressed timelines and extraordinary decision-making pressure.

# IX. Intervention Pathways II: Adult Enhancement Beyond Therapy

Some interventions relevant to AI governance aim not merely to restore impaired function but to expand specific dimensions of healthy cognition. These include tools aimed at enhancing sustained attention, modulating emotional regulation, and, more speculatively, supporting metacognitive monitoring. Further on the horizon are attempts to augment cognition through direct brain-computer interfaces. This is the more speculative tier of the paper's argument. The current evidence base for genuine cognitive augmentation in healthy adults is thinner and more contested than the therapeutic evidence reviewed above. Most pharmacological cognitive enhancers show modest effects that vary significantly across individuals and tasks; most neurotechnological approaches remain early-stage. Standalone cognitive enhancement does not yet offer reliable tools for strengthening stewardship capacities at scale. What it offers is a set of trajectories worth monitoring — and, crucially, worth shaping.

Here the central paradox of the paper becomes sharpest: AI may simultaneously undermine human capacity through dependency and accelerate the tools that might strengthen it. AI is being applied to drug discovery in ways that may compress development timelines [@serrano2024], and similar applications in neuroscience may eventually support more precise interventions [@onciul2025], though translation to clinical practice remains uncertain. The strategic question is not only what works today, but what trajectories are opening and whether governance attention is needed now to shape their direction: governance frameworks designed only around today's tools risk being overtaken by tomorrow's.

Brain-computer interfaces illustrate the speed and stakes of this dynamic. The most clinically mature BCI applications remain therapeutic, restoring lost function in motor control and communication [@chen2025], though early-stage augmentative work in attention enhancement and collaborative decision-making has shown proof-of-concept results [@cinel2019]. The boundary between therapeutic restoration and cognitive augmentation may narrow over time, and the governance question is already live: will BCIs be developed toward agency and autonomous self-regulation, or toward entertainment, productivity optimization, and behavioral control? The values embedded in early design choices and regulatory frameworks will shape whether these tools become instruments of capacity-building or dependency.

# X. Intervention Pathways III: Early Educational and Developmental Intervention

A serious response to the human-capacity bottleneck cannot begin only in adulthood. Many of the capacities most relevant to AI stewardship (including metacognition, emotional regulation, empathy, and civic reasoning) are developmental achievements shaped substantially by early experience, schooling, and culture. They are not fixed at birth, but neither are they equally plastic across the lifespan; childhood and adolescence represent periods of particular opportunity for cultivation. This makes educational and developmental intervention potentially among the least coercive, most scalable, and institutionally legitimate forms of capacity-building available. Unlike pharmacological or neurotechnological approaches, which raise immediate questions about safety, access, and consent, educational programs operate within institutions that already exist, enjoy broad public support, and can in principle reach entire populations. The question is whether these programs can be designed to cultivate the specific capacities that matter for governance (not merely academic performance) and whether the evidence shows that they can.

On that question, the evidence is encouraging if uneven. A meta-analysis of social and emotional learning programs encompassing 424 studies across 53 countries and more than 575,000 students found that well-implemented SEL programs are associated with gains in social-emotional competence, prosocial behavior, and academic outcomes [@cipriano2023]. Early interventions targeting executive function have shown promising effects, especially for children in high-stress environments where these capacities are most vulnerable [@diamond2016]. Mindfulness-based programs for children have shown effects on depression and anxiety, though effects on executive functioning and attention did not survive active-control comparison, suggesting that the most robust benefits are affective rather than purely cognitive [@dunning2019]. What connects these approaches is not any single program's effect size but the underlying principle: that capacities often treated as fixed personality traits are in fact trainable, and trainable early. Work from Immordino-Yang's group offers a particularly direct bridge: the capacity to connect immediate experience to broader ethical and civic meaning is itself a developmental achievement, linked to coordinated brain network development and predictive of psychosocial outcomes into young adulthood [@immordinoyang2024]. Educational systems that cultivate this capacity are doing governance work, whether or not they use that language.

The strategic logic of educational intervention differs from adult approaches in ways that matter. Education is slower: a generation-scale investment will not produce results in time to address near-term AI governance pressures. But what education offers in return is durability, equity, and political legitimacy that adult intervention struggles to match. Educational programs do not raise the same consent and bodily-intrusion issues as pharmacological or neurotechnological modification, and they can be embedded in public institutions rather than distributed through markets that favor the privileged. A serious capacity-building strategy therefore needs both layers: near-term adult intervention for the populations already making governance decisions, and longer-term developmental reform for those who will inherit the consequences. The adult tier addresses urgency; the educational tier addresses depth. Together they constitute something closer to a complete response, provided both are evaluated by the same standard: whether they build agency, not merely performance.

# XI. Contemplative Practice as an Existence Proof

Contemplative practice serves this paper's argument not as a proposed solution but as an existence proof. It provides some of the clearest evidence that capacities often treated as fixed (attentional stability, emotional regulation, metacognitive awareness) can be systematically cultivated. A meta-analysis of morphometric neuroimaging found consistent structural alterations in eight brain regions among long-term meditators [@fox2014]. Research on transcranial focused ultrasound has demonstrated that neuromodulation can alter mood and connectivity in self-referential processing networks [@sanguinetti2020], including modulating default mode network activity in a single-blind, sham-controlled design [@lord2024]. But traditional contemplative cultivation typically requires years of disciplined practice within frameworks that are neither scalable nor widely accessible. The question is whether neurotechnological scaffolding can broaden access to what contemplatives have shown is achievable — and whether such interventions can produce durable trait-level changes, rather than only transient state effects.

# XII. Capacity-Building Within the Broader AI Governance Portfolio

Human capacity-building is not a substitute for technical alignment, compute governance, regulation, or international coordination [see @maas2023]. A misaligned system remains dangerous regardless of the wisdom of its overseers, and poorly designed institutions can fail regardless of the individuals within them. This paper argues that capacity-building addresses a different problem: a neglected prerequisite that the existing governance portfolio assumes rather than examines.

That prerequisite is the quality of human judgment inside governance systems. Each major governance approach depends on it. Alignment research assumes that humans can specify goals well enough to align AI systems toward them and can detect misalignment when it occurs, both tasks that require sustained attention, metacognitive monitoring, and resistance to the very cognitive shortcuts AI may encourage. Regulation assumes that lawmakers and agency officials can understand the systems they govern, a challenge that grows harder as AI systems become more opaque, more general, and more deeply embedded in social and economic infrastructure. International coordination assumes that negotiators can manage uncertainty, resist short-term competitive pressure, and sustain cooperation under conditions where defection is tempting and verification is difficult. In each case, the governance mechanism is only as strong as the judgment capacities brought to bear. Capacity-building is not one governance strategy among many; it is a background condition for the effectiveness of the others.

This framing helps explain why the dimension has been overlooked. Background conditions are, by definition, easy to take for granted. Physical infrastructure rarely enters policy debate until it fails; the cognitive infrastructure of governance has not yet failed visibly enough to attract strategic attention. Capacity-building also falls between disciplinary boundaries: it belongs fully to neither health policy, nor education policy, nor technology policy, nor national security, and problems that lack a clear institutional home tend to remain invisible even when they are important. The result is that the governance portfolio as currently constructed optimizes for what to do about AI while largely ignoring whether the humans doing it are adequate to the task.

# XIII. Obstacles to Pursuing a Capacity-Building Agenda

A serious case for human capacity-building must also explain why this agenda has remained marginal despite the governance gap documented above. The question is not merely what obstacles stand in the way, but why a potentially important dimension of AI governance has remained institutionally invisible. Several factors converge. Regulatory frameworks for enhancement and neurotechnology remain incomplete and slow-moving; approval pipelines designed for conventional therapeutics are poorly suited to combined interventions, emerging neuromodulatory tools, or governance-relevant aims that do not map neatly onto standard clinical endpoints. Stigma poses a different kind of barrier: public attitudes toward pharmacological cognitive enhancement tend toward caution, with safety, coercion, and fairness as dominant concerns [@schelle2014], and the association with transhumanist excess or competitive cheating makes serious academic and policy discussion harder even when the underlying questions are legitimate. The lag is not new: Greely et al. called for accelerated research, professional guidelines, and legislative reform on cognitive enhancement in 2008; nearly two decades later, little of that agenda has materialized [@greely2008]. The 2024 FDA rejection of MDMA-assisted therapy, noted in Section VIII, illustrates how these barriers compound. And the evidence base itself remains early, mixed, and unevenly distributed across the intervention categories this paper has reviewed: strong in some domains (social-emotional learning, rTMS for depression), promising but unsettled in others (psychedelic-assisted therapy, executive function training), and largely speculative in still others (cognitive augmentation via BCI, AI-accelerated enhancement tools). Honest uncertainty about what works, for whom, and at what cost is a legitimate reason for caution — though not, this paper argues, a reason for inattention.

Structural obstacles may be more formidable than evidentiary ones. Market incentives in pharmacology and neurotechnology tend to favor interventions that maximize pleasure, productivity, compliance, and engagement, precisely the pseudo-enhancement dynamics this paper identified in Section V. A drug that makes workers more focused and less anxious may be more commercially viable than one that makes citizens more reflective and harder to manipulate, even if the latter matters more for governance. Political fragmentation compounds the problem: different ideological constituencies resist enhancement for different reasons, creating paralysis even where selective, cautious progress might be justified. And the deepest structural obstacle may be the coordination failure that runs through the entire paper: the capacities needed to design and implement a wise capacity-building agenda are themselves the capacities currently most at risk. Better judgment is needed to develop better tools for judgment. If the agenda has remained marginal, it is not because the underlying questions are trivial. It is because the institutions, incentives, and capacities required to pursue them wisely are themselves fragile. These obstacles are reasons for caution, sequencing, and institutional humility, not reasons to ignore a potentially important dimension of AI governance.

# XIV. Risks, Misuse, and Failure Modes

Any serious argument for human capacity-building must confront the possibility that the tools themselves become instruments of domination. The same pharmacological, neurotechnological, and therapeutic interventions this paper has reviewed as potential supports for human agency could, under different institutional conditions, become mechanisms of control. The risk begins with mental privacy. Neural data is uniquely intimate: it may reveal patterns of attention, emotion, cognition, and vulnerability that no other dataset captures as directly. The transition from behavioral surveillance (tracking what people do) to cognitive surveillance (inferring aspects of what people think, feel, attend to, or are vulnerable to) would mark a qualitative moral threshold, not merely a quantitative extension of existing monitoring. Some jurisdictions have begun to recognize this: Chile has amended its constitution to protect neurorights, and scholarly work mapping the ethical and legal foundations of cognitive liberty has accelerated [see @ligthart2023]. But recognition is not protection. The infrastructure for cognitive monitoring may develop faster than the legal frameworks needed to constrain it. And the same tools that modulate mood, attention, or cognition therapeutically can in principle be used to override rather than support individual judgment, turning instruments of self-regulation into instruments of external control.

The more likely danger, however, is not overt override but soft coercion. Enhancement may become practically mandatory without formal mandates if refusal carries significant social or economic costs. An employer who offers cognitive enhancement tools as a workplace benefit need not require their use; the competitive disadvantage of declining may be sufficient [@bostrom2009]. Similar dynamics apply in military and educational contexts. Unequal access introduces a further risk: governance institutions may become stratified between those able to afford cognitive support and those expected to compete without it. Veit has argued that the inequality objection does not justify opposing enhancement (and that enhancement may in principle reduce inequality) but acknowledges that the distributional design challenge is real and requires active policy attention [@veit2018]. In each case, the coercion is structural rather than explicit, and therefore harder to identify, regulate, or resist.

Market incentives compound this risk. As Section V argued, commercial pressures tend to favor interventions that maximize productivity, compliance, engagement, and measurable performance rather than the harder-to-quantify capacities this paper cares about: wisdom, reflective judgment, moral restraint, cooperative disposition. The result could be a form of optimization that produces smarter but less trustworthy actors: the intelligence-without-wisdom problem, one of the most important failure modes for this agenda.

Two additional risks deserve attention. The first is cognitive monoculture. Governance under uncertainty benefits from heterogeneity in perception, reasoning style, and social cognition; as Volkman and Gabriels have argued, diverse perspectives serve an epistemic function analogous to scientific pluralism, and abstracting that diversity away risks weakening collective judgment [@volkman2023]. If capacity-building programs converge on a standardized profile of ideal cognition (optimized for a particular set of performance metrics, cultural values, or institutional needs), the result could be a reduction in cognitive diversity that weakens collective resilience and adaptive capacity. The second risk is an enhancement arms race. If nations, firms, or individuals treat cognitive enhancement as a competitive domain, pressure to escalate may outstrip the caution that safety and ethics demand, reproducing at the level of human cognition the very race dynamics this paper has criticized in AI development itself.

These dangers do not refute the case for capacity-building, but they do define the conditions under which it can be pursued responsibly. Combined interventions (the very model this paper has endorsed as most promising) may become powerful enough to blur the distinction between helping people govern themselves and training them into compliance, especially if capacity-building is justified in the language of public safety or AI-governance necessity. The evaluative criteria proposed in Section V (net effect on autonomous functioning, effect on metacognition and self-regulation, effect on underlying competence and resilience) apply here with particular force. Any intervention that fails these tests is not capacity-building in the sense this paper intends, regardless of what it is called. These risks are real, and any paper that proposes this agenda bears the burden of naming them clearly.

# XV. Research and Policy Agenda

If human capacity-building matters for AI governance, then a responsible path forward requires not enthusiasm but a structured research and policy agenda. The research priorities follow directly from the analysis in this paper: which capacities matter most for AI stewardship, how those capacities should be measured, whether combined interventions outperform standalone ones, and how durable any gains prove to be over time. The table in Section VII offers a starting framework, but rigorous empirical work is needed to identify which capacities most strongly predict governance-relevant outcomes: sound judgment under uncertainty, resistance to manipulation, sustained cooperative behavior, as opposed to narrow performance metrics. The combined-intervention pattern identified in Section VIII needs systematic testing across other intervention categories. Durability testing (with follow-up periods measured in years, not weeks) is essential for any intervention proposed as governance-relevant. Research should also prioritize measures that distinguish genuine capacity-building from pseudo-enhancement: instruments that assess metacognitive monitoring, resistance to manipulation, cooperative disposition, and reflective judgment, not merely cognitive speed or symptom reduction. A realistic agenda also requires sequencing: lower-risk, scalable, and noncoercive interventions should generally be studied and deployed before more invasive or easily weaponized ones.

Educational priorities are equally important but operate on different timescales. Research on childhood mindfulness, executive function development, social and emotional learning, and civic reasoning education should focus not only on academic outcomes but on the specific capacities this paper has identified as governance-relevant. The key methodological challenge is distinguishing programs that genuinely strengthen metacognition, emotional regulation, and reflective reasoning from those that merely teach compliance or produce short-term behavioral effects that fade. The ideological sensitivity of educational intervention also demands care: programs aimed at cultivating civic reasoning or critical thinking must be clearly distinguishable from ideological programming, and research designs should address that concern directly.

A responsible policy framework must address safeguards and justice at the same time. Cognitive liberty (the right to mental self-determination) should be recognized as a foundational principle, alongside robust protections for informed consent, neural and psychological privacy, and freedom from coercive or manipulative applications. Independent oversight of high-risk interventions, particularly those involving neurotechnology or pharmacological modification, is essential. And the justice dimension cannot be an afterthought: if capacity-building tools prove effective, access must not be limited to elites, corporations, or militaries. The most promising interventions from a governance perspective may be precisely those that are scalable, low-cost, and noncoercive (educational programs, therapeutic frameworks, community-based approaches) rather than expensive technological augmentations available only to the privileged. Treating capacity-building as a public-interest issue rather than a luxury market is not merely an ethical preference; it is a strategic necessity. A governance system in which only some actors have access to enhanced judgment is not more stable; it is more fragile.

# XVI. Discussion

Much of the evidence relevant to this paper remains preliminary, uneven, or indirect. Some interventions reviewed here may fail or introduce tradeoffs that complicate their use, and the chicken-and-egg problem identified in Section XIII is real. But the case for human capacity-building does not depend on optimism about any particular tool. It depends on the narrower claim that the human-capacity bottleneck is real and currently underexamined. The paper's contribution is best understood not as a policy blueprint but as a framework for research, governance, and institutional design.

What this paper does claim is that three distinctions should structure future work. The first is conceptual: *agency-building* versus *dependency-building* — whether an intervention expands autonomous functioning or erodes it. The second is strategic: *near-term adult intervention* versus *longer-term developmental reform*, both needed, neither sufficient alone. The third is ethical: *support for human flourishing* versus *systems of optimization aimed at control*, a boundary this paper bears the burden of insisting on. These distinctions frame a research agenda worth pursuing even if many specific interventions prove ineffective. The question is not whether we know enough to act with certainty. It is whether we know enough to stop treating human capacity as a background assumption in AI governance, and to recognize continued inattention as a risk in its own right.

# XVII. Conclusion

Advanced AI creates not only a problem of machine capability but a problem of human adequacy. The governance challenges posed by advanced AI depend for their resolution on human capacities that are not guaranteed and may be actively eroding. This paper has argued that human capacity-building deserves serious attention as a dimension of AI governance: that governance quality depends partly on the minds producing it. Three pathways merit sustained investigation: assisted adult therapeutic intervention, which may strengthen judgment and self-regulation in populations already making governance decisions; adult enhancement, whose AI-accelerated trajectories require governance attention before mature evidence arrives; and early educational and developmental intervention, which offers the most durable, equitable, and institutionally legitimate route to cultivating stewardship capacities at scale.

The prudent response is neither techno-utopianism nor blanket refusal, but serious interdisciplinary work on which forms of capacity-building genuinely support agency and which deepen the dependency they claim to cure. At minimum, human capacity should no longer be treated as a background assumption in AI governance: the bottleneck is real, the relevant capacities are at least partly trainable, and the consequences of neglecting them are too serious to ignore.
