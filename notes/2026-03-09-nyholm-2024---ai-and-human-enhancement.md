## Summary

Sven Nyholm examines two opposing views about the relationship between AI and human intelligence. The first is a worry about "human downgrading" -- that heavy reliance on AI technologies may reduce opportunities to exercise and develop our own intelligence, effectively deskilling us. The second is a hope that AI might serve as genuine cognitive enhancement. Nyholm explores the enhancement possibility through two distinct mechanisms: (1) AI as extending human minds in the Clark-and-Chalmers extended mind sense, thereby constituting internal enhancement, and (2) AI as enabling people to *behave* in artificially intelligent ways without actually improving their natural cognitive capacities -- a form of "artificial human intelligence." He considers both individual and group-level enhancement, and concludes that only AI technologies functioning as genuine mind extenders (requiring extensive human engagement) constitute true cognitive enhancement; those that operate too autonomously merely produce "artificial cognitive enhancements" that do not genuinely improve the user.

## Key Findings

- **Four possible ways AI could enhance humans:**
  1. AI extends individual minds (internal enhancement via extended mind thesis)
  2. AI enables individuals to behave as if cognitively enhanced ("artificial human intelligence")
  3. AI extends corporate/group minds (enhancing group-level cognition)
  4. AI enables groups to behave as if they have enhanced collective cognitive abilities

- **The human downgrading concern:** Draws on Tristan Harris and Aza Raskin's "human downgrading" concept and Shannon Vallor's work on "moral deskilling." If we hand over intelligence-requiring tasks to AI, we may have fewer occasions to develop our intellectual potential. Norwegian research showing declining IQ scores is cited as suggestive evidence.

- **Extended mind thesis applied to AI:** Clark and Chalmers's criteria for a "coupled cognitive system" require (1) active causal roles between components, (2) system components regulating behavior as a mind would, and (3) diminished capacity if external parts are removed. Nyholm argues -- drawing on Karina Vold and David Chalmers -- that the *more autonomously* an AI operates, the *less* it qualifies as a mind extension. ChatGPT and fully autonomous systems operate too independently to count.

- **The praiseworthiness test:** Nyholm proposes that genuine cognitive enhancement should be linked to whether the resulting behavior is praiseworthy. If AI merely performs tasks for us (like the AlphaGo stone-mover or Chinese room occupant), the outputs are not praiseworthy in the way natural intelligence is. Only mind-extending AI that supports genuinely creditable performance counts as true enhancement.

- **Group-level enhancement more plausible:** At the group level, it may be more plausible that AI constitutes genuine enhancement, because group-level agency (per Christian List and Philip Pettit) is already a form of artificial intelligence -- the group's intelligent behavior transcends any individual member's natural intelligence.

- **"Artificial cognitive enhancements" as quasi-enhancement:** Technologies that make us behave as if enhanced without extending our minds are a distinct category -- not genuine human enhancement, but not nothing either.

## Methodology

Conceptual/philosophical analysis. The paper proceeds through: (1) reviewing public debates about AI and declining intelligence, (2) analyzing definitions of human intelligence (behavioristic vs. consciousness-requiring), (3) applying the extended mind thesis to AI technologies, (4) using Searle's Chinese room argument as an analogy for AI-enabled "artificial human intelligence," and (5) evaluating enhancement claims through the lens of praiseworthiness. No empirical data collection; relies on philosophical argument and thought experiments (AlphaGo stone-mover, ChatGPT Chinese room).

## Relevance to This Work

**This is the closest neighbor to our paper and requires careful differentiation.**

1. **Key overlap:** Nyholm directly addresses whether AI can cognitively enhance humans -- our paper's central question. His framing of the human downgrading vs. enhancement tension maps onto our argument that cognitive enhancement is needed *precisely because* AI otherwise risks degrading human governance capacity.

2. **Where we diverge:** Nyholm frames AI-as-enhancement as a philosophical puzzle about the nature of mind extension and praiseworthiness. Our paper takes a *policy and governance* orientation -- we argue cognitive enhancement is needed as a deliberate strategy to maintain human oversight capacity over AI systems. We are less concerned with whether AI *itself* is the enhancer and more focused on the full portfolio of enhancement modalities (pharmacological, contemplative, neurotechnological, educational) needed to keep humans capable of governing AI.

3. **The human downgrading argument supports us:** Nyholm's careful articulation of the deskilling/downgrading risk strengthens our Section II argument that the human-AI capability gap is widening. His worry that "if we hand over too many intelligence-requiring tasks to AI technologies, we might end up with fewer opportunities to train our own intelligence" is exactly the problem we propose to solve.

4. **His praiseworthiness criterion is orthogonal to our concerns:** Nyholm concludes that AI enabling us to merely *behave* intelligently (without genuine mind extension) is not true enhancement. For governance purposes, this distinction may matter less -- what matters is whether humans can *effectively oversee* AI, regardless of whether their oversight capacity is "praiseworthy" in the philosophical sense.

5. **Group-level analysis is underdeveloped:** Nyholm gestures at group-level cognitive enhancement via AI but admits he lacks space to develop it. Our paper could fill this gap by connecting group-level cognitive enhancement to institutional design for AI governance.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- Nyholm's autonomy criterion (more autonomous AI = less mind-extending) has direct implications for our argument. If the most capable AI systems are too autonomous to count as mind extensions, does this mean the enhancement must come from *non-AI* sources? This would strengthen our multi-modal portfolio argument.
- How does Nyholm's praiseworthiness test interact with democratic accountability in AI governance? Overseers need to be genuinely competent, not just appear competent.
- The Vold distinction (support systems requiring engagement vs. autonomous systems) could map onto our modality analysis -- collaborative AI tools as one enhancement pathway alongside pharmacological and contemplative approaches.
- Follow up on Erler and Muller (cited in note 5) on "AI as IA" -- AI as intellectual augmentation.
