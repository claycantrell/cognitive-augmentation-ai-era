## Argument

This section defines "human capacity-building" and lays out the five intervention categories the paper uses. It is taxonomic, not argumentative — its job is to establish vocabulary so Sections VII–X can use it without pausing to define terms. It also maps the key distinctions (therapy/enhancement, adult/developmental, standalone/combined, self-directed/imposed) that become central in the ethical analysis of Section XIV.

## Evidence Bridge

1. The five categories are drawn from the existing enhancement, neurotechnology, and educational literatures, not invented for this paper.
2. The distinctions (therapy vs enhancement, standalone vs combined, self-directed vs imposed) are standard in bioethics but need to be named here so the paper can use them precisely later.
3. The overlap between categories is itself important: combined interventions (Section VIII's core argument) only make sense if the categories are understood as overlapping rather than competing.

## Draft (~500 words, 2 paragraphs)

This paper uses "human capacity-building" as an umbrella term for interventions aimed at improving capacities relevant to judgment, self-regulation, cooperation, understanding, or resilience. The interventions most relevant to this argument fall into five overlapping categories. *Therapeutic and behavioral* interventions include psychotherapy, cognitive-behavioral approaches, trauma-focused treatment, coaching, and structured integration protocols — the scaffolding that often determines whether other tools produce durable change. *Pharmacological* interventions include substances that alter mood, cognition, or motivation in ways that may affect governance-relevant capacities, including stimulants, nootropics, psychedelics, and targeted neurochemical treatments. *Neurotechnological* interventions include devices that modulate neural activity directly rather than through chemistry, such as neurofeedback, noninvasive brain stimulation, transcranial focused ultrasound, and brain-computer interfaces. *Contemplative and reflective* interventions include meditation, attentional training, compassion practice, and metacognitive exercises — practices that cultivate awareness and self-regulation through sustained discipline. *Developmental and educational* interventions include childhood mindfulness programs, executive-function training, social and emotional learning, reflective pedagogy, and civic reasoning education — approaches that shape capacities during developmental periods when these capacities are especially plastic. These categories are not mutually exclusive, and many of the most promising approaches involve combinations across them.

Because these categories overlap, several distinctions become important for the analysis that follows. The *therapy/enhancement* distinction matters conceptually, even if it is often blurred in practice; an antidepressant that restores executive function may also improve it beyond baseline. The *adult/developmental* distinction matters strategically: adult tools can respond to near-term governance pressure, while developmental approaches are slower but potentially more durable and more equitable. The *standalone/combined* distinction matters empirically: as Section VIII will argue, pharmacological and neurotechnological tools often produce their most durable effects when embedded in therapeutic, behavioral, or contemplative scaffolding rather than administered in isolation. And the *self-directed/imposed* distinction matters ethically: an individual choosing a meditation practice or seeking therapy occupies a fundamentally different position from one required to undergo cognitive modification by an employer, a military, or a state. These distinctions become especially important when the paper turns to coercion, misuse, and governance risk in Section XIV.
