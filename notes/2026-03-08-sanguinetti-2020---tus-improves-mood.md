## Summary

Flagship paper demonstrating that transcranial focused ultrasound (TUS) to the right inferior frontal gyrus (rIFG) improves mood and alters functional brain connectivity in healthy humans. Five experiments with 200+ participants. A brief 30-second sonication produced significant mood enhancement lasting up to 30 minutes. fMRI showed altered functional connectivity in prefrontal networks associated with emotional regulation. Double-blind, sham-controlled.

## Key Findings

- 30-second TUS to the right prefrontal cortex significantly improved mood on multiple measures (PANAS, VAS)
- Effects lasted up to 30 minutes after a single brief treatment
- fMRI showed changes in functional connectivity — the ultrasound didn't just feel different, it measurably changed how brain regions talked to each other
- Effects were specific to the targeted region — sham stimulation showed no effect
- One of the first human experiments demonstrating that focused ultrasound can enhance mood in healthy (not clinical) populations
- 179 citations — widely recognized as foundational in the field

## Methodology

- Double-blind, sham-controlled across 5 experiments
- 200+ healthy volunteers
- Low-intensity focused ultrasound targeting right inferior frontal gyrus
- 30-second sonication duration
- Mood measured via PANAS (Positive and Negative Affect Schedule) and Visual Analog Scales
- fMRI imaging in some experiments to measure connectivity changes

## Relevance to This Work

**Critical for Sections V and VI.** This is the strongest published evidence that noninvasive brain stimulation can meaningfully alter emotional states in healthy people with a brief, safe intervention. Directly supports the argument that enhancement can provide the emotional regulation humans need to manage AI wisely. If a 30-second treatment can improve mood and prefrontal connectivity, a refined version could potentially enhance the patience, emotional stability, and long-term thinking our paper argues are necessary.
