## Argument

This section identifies the specific human capacities most relevant to AI governance. It argues that stewardship demands not raw intelligence but a broader cluster of cognitive, affective, and social capacities — and that the goal should be wiser and more cooperative cognition, not merely faster or more productive minds.

## Evidence Bridge

1. Decision science and cognitive psychology document the specific capacities that matter under high-stakes, high-uncertainty conditions.
2. The eight capacities map directly onto the governance challenges identified in Sections II–IV: the cognitive limits, timeline pressure, and dependency risks each implicate specific capacities.
3. The table format connects each capacity to both its governance rationale and the intervention paths in VIII–X, making the paper's structure explicit.

## Draft (~600 words, 2 paragraphs + table)

The capacities most relevant to AI stewardship are not reducible to raw intelligence. Processing speed, memory, and analytical power matter, but the governance challenges posed by advanced AI demand a broader cluster of cognitive, affective, and social capacities — what this paper calls *stewardship capacities*: the cluster of abilities most relevant to governing powerful systems under uncertainty. The following table identifies eight such capacities, explains why each matters for AI governance specifically, and maps each to the intervention paths developed in Sections VIII–X.

| Capacity | Why it matters for AI governance | Example intervention paths |
|----------|----------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Long-term thinking | Resist race incentives, plan beyond immediate advantage, weigh consequences across decades | Contemplative training, psychedelic-assisted therapy |
| Metacognition | Notice one's own biases, dependencies, limits of understanding, and susceptibility to manipulation | Mindfulness, neurofeedback, reflective education |
| Attention and cognitive endurance | Stay engaged with complex systems rather than outsourcing comprehension | Attentional training, therapeutic support, executive-function development |
| Emotional regulation | Sustain sound judgment under uncertainty, conflict, and existential stress | Psychotherapy, meditation, neurostimulation |
| Empathy and social cognition | Support cooperation, institutional legitimacy, and human solidarity | Compassion practice, social-emotional learning, psychedelic-assisted therapy |
| Epistemic humility | Recognize the limits of one's knowledge in domains too complex for any individual to fully master | Reflective pedagogy, metacognitive training |
| Civic reasoning | Connect technical choices to long-term public consequences | Civic education, reflective pedagogy |
| Resistance to dependency and manipulation | Maintain agency in environments optimized to reward outsourcing and passivity | Metacognitive training, critical media literacy |

The list is not intended as exhaustive; it identifies the capacities most directly implicated by the governance failures, timeline pressures, and dependency risks outlined in the preceding sections. Taken together, these capacities describe the kind of mind advanced AI governance requires, even if no individual or institution will embody them perfectly. Several features of this list deserve emphasis. First, many of these capacities are relational and affective rather than narrowly cognitive. Empathy, emotional regulation, and civic reasoning are not usually classified as "intelligence," yet they may matter as much for AI governance as any form of analytical skill. Second, the capacities overlap and reinforce one another: metacognition supports resistance to manipulation, emotional regulation supports long-term thinking, and epistemic humility supports cooperation. Third, the list is oriented toward *judgment* rather than *performance*. The goal is not to produce faster or more productive minds, but wiser, more reflective, and more cooperative ones. A governance system populated by highly intelligent actors who lack empathy, epistemic humility, or resistance to manipulation would not be safer — it might be more dangerous. The normative core of this paper is that stewardship requires not maximal cognitive power but a particular *quality* of cognition, and that this quality of cognition can, at least in principle, be cultivated.
