## Summary

Yadav develops a philosophical framework for "cognitive sustainability" -- the preservation of human expertise and cognitive autonomy in increasingly AI-dependent systems. Drawing on Clark and Chalmers's extended mind theory (1998) and Hutchins's distributed cognition (1995), Yadav investigates the ontological and ethical implications of AI systems that do not merely extend human cognition but potentially substitute for core cognitive processes. The paper's central contribution is a mathematical model of expertise atrophy: individual expertise decay follows exponential patterns modulated by a "Cognitive Retention Coefficient," while organizational patterns follow S-curve dynamics that mask underlying erosion until critical thresholds are crossed. The framework identifies four pathways of AI-induced competency decay and introduces the concept of "cognitive stratification" -- a new form of social inequality based on differential cognitive dependency on AI systems. This is arguably the closest existing work to our manuscript's atrophy/dependency concern.

## Key Findings

- AI systems increasingly substitute for (rather than extend) core cognitive processes, creating a qualitatively different relationship than traditional cognitive tools
- **Four pathways of competency decay**:
  1. Individual skill atrophy -- direct erosion of practiced cognitive abilities through disuse
  2. Structural erosion of expertise development systems -- training pipelines and apprenticeship models disrupted
  3. Systemic organizational vulnerability -- institutional knowledge becomes brittle as human backup expertise erodes
  4. Fundamental redefinition of cognitive requirements -- what counts as "necessary knowledge" shifts, potentially hollowing out deep expertise
- **Cognitive Retention Coefficient**: A mathematical formalization of how individual expertise decay follows exponential patterns when cognitive tasks are offloaded to AI
- **S-curve organizational dynamics**: At the organizational level, competency erosion follows S-curve patterns that mask underlying decay -- performance appears stable until a critical threshold, then collapses rapidly
- Empirical support from WEF skills forecasts (2023-2025): fundamental cognitive capabilities like mathematics are being recategorized from "core skills" to "out-of-focus skills" with a net decline in importance (-4%) as AI adoption accelerates
- **Cognitive stratification**: AI dependency creates a new axis of social inequality where populations divide into those who retain autonomous cognitive capability and those who become functionally dependent
- Yadav challenges the extended mind thesis itself: cognitive offloading to AI is not a "natural extension of mind" but a "compensatory response to cognitive fatigue" -- effort substitution rather than genuine cognitive expansion

## Methodology

Philosophical framework analysis combined with mathematical modeling. Uses extended mind theory and distributed cognition as theoretical foundations, develops formal mathematical models (exponential decay with Cognitive Retention Coefficient for individuals, S-curve dynamics for organizations), and validates against WEF empirical workforce data. Also engages with the parity principle from philosophy of mind.

## Relevance to This Work

**Most directly relevant paper to our core thesis.** Yadav's cognitive sustainability framework is essentially the problem our manuscript proposes to solve. His four pathways of competency decay map precisely onto the concerns we raise about AI dependency undermining human governance capacity. The Cognitive Retention Coefficient could provide formal backing for our argument that cognitive atrophy follows predictable, measurable patterns. The cognitive stratification concept strengthens our equity argument (Section X): if AI creates cognitive haves and have-nots, then cognitive enhancement as governance strategy must address distributional concerns. His challenge to extended mind theory -- that AI offloading is compensatory rather than constitutive -- supports our argument that humans need to actively maintain and enhance cognitive capacity rather than passively cede it to AI systems. The S-curve organizational dynamics (appearing stable until sudden collapse) is especially relevant to our governance gap argument: institutions may appear to function normally while human expertise quietly erodes beneath the surface.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- How does the Cognitive Retention Coefficient compare to empirical data on skill decay in specific domains (e.g., medical diagnosis, navigation after GPS adoption)?
- Can the framework be extended to predict at what level of AI integration the S-curve collapse becomes likely?
- Does Yadav address potential interventions -- i.e., cognitive enhancement as a countermeasure to the erosion he describes? If not, this is precisely the gap our manuscript fills
- How does cognitive stratification interact with existing socioeconomic stratification? Do they reinforce each other or operate independently?
- The challenge to extended mind theory is provocative -- does Yadav account for cases where AI genuinely extends rather than substitutes for cognition?

## Key Quote

"Such off-loading is not a natural extension of mind but a compensatory response to cognitive fatigue." -- Yadav's reframing of cognitive offloading to AI, arguing that unlike genuine cognitive extension, AI dependency represents effort substitution that leaves the underlying cognitive capacity to atrophy. This directly supports our manuscript's argument that passive AI reliance degrades the human cognitive infrastructure needed for governance.
