## Summary

Ilieva, Boland, and Farah conducted an adequately powered double-blind crossover placebo-controlled trial testing whether mixed amphetamine salts (MAS/Adderall) enhance cognition in healthy young adults. Across 13 cognitive measures spanning memory, working memory, inhibitory control, creativity, intelligence, and scholastic achievement, MAS produced no significant enhancement for participants overall. However, participants nevertheless believed the drug improved their performance more than placebo -- demonstrating a critical dissociation between perceived and actual cognitive enhancement.

## Key Findings

- No significant enhancement on any of 13 cognitive measures (episodic memory, working memory, inhibitory control, creativity, Raven's Progressive Matrices, SAT Math, SAT Verbal) from 20mg MAS vs. placebo
- The study was powered at 95% to detect a medium effect size (Cohen's d >= 0.5) -- null results are meaningful, not just underpowered
- Despite null objective results, participants perceived MAS as significantly more enhancing than placebo (M = 55.18 vs. 50.25 on 1-100 scale, p = .05) -- a "pseudo-enhancement" or illusory enhancement effect
- Perceived enhancement did not correlate with actual enhancement (r = -.06, p = .76 for composite score)
- Suggestion of moderation by baseline ability: lower-performing participants showed enhancement on word recall (p = .02), embedded figures (p = .01), and Raven's Matrices (p = .03), while higher performers did not
- Mixed evidence for COMT genotype moderation: val-val carriers showed relatively greater enhancement on SAT Math consistent with the inverted-U dopamine model
- The authors attribute the mixed positive findings in the published literature to low study power, flexibility in outcome selection, and publication bias -- citing Ioannidis (2005) on "why most published research findings are false"

## Methodology

- Double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover design
- N = 46 healthy Caucasian adults (22 male, 24 female), ages 21-30 (M = 24)
- 20mg mixed amphetamine salts (equivalent to Adderall) vs. placebo in visually indistinguishable capsules
- 7 sessions total: practice, 2 baseline (no pill), 4 on-pill sessions (2 MAS, 2 placebo, counterbalanced)
- Independent baseline measurement to avoid regression-to-mean confounds
- COMT genotyping for dopamine-related moderation analysis
- 13 cognitive measures: face recognition, word recall, word recognition, digit span forward/backward, object 2-back, go/no-go, flanker, remote associations, embedded figures, Raven's matrices, SAT math, SAT verbal
- Perceived enhancement measured via 1-100 self-report scale
- Exclusion criteria: prior stimulant use, psychiatric conditions, high blood pressure; urine drug testing at each session

## Relevance to This Work

**Critical for Section V (pseudo-enhancement evidence).** This is the key empirical paper demonstrating that pharmacological "cognitive enhancement" with stimulants may be largely illusory in healthy people. The dissociation between subjective belief in enhancement and objective null results directly supports our argument that current pharmacological approaches are insufficient for genuine capacity-building. The finding that users feel enhanced while not actually being enhanced is a powerful cautionary finding for any governance strategy that relies on pharmacological cognitive enhancement. It also highlights why our manuscript emphasizes non-pharmacological approaches (contemplative practices, neurostimulation targeting specific circuits) over the "smart drugs" narrative. The moderation-by-baseline finding -- that only lower performers benefit -- is relevant to our discussion of who enhancement might actually help.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- How do these null results for stimulants compare to effect sizes for tDCS, TMS, and neurofeedback in healthy populations? This comparison could strengthen our argument for non-pharmacological approaches.
- The crossover design used healthy young adults presumably near their cognitive ceiling. Would results differ for older adults or those under cognitive load/sleep deprivation?
- Ilieva et al. 2015 (PMID 25591060) published a follow-up meta-analysis in JOCN on stimulants' effects on inhibitory control, working memory, and episodic memory in healthy people -- should track that down for more comprehensive effect size estimates.
- The "feels like enhancement" phenomenon may explain why pharmacological enhancement has cultural traction despite weak evidence -- relevant to public attitudes section and Schelle et al. 2014 review.
- The confidence-without-competence finding has direct relevance to AI governance -- people who feel more cognitively capable may make riskier decisions. Enhancement approaches must improve actual decision quality, not just confidence.
- The baseline-dependent effect (lower performers benefit more) supports a "cognitive equity" framing -- enhancement may be most effective and ethically justified for those who need it most.

## Key Quote

> "Despite the lack of enhancement observed for most measures and most participants, participants nevertheless believed their performance was more enhanced by the active capsule than by placebo. We conclude that MAS has no more than small effects on cognition in healthy young adults, although users may perceive the drug as enhancing their cognition."
