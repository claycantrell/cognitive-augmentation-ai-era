## Summary

Grace, Salvatier, Dafoe, Zhang, and Evans surveyed 352 machine learning researchers (21% response rate from ~1,634 contacted) who had published at NeurIPS or ICML 2015 about their predictions for when AI would surpass human performance across a range of tasks. The survey elicited both task-specific milestones and aggregate predictions for "high-level machine intelligence" (HLMI) — defined as AI that can perform every task better and more cheaply than human workers. The results reveal wide variance and significant demographic differences in predictions, underscoring deep uncertainty about AI timelines even among domain experts.

## Key Findings

- **50% probability of HLMI in 45 years** (from 2016, i.e., ~2061), with full automation of all human jobs predicted at 120 years (~2136)
- **Specific milestone predictions (50% probability):**
  - Translating languages: by 2024
  - Writing high-school essays: by 2026
  - Driving a truck: by 2027
  - Working in retail: by 2031
  - Writing a bestselling book: by 2049
  - Working as a surgeon: by 2053
- **Large demographic variation:** Asian respondents predicted HLMI 44 years sooner than North American respondents on average
- **Question framing effects:** When asked about "full automation of labor" vs. "high-level machine intelligence," respondents gave substantially different timelines despite these being conceptually similar — suggesting anchoring effects and genuine uncertainty
- **Wide confidence intervals:** Individual predictions spanned decades, reflecting fundamental disagreement within the expert community
- **536+ citations** as of the search queue documentation, making it one of the most-cited AI forecasting papers

## Methodology

- **Sample:** 352 ML researchers who published at NeurIPS or ICML 2015
- **Response rate:** ~21% of 1,634 contacted
- **Survey design:** Elicited predictions for specific task milestones and aggregate HLMI/automation timelines
- **Analysis:** Aggregated median predictions with confidence intervals; compared responses by researcher demographics (region, career stage)

## Relevance to This Work

**Foundation for Section III (The Timeline Problem).** This paper provides the canonical expert survey on AI timelines that our manuscript cites to establish the governance urgency argument. Key uses:

1. **The variance IS the point:** The wide disagreement among experts — with some predicting HLMI within 10 years and others within 100+ — is itself evidence for why we cannot wait for consensus before building human capacity. If even ML researchers don't know when transformative AI arrives, governance strategies must be robust to a wide range of timelines.
2. **Supports the "neglected prerequisite" framing:** If human oversight is expected to remain critical for decades (even optimistic predictions place full automation far out), then the quality of human judgment operating inside governance systems matters enormously.
3. **Framing effects underscore cognitive limitations:** The finding that how you phrase the question changes expert predictions by decades illustrates exactly the kind of cognitive vulnerability our paper argues needs addressing.
4. **Demographic variation raises equity concerns:** Different cultural/regional perspectives on AI timelines suggest that governance frameworks will need to account for diverse stakeholder expectations.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- How have these predictions shifted since 2018? More recent surveys (e.g., Metaculus, AI Impacts 2022 update) show acceleration in predicted timelines — worth noting in Section III's "estimates vary widely" hedge.
- The framing effect finding could be cited more explicitly in our Section II discussion of cognitive biases in governance contexts.
- Response rate of 21% introduces potential selection bias — respondents may be systematically more or less optimistic than non-respondents.
