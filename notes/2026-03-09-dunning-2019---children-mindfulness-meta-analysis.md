## Summary

Meta-analysis of 33 randomized controlled trials (3,666 participants) examining the effects of mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs) on cognition and mental health in children and adolescents. When all RCT designs were included, MBIs showed positive effects across mindfulness, executive functioning, attention, depression, anxiety/stress, and negative behaviors (effect sizes d = .16 to .30). However, when restricted to studies with active control conditions (17 trials, 1,762 participants), significant effects narrowed to mindfulness (d = .42), depression (d = .47), and anxiety/stress (d = .18) only. This is a high-impact review (563 citations) that provides the most rigorous assessment of youth mindfulness interventions available.

## Key Findings

- **Broad benefits across all RCT designs:** Positive effects on mindfulness, executive functioning, attention, depression, anxiety/stress, and negative behaviors with modest effect sizes (d = .16 to .30)
- **Narrower benefits with active controls:** When restricted to active-control studies only, significant improvements limited to mindfulness (d = .42), depression (d = .47), and anxiety/stress (d = .18)
- **Executive functioning and attention effects did not survive** the more rigorous active-control comparison — suggesting some early findings may reflect expectancy or attention effects rather than mindfulness-specific mechanisms
- **Depression effect is notably robust** (d = .47 even against active controls) — strongest evidence for MBI impact in youth
- **33 RCTs, 3,666 participants** — substantial evidence base, though authors note need for larger definitive trials
- The authors recommend future work focus on **mechanisms of action** — understanding why MBIs work when they do

## Methodology

- Systematic search and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials only (gold standard methodology)
- 33 RCTs included, totaling 3,666 children and adolescent participants
- Key methodological innovation: separate analyses for all-RCT designs vs. active-control-only designs, revealing which effects are robust to more rigorous comparison conditions
- Effect sizes reported as Cohen's d
- Examined six outcome categories: mindfulness, executive functioning, attention, depression, anxiety/stress, and negative behaviors
