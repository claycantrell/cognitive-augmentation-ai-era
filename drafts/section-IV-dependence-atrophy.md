## Argument

This section shifts from external risk (Sections II–III: governance lag, timeline pressure) to internal risk: the possibility that AI gradually erodes the very human capacities needed for self-governance, even in the absence of catastrophe. It introduces the concept of civilizational atrophy and sets up the agency/dependency distinction that Section V develops.

## Evidence Bridge

1. Earlier digital technologies already demonstrated that systems optimized for engagement can degrade attention, self-regulation, and social cohesion. The precedent is established — this is not speculative.
2. Advanced AI may deepen these dynamics by making cognitive, emotional, and social outsourcing frictionless. The risk is not only that AI does things for us, but that the ease of outsourcing weakens the underlying capacities.
3. Yadav (2025) formalizes this as "competency erosion" — AI systems substituting for core cognitive processes rather than extending them, producing skill decay at individual and organizational levels.
4. The cognitive offloading literature documents a specific mechanism: when external tools handle cognitive work, the internal capacity for that work may atrophy rather than develop.
5. The result is not catastrophe but what might be called civilizational atrophy — a gradual erosion of the cognitive, motivational, and relational capacities needed for meaningful self-government.
6. This sets up Section V's central question: the issue is not whether enhancement exists, but whether it builds agency or deepens dependency.

## Draft (~700 words, 3 paragraphs)

One risk from advanced AI is not catastrophe but comfortable dependency. This risk is not speculative. Earlier digital technologies have already demonstrated how systems optimized for convenience and engagement can degrade the capacities they appear to support. Research on cognitive offloading has documented the pattern: when external tools reliably handle cognitive tasks — navigation, memory, arithmetic, information retrieval — the internal capacities for those tasks may weaken rather than develop. The question is not whether offloading occurs — it clearly does, and it can be efficient — but whether it produces net gains in autonomous functioning or net losses. Smartphones and social media offered a first large-scale demonstration: platforms engineered to capture and hold attention have been associated with declining attentional control, reduced tolerance for boredom and difficulty, and weakened self-regulation, particularly among populations whose capacities are still developing. The debate about effect sizes continues, but the underlying dynamic — that systems designed to make things easier can make people less capable — is no longer seriously in question.

Advanced AI may deepen these dynamics in ways that go well beyond what earlier technologies produced. If smartphones created frictionless access to information, advanced AI creates frictionless access to judgment. The shift matters because the capacities most relevant to governance — sustained reasoning, metacognitive monitoring, emotional self-regulation, tolerance for ambiguity, motivation to engage with difficult problems — are precisely the capacities that frictionless outsourcing is most likely to erode. Yadav has described this as "competency erosion," arguing that AI systems increasingly substitute for core cognitive processes rather than merely extending them, producing skill decay at both individual and organizational levels [@yadav2025]. The potential dynamics are broad: reduced motivation to learn when answers are instantly available, diminished tolerance for effort and ambiguity when AI handles complexity, dependence on machine judgment when human judgment atrophies from disuse, replacement of difficult human relationships with optimized artificial ones, and shrinking public comprehension of the systems that increasingly govern social life. The result may be what this paper calls civilizational atrophy — a gradual erosion of the cognitive, motivational, and relational capacities needed for meaningful self-government. This is not a scenario of annihilation. It is a scenario in which populations become increasingly comfortable, passive, and incapable of meaningful self-direction — a best-case dystopia in which the failure mode is not that AI destroys agency, but that it makes agency unnecessary and therefore vestigial.

The question this section raises is therefore not whether AI will assist humanity — it will, and in many domains it should — but whether the pattern of assistance gradually weakens the capacities needed to remain agents rather than dependents. This is the first substantive appearance of the distinction that structures the rest of the paper. An AI system that handles routine cognitive tasks so that a person can focus on higher-order judgment is functioning differently from one that handles so much cognitive work that the person's capacity for higher-order judgment never develops or slowly decays. The first is a tool; the second is a dependency-producing substitute. Section V develops this distinction into the paper's central evaluative framework. But the concern identified here — that advanced AI may erode the very capacities that governance requires humans to possess — is what makes that framework necessary.
