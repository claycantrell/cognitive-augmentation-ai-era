## Summary

Proposes a research framework for empirically studying mindfulness meditation as a developmental process — not just an 8-week intervention but a skill that changes the practitioner over months, years, and decades. Argues that meditation effects aren't linear: complex interactions between practice duration, accumulated skill, and individual history produce a wide range of experiences, from profoundly positive to potentially harmful. Calls for studying the full arc of meditative development with appropriate methodology.

## Key Findings

- Meditation is a developmental process with distinct stages, not a static intervention
- An estimated half of people who learn mindfulness continue practicing regularly, but long-term effects are poorly studied
- Effects vary systematically over time based on dosage, accumulated practice, developing skill, and interaction with past experiences
- Both positive and challenging/harmful experiences can arise — the field needs to study both
- Current research focuses almost entirely on beginners (8-week MBSR); the advanced stages are largely unstudied scientifically
- 66 citations — influential in the contemplative science field

## Methodology

- Review paper proposing a theoretical framework
- Synthesizes existing research on mindfulness meditation outcomes
- Proposes developmental stages and research methodology for studying them

## Relevance to This Work

**Supports Section VI — the timeline problem.** This paper establishes that meditation produces real, staged cognitive changes but that these develop over very long timescales. The framework they propose implicitly reveals the problem our paper addresses: if meditation-induced cognitive changes require years or decades of practice, and we need enhanced cognition urgently (within the AGI timeline), then we need technological shortcuts to these developmental stages. The paper is the scientific basis for saying "meditation works, but it's too slow."
