## Nyholm 2024
- **Core claim:** AI may enhance cognition via "extended mind" or merely produce quasi-enhancement; cognitive atrophy is a real risk of AI adoption.
- **Overlap:** Shares our concern that AI can hollow out capacities rather than build them; flags dependency as a central problem.
- **Difference:** Philosophical/conceptual analysis of enhancement categories — no governance framework, no prescriptive agenda, no institutional lens.
- **Quote:** Use his taxonomy of "genuine enhancement" vs "quasi-enhancement" as a foil for our Section V distinction.

## Laux 2023
- **Core claim:** Human overseers of AI are systematically unreliable; institutional design should anticipate this fallibility rather than assume competence.
- **Overlap:** Directly supports our bottleneck claim — governance assumes human capacities that may not exist.
- **Difference:** His solution is institutional design (distrust structures); ours is building the capacities themselves. Complementary, not competing.
- **Quote:** "Overseeing automated systems is an impossible assignment for humans" — use to motivate the capacity gap in Section II/III.

## Lara 2021
- **Core claim:** AI moral enhancement must aim at reflective self-determination, not behavioral steering; proposes "SocrAI" (Socratic AI assistant).
- **Overlap:** Shares our autonomy-over-control principle; argues enhancement should strengthen deliberation, not replace it.
- **Difference:** Focused narrowly on moral deliberation via a specific tool design; our paper addresses governance strategy across cognitive, emotional, and temporal capacities.
- **Quote:** "The objective now would be for the user, with the exercise of their deliberative capacities, to learn to decide better and, with time, this would favour the ability to do so on one's own."

## Volkman & Gabriels 2023
- **Core claim:** AI should serve as plural interactive mentors (trained in distinct wisdom traditions) that cultivate practical wisdom through Socratic dialogue, not replace moral reasoning.
- **Overlap:** Same agency-over-dependency principle; warns of "moral disengagement" and "atrophy of human faculties."
- **Difference:** Designing a better tool for moral education; our paper argues the entire governance paradigm must account for capacity-building as a first-order policy concern.
- **Quote:** "Relying on an AI to do our thinking for us not only neglects the cultivation of moral excellence but actively undermines it, exposing agents to risks of disengagement, atrophy of human faculties, and moral manipulation at the hands of the AI or its creators."

## Gordon & Seth 2024
- **Core claim:** Enhancement BCIs raise fundamental questions about selfhood, privacy, autonomy, inequality, and cognitive diversity; warns of "mental monoculture."
- **Overlap:** Shares our concern about privacy/autonomy risks and cognitive monoculture — directly relevant to Section XIV.
- **Difference:** Ethics of a specific technology (BCIs); our paper treats BCI as one case within a broader governance framework spanning pharmacological, therapeutic, educational, and contemplative interventions.
- **Quote:** Use "mental monoculture" concept to ground Section XIV's cognitive diversity concern.

## Yadav 2025
- **Core claim:** AI systems don't merely extend cognition but substitute for core cognitive processes, producing expertise atrophy and "cognitive stratification" between populations that retain skills and those that don't.
- **Overlap:** Same diagnosis — AI adoption hollows out capacities needed for autonomous agency; same concern about inequality.
- **Difference:** Primarily diagnostic (formalizes decay dynamics with mathematical models); our paper is primarily prescriptive (names the governance gap and argues for a design principle: build agency, not dependency).
- **Quote:** "AI systems don't merely extend human cognition but potentially substitute for core cognitive processes."
