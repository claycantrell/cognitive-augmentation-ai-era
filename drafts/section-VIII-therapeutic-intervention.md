## Argument

This section argues that the most promising near-term path toward strengthening human capacity for AI governance is not standalone enhancement but assisted adult intervention — combined approaches where pharmacology or neurotechnology catalyzes learning within a therapeutic framework. The key empirical pattern is that the tool matters less than the scaffolding around it. Psychedelic-assisted therapy, brain stimulation + psychotherapy, and restorative psychopharmacology each illustrate this principle.

## Evidence Bridge

1. The standalone vs combined distinction is empirically grounded: combined interventions consistently outperform standalone ones for durable capacity changes.
2. Psychedelic-assisted therapy is the paradigm case: Mitchell et al. 2021 (MDMA Phase 3) demonstrates that a pharmacological catalyst properly scaffolded can produce durable changes in emotional processing and self-understanding.
3. NIBS + psychotherapy meta-analysis (17 studies, 660 participants) extends this to brain stimulation: neuromodulation paired with therapy outperforms either alone.
4. Restorative psychopharmacology (ketamine/esketamine, aggression treatments) shows that treating capacity-eroding conditions is itself a form of capacity-building, but with important variation across symptom domains.
5. The FDA rejection of MDMA-assisted therapy (August 2024) illustrates regulatory caution, not scientific failure — the evidentiary bar is high.
6. All of this converges on the paper's central argument: the scaffolding/substitution distinction applies to therapeutic intervention, not just enhancement.

## Draft (~1000 words, 5 paragraphs)

A major near-term path toward strengthening human capacity for AI governance is not pharmacological or neurotechnological enhancement in isolation, but *assisted adult intervention* — combined approaches in which a pharmacological or neurotechnological tool acts as a catalyst for learning, emotional processing, or behavioral change within a therapeutic framework. The distinction matters because, in several of the most relevant domains, the evidence suggests that standalone interventions often produce weaker or less durable effects than combined ones. A pharmacological intervention may relieve symptoms, and psychotherapy may improve self-regulation; but when pharmacology is embedded in structured therapeutic support, the combination may produce changes that neither achieves as reliably alone. This principle — that the tool matters less than the scaffolding around it — is one of the most important empirical patterns relevant to this paper's argument. Three cases illustrate the pattern: psychedelic-assisted therapy, brain stimulation paired with psychotherapy, and restorative psychopharmacology aimed at conditions that directly erode agency.

Psychedelic-assisted therapy illustrates the combined-intervention model with unusual clarity. In this approach, a psychedelic compound (psilocybin, MDMA, or a related substance) is administered in a controlled therapeutic setting, with professional guidance during the experience and structured integration sessions afterward. The intervention is not the drug; it is the process — altered state, guided support, and meaning-making combined. Early clinical evidence has been promising: Mitchell et al. reported that MDMA-assisted therapy produced significant reductions in PTSD severity compared to therapy with placebo, with 67% of participants in the MDMA group no longer meeting diagnostic criteria at follow-up [@mitchell2021]. Psilocybin-assisted therapy has shown similar promise for treatment-resistant depression. The importance of these results for this paper is conceptual as much as clinical: they suggest that a pharmacological tool, properly scaffolded, can contribute to durable changes in emotional processing, self-understanding, and behavioral flexibility — capacities directly relevant to stewardship. The FDA's rejection of MDMA-assisted therapy in August 2024, citing methodological concerns about blinding and functional unblinding, illustrates the regulatory caution that currently governs this space. It does not by itself invalidate the combined-intervention model, but it does underscore that the evidentiary bar remains high and the path from promising results to approved treatment is neither fast nor guaranteed.

Noninvasive brain stimulation offers a parallel case. Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) has received FDA clearance for major depressive disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and smoking cessation, among other indications, establishing that targeted neuromodulation can produce clinically meaningful effects. Research combining brain stimulation with psychotherapy suggests that the two may be more effective together than either alone: a meta-analysis of noninvasive brain stimulation paired with psychotherapy found enhanced outcomes across 17 studies involving 660 participants. The evidence here remains diagnosis- and protocol-specific, but it points toward a broader pattern: neuromodulatory tools may be most valuable when used to support therapeutic learning rather than replace it. The framework extends naturally to other combinations — medication plus therapy, contemplative training plus coaching, device-assisted behavior modification with structured integration — in each case asking whether the combination produces durable gains in the capacities that matter for stewardship.

Restorative psychopharmacology represents a related but distinct dimension of adult intervention. Severe depression, chronic emotional dysregulation, pathological anger, and psychotic symptoms can directly erode the capacities this paper identifies as governance-relevant — agency, patience, trust, sustained reasoning, and cooperative judgment. The psychopharmacological landscape is shifting toward more targeted and faster-acting treatments, with ketamine and esketamine representing the most advanced case: rapid-acting antidepressants that can produce meaningful symptom relief within hours rather than weeks. But the picture varies significantly across symptom domains. Depression is the strongest case for pharmacological optimism; anger and aggression present a more mixed picture, with psychological treatments often matching or outperforming medication overall; and psychotic symptoms remain a cautionary case, where pharmacological management involves significant side-effect tradeoffs and where coercion concerns are most acute. The pattern reinforces the paper's broader argument: the most durable and agency-respecting outcomes tend to emerge from combined models in which pharmacology supports therapeutic work rather than substituting for it.

Why does this matter for AI governance specifically? The answer is not that assisted therapeutic intervention will produce radically superior minds. It is that even modest, widely distributed improvements in self-regulation, metacognition, emotional stability, and resistance to manipulation may matter under compressed timelines and extraordinary decision-making pressure. If the quality of human judgment operating inside governance institutions is a real constraint on AI safety, then interventions that improve that judgment even marginally deserve serious strategic attention. The near-term value of assisted adult intervention lies less in transformation than in resilience: slightly better judgment, slightly greater emotional stability, slightly stronger resistance to dependency, applied across the populations responsible for governing the most consequential technology in human history.
