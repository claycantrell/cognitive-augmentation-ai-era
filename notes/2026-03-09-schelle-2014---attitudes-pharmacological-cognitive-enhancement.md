## Summary

A systematic review of 40 empirical studies examining public attitudes toward pharmacological cognitive enhancement (PCE) -- the off-label use of prescription stimulants (methylphenidate/Ritalin, amphetamine/Adderall, modafinil/Provigil) by healthy individuals to improve mental performance. The review organizes findings around three primary public concerns: medical safety, coercion, and fairness (subdivided into equality of opportunity, honesty, and authenticity). The overarching finding is that public concerns largely mirror the normative academic debate, but with notable divergences between users and nonusers: users view PCE as safer and fairer than nonusers do. Published in Frontiers in Systems Neuroscience as part of a Research Topic on "Augmentation of Brain Function."

## Key Findings

- **Medical safety is the top public concern:** Nonusers view PCE as addictive, potentially inducing sleep disorders and mental health problems. The riskier the substance, the more people morally object. Natural remedies are perceived as less harmful than pharmaceutical enhancers.
- **Users vs. nonusers divergence:** Users consistently rate health risks as lower, deem PCE more morally acceptable, and have fewer fairness concerns than nonusers. This may reflect (a) risk-tolerant individuals self-selecting into use, (b) cognitive dissonance reduction, or (c) familiarity reducing perceived risk.
- **Both users and nonusers overestimate enhancement effects:** Reference to Ilieva et al. 2013 (and Finger et al. 2013) showing that people systematically overestimate how much these drugs actually improve cognition.
- **Coercion concerns are consistent across groups:** All subgroups (students, parents, healthcare providers) agree PCE should be a personal choice. But they recognize social/competitive pressure can undermine truly autonomous decisions. Peer pressure is complex -- students may not be influenced by others' *use* per se, but by others' *superior performance*.
- **Fairness is multidimensional:**
  - *Equality of opportunity:* Unequal access due to wealth is seen as unfair; unequal distribution due to biological disposition (e.g., low attention span) is more acceptable. This mirrors the treatment vs. enhancement distinction.
  - *Honesty:* ~50-60% of students view PCE as providing an unfair advantage; it is seen as more wrong in competitive contexts.
  - *Authenticity:* Public is roughly split -- about half believe PCE undermines authentic effort, the other half note "the work still has to be done" even with PCE.
- **Treatment seen as more acceptable than enhancement:** Consistent finding across studies that therapeutic use of the same substance is viewed more favorably than enhancement use.
- **Physicians are cautious gatekeepers:** Less comfortable prescribing PCE to young adults; worry about misuse; deem PCE for young people unnecessary but more open for elderly patients with declining cognition.
- **Status quo bias and cognitive dissonance** may explain user/nonuser attitude differences. Nonusers may irrationally prefer the status quo; users may adjust beliefs to match behavior.

## Methodology

- **Literature search:** Web of Science and Scopus databases, 1990-2014, using comprehensive search terms for cognitive enhancement and attitudes/opinions. Supplemented with Google Scholar, reference list cross-checking, and expert consultation.
- **Included:** 40 publications reporting empirical studies (qualitative and quantitative) on attitudes of groups outside the normative academic debate (students, physicians, parents, general public) toward PCE.
- **Countries represented:** USA, Canada, UK, Germany, Netherlands, Switzerland, Australia, Sweden, and a nine-country European citizens' panel.
- **Methods in reviewed studies:** Surveys/questionnaires (most common), focus groups, semi-structured interviews, experimental vignettes, and factorial design studies. Sample sizes range from 12 to 6,275.

## Relevance to This Work

**Essential source for understanding public acceptability of cognitive enhancement -- a critical governance consideration.** Key uses:

1. **Public concerns map onto governance challenges:** The three concerns (safety, coercion, fairness) identified in this review correspond directly to governance challenges our paper must address. Any cognitive enhancement-as-AI-governance proposal must show: (a) enhancement is safe, (b) it is not coercive, and (c) it does not exacerbate inequality.
2. **The user/nonuser divide is strategically important:** If the main opposition to cognitive enhancement comes from those who have not experienced it, this suggests that pilot programs and transparent evidence could shift attitudes. But it also means we cannot dismiss safety concerns as uninformed -- they may represent genuine risk aversion.
3. **Treatment vs. enhancement framing matters:** The finding that therapeutic use is more accepted than enhancement use suggests our paper should frame cognitive enhancement as "cognitive health maintenance" or "cognitive resilience" in the age of AI, rather than as "getting smarter than normal."
4. **Coercion concerns are the most consistent:** Even supporters of PCE worry about coercive pressure. Our proposal must be explicitly voluntary and include protections against workplace or social mandates to enhance.
5. **Equality of opportunity is a leverage point:** The public already finds it acceptable to provide cognitive enhancement to those with lower baseline performance. This aligns with a "cognitive equity" frame for our proposal -- enhancing the most vulnerable to AI-driven cognitive demands.
6. **The overestimation of effects** (both by users and nonusers) means that public debate about cognitive enhancement is not well-calibrated to actual evidence. Our paper should help set realistic expectations.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- How have attitudes shifted since 2014? The rise of AI (ChatGPT, etc.) may have changed how people think about cognitive enhancement.
- The review focuses on pharmacological enhancement -- are attitudes toward neurostimulation (tDCS, TMS) different? People may view non-pharmacological approaches more favorably.
- The "authenticity" split is fascinating -- roughly half the public does not view enhancement as undermining authentic achievement. Can this frame be leveraged?
- The European citizens' panel recommendation that "people should be given the right to take whatever drug they want" (with information) is a remarkable data point for cognitive liberty arguments.
- How do these attitudes vary by cultural context? The review covers mostly Western democracies -- attitudes in East Asia, where academic competition is intense, might differ substantially.

## Key Quote

It also arouses strong suspicion of being indeed one-sided, biased or ideological -- thus illegitimate.'" (Schicktanz et al. 2012, quoted in the review)
