## Summary

Richard Volkman and Katleen Gabriels critically examine proposals for using AI to augment or replace human moral reasoning. They distinguish between "exhaustive enhancement" (AI as an oracle delivering correct moral answers) and "auxiliary enhancement" (AI as a support tool), arguing that both face serious problems. Exhaustive enhancement presumes moral knowledge "we are still in the process of working out" and risks moral disengagement; even modest auxiliary approaches either remain so neutral as to be unhelpful or collapse into exhaustive enhancement for users lacking the "correct" values. Their alternative proposal is a modular system of multiple AI interlocutors, each trained in a distinct wisdom tradition (e.g., Stoicism, Buddhism, Aristotelianism, Confucianism), functioning as interactive mentors rather than decision-makers. This system models the actual "socio-technical system of moral engagement" -- the historical process of diverse perspectives challenging one another through information technology -- and upgrades it with AI. Crucially, the system preserves moral pluralism by design and positions the user as "co-author and ultimate publisher and editor" of their own moral development.

## Key Findings

- **Critique of exhaustive enhancement:**
  - Presumes determinable moral truth; "without humans doing the thinking for themselves, there would be no standard against which to measure the accuracy and reliability of the machines."
  - Risks moral disengagement -- users become unable to "answer questions for ourselves," which is "a necessary part of the still open-ended process."
  - Moral judgment requires "complex interplay of affective and cognitive faculties" that atrophies without practice.
  - Jonathan Haidt's "moral dumbfounding" research shows humans don't fully understand their own moral reasoning, making it premature to encode it algorithmically.

- **Critique of even auxiliary enhancement:**
  - The pluralism dilemma: Systems must either remain neutral (limited value) or embed substantive values (collapsing into exhaustive enhancement).
  - "Pluralism itself is controversial" -- there is no neutral ground from which to design a single AI moral advisor.
  - Ideal Observer theory -- the conceptual basis for many proposals -- remains "a marginal player" in normative theory.

- **The modular system proposal:**
  - Multiple AI interlocutors, each embodying "a distinct point of view reflecting their training in a diversity of concrete wisdom traditions."
  - Examples: Virtual Socrates, Epictetus, Confucius; modules trained in Stoicism, Buddhism, Aristotelianism.
  - Modules engage users AND each other in ongoing dialogue.
  - System is modular: "Does not have to be built all at once," enabling piecemeal development.
  - Functions like "a library of such books from across a multitude of diverse perspectives."
  - Technical feasibility demonstrated: GPT-3 trained on Daniel Dennett's works produced outputs that "Dennett experts often mistake for Dennett's own writing."

- **Anti-monoculture argument (key contribution):**
  - "No competent philosopher would say, 'Read this one book, and you'll know all about morality.'"
  - Scientific analogy: "Just as scientists each favor their own hypotheses while we count on the diverse scientific community to expose these biases, it will not do to abstract away from that diversity to create just one neutral moral machine."
  - Invokes Mill's "experiments in living" -- society must grant "space to explore particular hypotheses regarding the true, the beautiful, and the good."

- **Internal vs. external transcendence (via Nussbaum):**
  - Warns against "external transcendence" -- trying to escape the human condition entirely, which risks becoming "the offer not so much of a better life, but of a different life."
  - Advocates "internal transcendence" -- moral improvement within our existing human condition, working "with our original starting place as concrete human agents."

- **The mentoring model:** "An excellent mentor does not dictate what one shall do. To benefit from such a relationship, one must exercise one's own judgment."

## Methodology

Philosophical analysis combining normative ethics with philosophy of technology. The paper: (1) taxonomizes existing AI moral enhancement proposals into exhaustive and auxiliary categories, (2) develops internal critiques of both categories drawing on moral epistemology and empirical moral psychology (Haidt), (3) proposes an alternative grounded in the socio-technical systems perspective, (4) draws on history of philosophy (wisdom traditions) and philosophy of science (pluralism, Millian experimentation) to justify the modular approach, and (5) addresses feasibility through reference to existing large language model capabilities.

## Relevance to This Work

**Directly informs our paper's approach to cognitive enhancement modalities and diversity.**

1. **Anti-monoculture argument strengthens our pluralism case:** Our paper argues for a *portfolio* of enhancement approaches precisely to avoid monoculture risks. Volkman and Gabriels provide a detailed philosophical argument for why relying on a single AI system (or a single enhancement modality) for moral/cognitive improvement is both epistemically and ethically dangerous. Their scientific community analogy -- diverse perspectives exposing individual biases -- applies directly to AI governance institutions.

2. **Socio-technical system framing aligns with our institutional design argument:** They argue moral improvement emerges from "the concrete mechanisms of moral improvement known to date -- namely, our current socio-technical system of moral enhancement comprised of people connected by information technology engaged in moral discourse." Our paper can draw on this to argue that cognitive enhancement for governance should upgrade existing democratic deliberation systems rather than replace them.

3. **Internal transcendence supports our "enhancement not replacement" thesis:** Their Nussbaum-derived distinction between internal and external transcendence maps onto our argument that cognitive enhancement should make humans *better at being human governors*, not transform them into something post-human or replace them with AI.

4. **Practical AI implementation model:** The modular system of wisdom-tradition-based AI interlocutors is a concrete example of how AI can enhance human moral and cognitive capacities without replacing human judgment -- exactly the kind of enhancement tool our paper advocates.

5. **Contrast with Lara 2021:** Where Lara proposes a single neutral Socratic assistant, Volkman and Gabriels argue neutrality is impossible and undesirable. Their pluralist alternative is more realistic for governance contexts where diverse stakeholder perspectives must be engaged.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- How would the modular system handle conflicts *between* wisdom traditions on urgent governance questions where a decision must be made? The paper acknowledges moral progress is "still open-ended" but governance requires timely action.
- The technical feasibility claim (GPT-3 on Dennett) was striking in 2023; with current LLMs this is much more achievable. How would we prevent these AI interlocutors from becoming echo chambers reinforcing users' pre-existing tradition preferences?
- Could this modular approach be applied to AI safety governance specifically -- e.g., AI interlocutors trained in different risk frameworks (precautionary principle, cost-benefit analysis, capability-based approaches)?
- The "user as editor" metaphor is powerful but may understate the difficulty of genuine moral growth through dialogue. How do we ensure engagement is substantive rather than performative?
