## Summary

Comprehensive review bridging psychedelic and meditation research through their shared effects on self-consciousness. Argues that self-consciousness is multidimensional — narrative (self-referential thought, mind-wandering, autobiographical memory) and multisensory (body ownership, bodily awareness, spatial self-location) — and that both meditation and psychedelics disrupt these dimensions in overlapping but distinct ways. Both involve decreased DMN activity. Proposes a multidimensional model of "self-loss" (Figure 1) and examines long-term outcomes including prosociality.

## Key Findings

- Self-consciousness is not unitary — it has narrative aspects (self-referential thought, mental time travel) and multisensory aspects (body ownership, bodily awareness, self-location)
- Both meditation and psychedelics decrease DMN activity, but via different mechanisms: meditation through attentional control, psychedelics through pharmacological disruption of 5-HT2A receptors
- Four meditation styles have dissociable neural signatures (Table 1, meta-analysis of 78 neuroimaging studies; Fox et al., 2016): FA (premotor cortex, dorsal ACC), MR (Broca's area, premotor cortex), OM (insula, IFG), LK (IPL, anterior insula)
- Drug-induced ego dissolution (DIED) neural correlates include increased DMN-TPN functional connectivity and decreased alpha power in PCC (Table 3)
- Meditation and psychedelics can both reduce self-referential thought, mind-wandering, and self-related thoughts — relevant to the narrative dimension of self-consciousness
- Both can disrupt body ownership, bodily awareness, and spatial self-location — the multisensory dimension
- "Selfless" states from meditation and psychedelics differ phenomenologically: psychedelic states typically involve rich sensory phenomenology (except 5-MeO-DMT), while meditation-induced states tend toward "sparse" phenomenal content (Table 4)
- Long-term effects: meditation practitioners show reduced resting-state DMN activity and altered connectivity, structural changes in PCC gray matter
- Prosociality connection: meditation fosters empathy and compassion as antecedents of prosocial behavior

## Methodology

- Theoretical review and synthesis paper, not an empirical study
- Draws on phenomenological reports, questionnaire data, and neuroimaging studies from both meditation and psychedelic research
- Published in Frontiers in Psychology, 23 pages

## Relevance to This Work

**Critical bridge paper for our argument.** Shows that psychedelics and meditation operate on overlapping neural substrates (DMN) and produce related alterations in self-consciousness. This directly supports our thesis that meditation demonstrates the brain's capacity for neuroplastic change in cognitive states, and psychedelics may offer a faster pharmacological route to similar states.

Key for Section V (enhancement technologies): Establishes psychedelics as producing meditation-like brain changes pharmacologically. The DMN overlap between meditation and psychedelics validates the mechanistic connection — both modalities target the same brain networks involved in self-referential processing.

Key for the prosociality argument: The paper notes that meditation fosters empathy and compassion, and that psychedelics may do the same through ego dissolution. Dambrun and Ricard (2011) suggest meditation supports a shift from "self-centered to selfless functioning, characterized by 'a weak distinction between self and others, and self and the environment as a whole,' which in turn 'is closely related to characteristics such as altruism, kindness, respect, empathy, compassion and the search for harmony'" (Millière et al. 2018, p. 20, citing Dambrun and Ricard 2011, p. 140).

## Questions & Follow-ups

- The paper is cautious about equating short-term "self-loss" experiences with lasting trait changes — we need additional evidence for durable prosocial effects
- Follow up on Dambrun and Ricard (2011) for the self-centered → selfless functioning argument
- The individual differences section is relevant: not everyone responds the same way to either meditation or psychedelics

## Key Quote

> "While both meditation and psychedelics are suspected to disrupt self-consciousness and its underlying neural processes, this general hypothesis requires careful qualification." (p. 2)

> "There is indeed evidence that meditation fosters these aspects of prosociality... Importantly, it has been hypothesized that these effects rely on a change in the sense of self." (p. 20)
