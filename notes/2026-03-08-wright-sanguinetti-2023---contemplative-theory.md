## Summary

Coauthored with Shinzen Young. Translates Buddhist contemplative theory into scientific terms and proposes the "Thin Model" — a functionalist model of the mind that maps subjective meditative states to testable neuroscientific hypotheses. Demonstrates the model by converting descriptions of jhāna (ecstatic meditative states) into specific predictions about neural activity. Bridges the gap between meditator self-reports and measurable brain states.

## Key Findings

- Buddhist contemplative theory is consistent with objective descriptions of mental function — it's not mysticism, it's phenomenology
- The "Thin Model" provides a systematic way to translate contemplative concepts into scientific hypotheses
- Successfully demonstrated by mapping jhāna states to specific, testable neural predictions
- Addresses a major problem in the field: the gap between what meditators report subjectively and what neuroscience can measure
- The nested structure of the model allows explanations at different levels of complexity
- Collaboration with Shinzen Young gives it credibility across both contemplative and scientific domains
- 35 citations

## Methodology

- Theoretical/translational paper
- Systematic translation of Buddhist contemplative theory into functionalist scientific terms
- Demonstration via jhāna states → neuroscientific hypotheses
- Collaboration between neuroscientists and contemplative practitioners

## Relevance to This Work

**Gold for Section VI.** This paper establishes that the cognitive states meditation produces — equanimity, metacognition, reduced reactivity, jhāna — are real, measurable brain states, not just subjective experiences. If we can map them scientifically, we can potentially engineer shortcuts to them via enhancement technologies. The Thin Model is essentially a specification for what cognitive enhancement needs to produce. Our paper can argue: "Wright, Sanguinetti, and Young have mapped the target states. Sanguinetti's ultrasound work is building the delivery mechanism."
