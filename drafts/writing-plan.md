# Writing Plan — Section-by-Section Drafting Skeleton

**Target length:** ~10,000 words (8,000–12,000 range)
**Structural spine:** The distinction between agency-building and dependency-building should appear early (Section I), anchor the conceptual center (Section V), recur through the paths forward (VIII–X), drive the dark side analysis (XIV), and close the paper (XVII).

**Drafting rule:** Each section should do only one argumentative job. If a section is trying to do two or three things at once, split the jobs or cut the extras. The outline is strong enough that the main remaining danger is not bad ideas — it's dilution.

**Tone rule:** Stay precise, controlled, and slightly cooler than the underlying idea. The argument is bold; the prose should be calm. Sections most likely to tempt prophetic drift: IV, V, IX, XIV.

---

## I. Introduction — 600 words (3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** The problem. AI governance has a human-capacity bottleneck. Current discourse focuses on alignment, regulation, institutions — but underweights the cognitive, emotional, and moral capacities of the humans operating inside those systems. One sentence on historical precedent (nuclear parallel). One sentence on what makes AI distinct.

**Paragraph 2:** The proposal. Adult intervention, cognitive enhancement, and early education merit serious attention as ways of strengthening the agency, judgment, and resilience needed to govern advanced AI. Introduce the three-path framework briefly.

**Paragraph 3:** The paper's deepest claim. These approaches must be designed to expand human autonomy rather than deepen dependency. The key question is not enhancement vs no enhancement, but **agency-building vs dependency-building**. This distinction structures the entire paper. **Name the contribution explicitly:** This paper contributes a conceptual framework for treating human capacity-building as a neglected dimension of AI governance. Roadmap of what follows.

**Literature positioning (end of intro or early in Section II):** Use the assertive version:

> Existing scholarship has illuminated important parts of this terrain, but not the argument advanced here. Work on AI and human enhancement has framed AI as both a potential cognitive enhancer and a source of human downgrading (Nyholm 2024); work on AI moral enhancement has emphasized autonomy, pluralism, and the risk of moral deskilling (Lara; Volkman & Gabriels); work on AI oversight has highlighted the fallibility of human overseers (Laux); and work on enhan dark sideh 2024). What remains underdeveloped is a governance framework that treats human cognitive and moral capacity itself as a strategic bottleneck in the age of advanced AI. This paper contributes such a framework. Its central claim is that adult intervention, enhancement, and early education should be assessed not simply by whether they improve performance, but by whether they build agency rather than dependency.

**Key citations needed:** Nyholm 2024 (AI enhancement vs downgrading), Lara (AI moral enhancement), Volkman & Gabriels (plural AI interlocutors), Laux (human oversight fallibility), Gordon & Seth 2024 (BCI ethics), Yanovski 2026 (first-mover enhancement argument), Yadav 2025 (cognitive sustainability), plus one broader AI risk source (Bostrom or Russell).

## II. The Governance Gap — 800 words (3–4 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Human cognitive limits — short-termism, attentional fragility, emotional reactivity, weak metacognition, poor coordination. These are well-documented in decision science. Brief, not exhaustive.

**Paragraph 2:** The historical pattern. Technologies repeatedly outpace governance. Don't enumerate every technology — use the pattern itself. Nuclear weapons as the clearest parallel: catastrophic stakes, narrow margins, repeated near-failures, governance lagging. 2–3 specific examples (Petrov, Able Archer, Cuban Missile Crisis) in a sentence each, not paragraphs.

**Paragraph 3:** Why AI may intensify this. AI increases the *consequences* of ordinary human weakness. Generality, speed, opacity, scale. The bottleneck is not only technical but cognitive.

**Paragraph 4 (optional):** Transition — if the bottleneck is partly cognitive, then cognitive capacity becomes a governance variable.

**Discipline note:** Do NOT write a full history of technology mismanagement. The pattern is the point, not the catalogue.

**Section job:** Answer **why is this a real class of problem?** (The general pattern of human limits + governance lag.) Section III then answers the different question: why is it urgent *in this case*? Keep these distinct — they will want to blend together during drafting.

**Key citations needed:** Kahneman or Tversky (cognitive biases), nuclear near-miss source, Collingridge dilemma or pacing problem paper.

## III. The Timeline Problem — 500 words (2 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** AI timelines may be compressed. Use scenario framing ("under plausible short-timeline assumptions"), not as premise. Reference expert surveys (Grace et al.) but don't assert certainty. Governance institutions take decades; capability development moves faster.

**Paragraph 2:** Strategic implications. This mismatch strengthens the case for adult-focused interventions (education alone may be too slow). But short timelines also make developmental approaches more urgent, not less — a layered strategy is needed. Race dynamics reduce incentives for caution.

**Discipline note:** Keep this SHORT. It's a framing section, not a prediction section. Two paragraphs max.

**Section job:** Answer **why is it urgent in this case?** (The strategic consequence of compressed timelines.) Section II established the general pattern; this section explains why AI makes it acute and why adult intervention matters now.

**Key citations needed:** Grace et al. survey, one governance-lag paper.

## IV. AI Dependence, Cognitive Offloading, and Civilizational Atrophy — 700 words (3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Digital technology precedent. Smartphones/social media already demonstrated how systems optimized for engagement degrade attention, self-regulation, social cohesion. Cite one major review (Haidt/Twenge or Odgers & Jensen). Don't over-detail — the precedent is established, move on.

**Paragraph 2:** How advanced AI deepens this. Frictionless intellectual, emotional, and social outsourcing. Reduced motivation to learn or create. Dependence on machine judgment. Replacement of difficult human relationships with optimized artificial ones. The "best-case dystopia" — not annihilation, but civilizational atrophy. **Define the term early in this paragraph:** civilizational atrophy = a gradual erosion of the cognitive, motivational, and relational capacities needed for meaningful self-government. Pin it down so it doesn't sound vague.

**Paragraph 3:** The key distinction (first appearance of the spine). The issue is not whether AI assists us, but whether it gradually weakens the capacities needed to remain agents rather than dependents. This sets up Section V.

**Discipline note:** Risk of sprawl here. Stay at the level of dynamics, not anecdotes. Three paragraphs, not five.

**Key citations needed:** Smartphone/attention review, cognitive offloading paper, AI companions study (if available).

## V. Good Enhancement, Bad Enhancement, and the Problem of False Expansion — 800 words (3–4 paragraphs)

**THIS IS A KEY CONTRIBUTION SECTION. Give it room.**

**Paragraph 1:** The central distinction. Capacity-building enhancement (improves durable judgment, self-regulation, metacognition, agency) vs pseudo-enhancement (feels empowering but increases pleasure, compliance, productivity, or dependency while narrowing autonomy).

**Paragraph 2:** The "chemical opium" risk. AI-accelerated drug discovery may produce not only genuine enhancement tools but also sophisticated forms of sedation disguised as empowerment. The same logic applies to AI-mediated digital systems optimized for engagement and behavioral compliance. The market will produce both — the question is whether societies can tell the difference.

**Paragraph 3:** Evaluative criteria. Reviewers will ask: how do we tell the difference? Offer three tests:
1. **Net effect on autonomous functioning over time** — does it expand self-direction or merely substitute?
2. **Effect on metacognition and self-regulation** — does it increase reflective control, or mainly reduce discomfort / increase compliance?
3. **Effect on underlying competence and resilience** — does it scaffold growth, or create fragility and erosion?
These do not penalize ongoing external support — they ask whether support expands or erodes functioning. Not a formal framework, but enough analytic teeth to work with.

**Paragraph 4:** Why this matters for the paper. Not all enhancement is stewardship enhancement. A system that makes people faster or happier is not necessarily making them wiser or more capable of governing powerful technologies. This distinction structures the rest of the paper: Sections VIII–X evaluate interventions partly through this lens, and Section XIV confronts what happens when the line blurs.

**Discipline note:** This is the paper's most original idea. Write it clearly and precisely. Don't hedge it into mush.

**Key citations needed:** AI drug discovery paper (Serrano 2024), enhancement vs therapy distinction paper, pseudo-enhancement/dependency paper.

## VI. What Counts as Human Capacity-Building? — 500 words (2 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Five categories. Pharmacological, neurotechnological, therapeutic/behavioral, contemplative/reflective, developmental/educational. One sentence defining each. This is a taxonomy, not an argument — keep it clean.

**Paragraph 2:** Key distinctions. Therapy vs enhancement. Restoration vs augmentation. Adult vs early development. Standalone vs combined interventions. Self-directed vs externally imposed. These distinctions matter for the ethical analysis later (XIV).

**Discipline note:** Definitional section. Do NOT turn this into a literature review. Two paragraphs.

**Key citations needed:** Taxonomy paper, Greely 2008 (responsible use).

## VII. Which Capacities Matter Most for AI Stewardship? — 600 words (2–3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Present the eight capacities as a **table**, not prose. This saves ~200 words and makes the paper feel more systematic.

| Capacity | Why it matters for AI governance | Example intervention paths |
|----------|----------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Long-term thinking | Resist race incentives, plan beyond immediate advantage | Contemplative training, psychedelic-assisted therapy |
| Metacognition | Notice bias, dependency, susceptibility to manipulation | Mindfulness, neurofeedback, reflective education |
| Attention / endurance | Stay engaged with complex systems rather than outsourcing | Attentional training, stimulants, executive-function development |
| Emotional regulation | Sound judgment under uncertainty, conflict, existential stress | Psychotherapy, meditation, neurostimulation |
| Empathy / social cognition | Cooperation, legitimacy, human solidarity | Compassion practice, SEL, psychedelic-assisted therapy |
| Epistemic humility | Navigate domains too complex for any individual | Reflective pedagogy, metacognitive training |
| Civic reasoning | Connect technical choices to long-term public consequences | Civic education, reflective pedagogy |
| Resistance to dependency | Preserve agency in environments optimized for passivity | Metacognitive training, critical media literacy |

**Paragraph 2:** The normative core. The goal is not maximal IQ but wiser, more reflective, more cooperative cognition. Raw processing speed without judgment, empathy, or restraint could make things worse, not better. These capacities map onto the three intervention paths that follow.

**Discipline note:** The table IS the section. One paragraph of normative framing after it. Don't elaborate each capacity in prose.

**Key citations needed:** Human factors in AI governance paper (if found), decision science capacity framework.

## VIII. Paths Forward I: Assisted Therapeutic Intervention — 800 words (3–4 paragraphs)

**Boundary with IX:** Section VIII covers interventions already legible as **therapeutic or therapeutic-adjacent** — combined models where pharmacology/neurotechnology supports psychotherapy, coaching, or structured behavior change. Section IX covers genuinely **augmentative or forward-looking** capacity expansion beyond therapy. If a claim feels like it belongs in IX, move it there. Don't let IX swallow VIII.

**Paragraph 1:** The core idea. Not enhancement in isolation, but assisted adult intervention. Tools work best when combined with psychotherapy, coaching, contemplative training, structured behavior change. Pharmacology and neurotechnology as catalysts for learning, not standalone performance boosters.

**Paragraph 2:** Psychedelic-assisted therapy as paradigm case. Chemistry + guided support + structured integration. This is conceptually important: it models intervention as a process, not a pill. Cite NIH/NCCIH framing. Brief mention of evidence status (psilocybin promising but no FDA approval, MDMA rejected August 2024).

**Paragraph 3:** Brain stimulation + therapy and broader framework. rTMS FDA-cleared for several indications. Research combining TMS + psychotherapy. Cite the meta-analysis (17 studies, 660 participants). The broader adult-intervention framework: medication+therapy, stimulation+therapy, contemplative practice+coaching, post-intervention integration.

**Paragraph 4:** Restorative psychopharmacology. Severe depression, emotional dysregulation, and related conditions directly erode the capacities this paper cares about — agency, patience, trust, judgment. The field is shifting toward more targeted, faster-acting treatments (ketamine/esketamine as the arrived case). But symptom domains differ: depression is the strongest case; anger/aggression is mixed (CBT often outperforms medication overall); paranoia/psychosis is cautionary (side-effect tradeoffs, coercion risk). Frame as **precision adult intervention within assisted intervention**, not "the future of psychiatric drugs." The best outcomes come from combined models, not drugs in isolation.

**Paragraph 5:** Why this matters for AI governance. Even modest improvements in self-regulation, metacognition, and emotional stability may matter under compressed timelines. The value is slightly better judgment under extraordinary pressure, not radically superior minds.

**Discipline note:** Evidence tiering is key here. Be precise about what's established, promising, and speculative. Don't oversell.

**Key citations needed:** Mitchell 2021 (MDMA Phase 3), NIBS+psychotherapy meta-analysis, rTMS clinical review, Sanguinetti 2020 (TUS), rapid-acting antidepressant review (ketamine/esketamine), aggression treatment meta-analysis (2025).

## IX. Paths Forward II: Adult Enhancement Beyond Therapy — 700 words (3 paragraphs)

**Boundary with VIII:** This section covers genuinely **augmentative or forward-looking** capacity expansion — interventions that aim to go beyond restoration or therapeutic support toward expanding what healthy cognition can do. Keep the boundary sharp: if it's therapy-adjacent, it belongs in VIII.

**Paragraph 1:** Some interventions aim to expand capacity, not just restore function. Attention enhancement, memory support, metacognitive enhancement, future BCI-mediated augmentation. This is the more speculative tier.

**Paragraph 2:** AI as both threat and accelerant. AI is accelerating drug discovery, neuroscience analysis, biomarker identification, trial design. The strategic question is not only what works now but what trajectories are opening. Current evidence is seeds — AI will grow them. The paradox: AI may erode human capacity AND accelerate tools to strengthen it.

**Paragraph 3:** BCI as case study. Not a deep dive into Neuralink specs — use BCI to illustrate how fast neurotechnology moves from theory to application. Current systems are limited and domain-specific. But the governance question is already live: will BCIs be developed toward agency and understanding, or toward entertainment, productivity, and control?

**Discipline note:** This is the most speculative section. Handle it with discipline. Scenario framing, not prediction. **Internal rule: every speculative claim in IX must be tied either to an existing trajectory or to a governance implication that arises now.** Less "this will happen," more "if this trajectory continues, governance questions arise now."

**Key citations needed:** Cinel 2019 (neurotechnology overview), Serrano 2024 (AI drug discovery), BCI review, Neuralink PRIME study (if found).

## X. Paths Forward III: Early Educational Intervention — 700 words (3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** The developmental case. Many stewardship capacities are shaped early. Education may be the least coercive, most scalable, and most publicly legitimate form of enhancement. Children can be taught metacognition, attention, emotional regulation, and civic reasoning.

**Paragraph 2:** The evidence base. SEL (cite 2023 meta-analysis: 424 studies, 53 countries, 575K students). Executive function development (cite Harvard Center on the Developing Child). Annaka Harris as concrete entry point for early mindfulness. Immordino-Yang on civic reasoning and transcendent thinking as bridge to the paper's core claim.

**Paragraph 3:** Why education matters even under short timelines. It's slower than adult intervention but more durable, more equitable, and less vulnerable to coercive misuse. A serious strategy needs both near-term adult tools and long-term developmental reform.

**Discipline note:** Don't write a full education review. Three paragraphs: the case, the evidence, the strategic logic.

**Key citations needed:** 2023 SEL meta-analysis, Harvard executive function, Immordino-Yang, children's mindfulness meta-analysis.

## XI. Contemplative Practice as Proof of Concept — 500 words (2 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Contemplative practice provides evidence that attentional stability, emotional regulation, metacognitive awareness, and empathy can be cultivated. These are not fixed traits. Cite the Sanguinetti/SEMA lab work: TUS + contemplative training, mood enhancement, functional connectivity changes. The challenge: traditional cultivation takes thousands of hours.

**Paragraph 2:** The role in the paper. Contemplative traditions reveal the targets; therapeutic and technological scaffolds may broaden access. This is not "replace meditation with pills" — it's "meditation shows what's possible; can we make it more accessible?" Be careful not to overclaim.

**Discipline note:** Two paragraphs. Proof of concept, not a meditation review.

**Flexibility note:** If the draft runs long, this is the first section to consider compressing. It could be partially absorbed into VIII (as proof-of-concept for adult scaffolded capacity-building) or X (as a bridge between adult and developmental cultivation). Keep it standalone for now, but treat it as flexible.

**Key citations needed:** Sanguinetti 2020, Lord 2024, Wright & Sanguinetti 2023, Fox et al. 2014 (meditation neuroplasticity, if found).

## XII. Capacity-Building Within the Broader AI Governance Portfolio — 400 words (2 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** What capacity-building does not replace: technical alignment, compute governance, regulation, international coordination, public-interest oversight. List these, don't elaborate.

**Paragraph 2:** What it adds. Technical systems still require human judgment. Institutions depend on the minds operating within them. Capacity-building is a background condition for governance quality, not a substitute for governance structure. This reframes enhancement as civilizational infrastructure rather than transhumanist ambition.

**Discipline note:** SHORTEST section in the paper. Two tight paragraphs. The point is positioning, not argument.

**Key citations needed:** AI governance framework paper (if found).

## XIII. Roadblocks — 600 words (2–3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Why this agenda hasn't already been pursued. Regulatory lag (approval pipelines are slow, neurotechnology frameworks incomplete). Stigma (enhancement associated with cheating, transhumanism, recklessness). Safety and uncertainty (long-term effects unclear). Present as a list, not a sermon.

**Paragraph 2:** Structural obstacles. Market incentives favor pleasure, compliance, and productivity over wisdom and stewardship (connects back to Section V). Political fragmentation (left and right resist for different reasons). Coordination failure (equitable deployment is hard; the capacities needed to coordinate are the very ones currently lacking — the chicken-and-egg).

**Paragraph 3 (optional):** One sentence only: these are reasons to proceed carefully, not reasons to stop. Don't elaborate — the point lands harder if it's brief.

**Discipline note:** Keep this tight and analytic. Don't re-argue the whole paper.

**Key citations needed:** Enhancement stigma survey, regulatory lag paper, MDMA FDA rejection as concrete example.

## XIV. The Dark Side — 800 words (3–4 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Mental privacy and cognitive surveillance. Neural data is uniquely intimate. The move from behavioral surveillance to cognitive surveillance marks a major moral threshold. Who owns neural data? Briefly mention Chile's neurorights. Then: from influence to override — the same tools that regulate mood therapeutically can be used coercively.

**Paragraph 2:** Soft coercion and misaligned optimization. Enhancement could become practically mandatory without formal mandates (workplace, military, educational pressure). Markets may optimize for productivity, compliance, and performance rather than wisdom. The intelligence-without-wisdom problem: competitive systems may produce smarter but less trustworthy actors.

**Paragraph 3:** Cognitive monoculture and the arms race. Standardizing ideal cognition could reduce diversity and adaptive resilience. If enhancement becomes competitive, pressure to escalate may outstrip caution — reproducing the very race dynamics the paper criticizes in AI development.

**Paragraph 4:** Therapeutic dependence and paternalism. Combined interventions may blur the line between helping people govern themselves and training them into compliance. This is especially dangerous if justified in the language of public safety or AI governance necessity. These dangers do not invalidate the project; they set the terms for how cautiously it must proceed.

**Discipline note:** This section earns credibility. Don't soft-pedal it. But also don't turn it into a separate paper on neuroethics. Four paragraphs covering the major failure modes.

**Key citations needed:** Neural data privacy/neurorights paper, coercive enhancement paper, cognitive monoculture/neurodiversity paper, dual-use brain stimulation paper.

## XV. Research and Policy Agenda — 600 words (2–3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Research priorities. Which capacities matter most for AI stewardship? Standalone vs combined interventions. Durability of gains. Narrow performance vs deeper judgment gains. Clinical/therapeutic priorities: study combined models, develop measures for metacognition and manipulation resistance, distinguish symptom relief from capacity-building.

**Paragraph 2:** Educational priorities. Children's mindfulness, executive function, SEL, civic reasoning. Distinguish developmental support from ideological programming.

**Paragraph 3:** Governance safeguards and justice. Cognitive liberty. Informed consent. No coercive adoption. Neural privacy protections. Independent oversight. Equitable access — treat capacity-building as a public-interest issue, not a luxury market.

**Discipline note:** Be concrete and actionable. This is where the paper becomes practical.

**Key citations needed:** May not need many — this is the paper's own proposal.

---

## XVI. Discussion — 500 words (2 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Limitations and honest uncertainty. Evidence is early, mixed, indirect. The paper does not claim any single intervention solves AI risk. The chicken-and-egg problem: better judgment may be needed to develop better tools for judgment. Some interventions may fail or produce tradeoffs.

**Paragraph 2:** What the paper does claim. The human-capacity bottleneck is real and underexamined. The three most important distinctions: (1) agency-building vs dependency-building (conceptual), (2) short-term adult intervention vs long-term developmental reform (strategic), (3) support for flourishing vs optimization for control (ethical). These frame a research agenda worth pursuing even if many specific tools prove ineffective.

**Discipline note:** Two paragraphs. Honest about limits, clear about contribution.

---

## XVII. Conclusion — 400 words (2 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1:** Advanced AI creates a problem of human adequacy, not only machine capability. Humanity's future with AI may depend partly on whether it can strengthen the minds responsible for guiding it — through adult therapeutic intervention, cognitive enhancement, and early educational development.

**Paragraph 2:** Return to the spine. The prudent response is neither techno-utopianism nor blanket refusal. It is serious work on which forms of capacity-building genuinely support agency, wisdom, cooperation, and democratic self-governance — and which merely deepen the dependency they claim to cure.

**Discipline note:** End on the agency-vs-dependency distinction. That's the paper's signature idea.
