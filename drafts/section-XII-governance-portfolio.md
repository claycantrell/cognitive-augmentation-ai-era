## Argument

This section positions human capacity-building as complementary to — not competing with — the existing AI governance portfolio. It argues that alignment, regulation, and coordination all have human-capacity prerequisites that are rarely examined and never guaranteed. Capacity-building is a background condition for governance quality, not a substitute for governance structure.

## Evidence Bridge

1. Each major governance strategy (alignment, regulation, coordination) addresses a real dimension of AI risk that capacity-building cannot replace.
2. But each also depends on cognitive, emotional, and moral capacities in the humans operating inside the system.
3. Those capacities are the ones this paper has identified as vulnerable to erosion (Section IV) and potentially trainable (Sections VIII–XI).
4. Therefore capacity-building is not a competing proposal but a background condition for the effectiveness of existing proposals.

## Draft (~400 words, 2 paragraphs)

Human capacity-building is not a substitute for technical alignment, model evaluation, compute governance, law and regulation, international coordination, or public-interest oversight. Each of these strategies addresses a real and irreducible dimension of AI risk. A technically misaligned system remains dangerous regardless of the wisdom of its overseers, and poorly designed institutions can fail regardless of the quality of the individuals within them. This paper does not argue that human capacity-building solves these problems. It argues that it addresses a different one — a neglected prerequisite that the existing governance portfolio often assumes rather than examines.

That prerequisite is the quality of human judgment operating inside governance systems. Alignment research assumes that humans can specify goals, evaluate outcomes, and correct course when systems behave unexpectedly. Regulation assumes that lawmakers and regulators can understand the systems they govern well enough to write effective rules. International coordination assumes that negotiators can manage uncertainty, resist short-term incentives, and sustain cooperation under pressure. In each case, governance quality depends partly on the quality of the judgment capacities brought to bear. Capacity-building does not compete with these strategies; it is a background condition for their effectiveness. The point is not to displace the existing governance portfolio, but to identify a neglected prerequisite shared across it.
