## Summary

Bostrom and Sandberg provide a comprehensive taxonomy of cognitive enhancement methods spanning education, pharmacology, brain stimulation, and genetic interventions, alongside a systematic analysis of the ethical issues each raises. They argue that cognitive enhancement is not new -- education and caffeine are longstanding examples -- but that emerging technologies amplify familiar ethical concerns about authenticity, fairness, coercion, and the proper role of medicine. The paper frames regulatory challenges as requiring nuanced governance that avoids both blanket prohibition and uncritical permissiveness.

## Key Findings

- Cognitive enhancement methods exist on a continuum from conventional (education, nutrition, sleep optimization, caffeine) to emerging (pharmacological nootropics, brain stimulation, genetic selection/modification)
- The inverted-U relationship between dopamine and cognitive performance means enhancement drugs may help some individuals while impairing others, depending on baseline neurochemistry
- Intelligence correlates with many positive life outcomes (health, longevity, income) but not directly with happiness
- The distinction between "treatment" and "enhancement" is conceptually unstable but practically important for regulation
- Cognitive liberty -- the right to modify one's own mental states -- can be argued as a negative right
- Social organization improvements (institutions, information technology, collaborative tools) represent a form of cognitive enhancement often overlooked in debates focused on biomedical interventions
- The Flynn Effect (rising IQ scores over decades) demonstrates that environmental and educational factors already produce population-level cognitive enhancement

## Methodology

Narrative review and philosophical analysis. Surveys the empirical literature on enhancement methods across multiple domains (nutrition, pharmacology, neurostimulation, genetics, software/organizational tools) and maps the ethical arguments for and against each. Not a systematic review but a comprehensive interdisciplinary synthesis.

## Relevance to This Work

**Critical for Section XIV (coercive enhancement).** Bostrom and Sandberg's analysis of the coercion concern is directly relevant: if cognitive enhancement becomes available, competitive pressures could effectively force adoption even without mandates. Their taxonomy of enhancement methods provides the structural framework our manuscript builds on when arguing that human capacity-building should be a governance strategy. Their observation that cognitive liberty can be framed as a negative right connects to our argument that individuals should have the right to enhance (or refuse enhancement) as AI reshapes cognitive demands. Their point about social/organizational enhancements being overlooked parallels our argument that governance frameworks fixate on AI regulation while ignoring the human side.

## Key Quote

"The relationship between dopamine activity and cognitive performance is believed to follow an inverted U-shaped curve, in which intermediate dopamine levels are optimal for cognitive performance, whereas low and high levels are detrimental." (Paraphrased from the review's discussion of pharmacological enhancement, drawing on Robbins & Arnsten 2009 -- this framing appears across the enhancement literature and is foundational to understanding why "smart drugs" show inconsistent results.)
