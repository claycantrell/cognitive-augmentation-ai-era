## Summary

A major interdisciplinary collaboration among 20 scholars from philosophy, biomedical ethics, law, neuroscience, and medicine, this paper provides conceptual, ethical, and legal foundations for three "neurorights" families: mental integrity, mental privacy, and cognitive liberty. The authors do not advocate for any single position on whether novel neurorights are needed. Instead, they map the existing human rights landscape (UDHR, ICCPR, ECHR, ACHR, CFR) to show where protection of the mind already exists and where gaps may emerge as neurotechnologies advance. The paper was motivated by active policy initiatives at UNESCO, the Council of Europe, and national legislatures (notably Chile) that are considering whether existing rights adequately protect minds and brains from neurotechnological misuse.

Note: The user-provided filename references "Bublitz 2023" but Bublitz is one of 20 co-authors. The paper is led by Sjors Ligthart with corresponding author Philipp Kellmeyer.

## Key Findings

- **Three neurorights families identified:** Mental integrity (protection from interference with one's mind), mental privacy (protection from access to one's mind), and cognitive liberty (protection of mental self-determination, including both negative freedom from coercion and positive freedom to control one's own brain/mind).
- **Three positions on neurorights:** (1) Novel brain-specific rights are necessary because existing rights are insufficient; (2) adaptive reinterpretation of existing rights is sufficient; (3) no reforms needed at all. The authors do not take a position but provide foundations for all three.
- **Mental integrity as analog to bodily integrity:** Non-invasive brain stimulation (tDCS, TMS) could severely interfere with mental states without significant physical changes to the brain, making mental integrity conceptually distinct from bodily integrity.
- **Mental privacy is unique:** Brain-derived data provide access to information a person may never have externally manifested. This makes mental privacy qualitatively different from other forms of informational privacy.
- **Cognitive liberty defined** as "mental self-determination" (Bublitz) or "the right to self-determination over our brains and mental experiences" (Farahany). It encompasses both the freedom to use neurotechnologies and protection from their coercive use.
- **Existing legal protections partially cover neurorights:** The right to mental integrity is explicit in Article 3(1) CFR and Article 5(1) ACHR; mental privacy receives implicit protection under privacy rights, freedom of thought, and freedom of expression; cognitive liberty has no explicit basis in established human rights law but may be derivable from freedom of thought.
- **Neurotechnology capabilities driving rights concerns:** (1) accessing mental states, (2) verifying subjective reports, (3) contesting first-person authority, and (4) controlling decoded mental states through brain stimulation.

## Methodology

Interdisciplinary conceptual analysis combining perspectives from philosophy, biomedical ethics, legal philosophy, and law. The paper maps ethical concepts (mental integrity, mental privacy, cognitive liberty) onto existing human rights instruments (UDHR, ICCPR, ACHR, ECHR, CFR, CRPD, GDPR) through systematic legal analysis. Three-part structure: (1) overview of neurotechnologies and their applications; (2) conceptual and ethical analysis of the three neurorights; (3) legal foundations mapping.

## Relevance to This Work

**Central to the legal/rights framework in our paper's governance argument.** Key uses:

1. **Cognitive liberty as foundation for enhancement rights:** The paper's definition of cognitive liberty includes the positive right to "alter one's mental states with the help of neurotools," which directly supports our argument that cognitive enhancement should be a protected activity under human rights law. If individuals have a right to mental self-determination, then restricting access to safe cognitive enhancement technologies could infringe that right.
2. **The "freedom to enhance" side of neurorights:** While the paper frames neurorights primarily as protections against misuse, the cognitive liberty framework inherently supports the right to self-enhance. Our paper can argue that the same rights framework that protects against coercive neurotechnology use also protects the freedom to voluntarily enhance.
3. **AI governance connection:** The paper's discussion of neurotechnology capabilities (accessing, verifying, contesting, controlling mental states) parallels the information asymmetry problem in AI governance. Just as neurorights protect against technological manipulation of minds, cognitive enhancement could serve as a defense against AI-driven manipulation.
4. **Policy momentum:** The paper documents active engagement by UNESCO, Council of Europe, OECD, and Chile's legislature -- showing that neurorights are not merely academic but moving into law. This bolsters our paper's argument that governance infrastructure for cognitive enhancement is emerging.
5. **Treatment vs. enhancement distinction is unstable:** The paper notes existing rights frameworks distinguish treatment from enhancement, but this distinction is "not based on biological facts, but rather reflecting subjective valuation." This supports our argument against rigid treatment/enhancement gatekeeping.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- How has Chile's constitutional neurorights reform progressed since 2023? Could serve as a case study in our paper.
- The paper does not address whether cognitive liberty includes a right to pharmacological self-enhancement (as opposed to neurotechnological) -- our paper could extend this framework.
- The "contested first-person authority" capability (where neurotechnology overrides self-reports) raises profound questions about AI systems that might similarly override human judgment.
- How do the three positions on neurorights map onto the political spectrum? Understanding this could help frame our governance proposals.
- The freedom of non-expression (right not to transmit brain data) could be analogized to a right not to have one's cognitive limitations exploited by AI systems.

## Key Quote

> "Cognitive liberty has been defined as a person's autonomous, unhindered control or mastery over their mind... This right comprises, among others, two fundamental and intimately related principles: (1) the right of individuals to freely use emerging neurotechnologies and (2) the protection of individuals from coercive or unconsented use of such technologies."
