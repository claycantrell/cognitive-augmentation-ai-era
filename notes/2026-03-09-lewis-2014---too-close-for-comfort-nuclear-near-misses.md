## Summary

Lewis, Williams, Pelopidas, and Aghlani document at least 13 instances between 1962 and 2002 when nuclear weapons were nearly used due to miscalculation, miscommunication, or technical errors. Published as a Chatham House (Royal Institute of International Affairs) report, the analysis reveals a disturbing pattern: in multiple cases, the only thing preventing nuclear use was the independent judgment of individual officers or officials who chose to disregard protocols or delay action. The report argues that with approximately 1,800 warheads maintained on high alert by Russia and the US (requiring only 5-15 minutes for launch authorization), the risks of accidental or inadvertent nuclear use remain unacceptably high. The authors propose policy changes to expand decision-making time, reduce alert status, and broaden participation in nuclear command-and-control decisions.

## Key Findings

- **13+ documented near-miss incidents** between 1962-2002, caused by technical faults, false alarms, miscommunication, and political miscalculation
- **Individual human judgment was the decisive factor** in preventing nuclear use in multiple cases -- often by officers defying protocol or delaying action
- Notable cases include:
  - **1962 Cuban Missile Crisis**: Soviet submarine captain nearly launched nuclear warheads after depth charge strikes; a second officer intervened to prevent launch
  - **1979 Computer Error**: US National Security Advisor Brzezinski was told 220 Soviet missiles were inbound; he delayed calling the President, and within a minute learned a faulty computer chip caused the alert
  - **1983 Soviet Early Warning**: Sunlight reflection on clouds was misidentified as incoming missiles by Soviet satellite systems; Lt. Col. Stanislav Petrov judged it a false alarm and did not report it up the chain
  - **1983 NATO Able Archer Exercise**: A realistic nuclear war simulation put Soviets on genuine alert during a period of extreme US-Soviet tension, despite warnings from the US National Security Advisor
  - **1995 Black Brant Incident**: A Norwegian research rocket triggered Russian nuclear missile-readiness procedures; President Yeltsin had minutes to decide on retaliation before the rocket's trajectory was correctly identified
  - **1999 Kargil Crisis**: India-Pakistan conventional conflict threatened nuclear escalation until US mediation intervened
- **Leadership vulnerability**: Report notes concerns about decision-makers under personal impairment (Nixon and Yeltsin's drinking, Mitterrand leaving nuclear codes in dry cleaning, Reagan's codes missing during his shooting)
- **Time pressure is the core structural problem**: Current alert systems compress decision windows to minutes, leaving no room for deliberation, verification, or consultation
- **Policy recommendations**:
  - Remove weapons from "hair trigger" alert status to allow more decision-making time
  - Retarget nuclear weapons to ocean areas
  - Refrain from large-scale military exercises during periods of heightened tension
  - Expand the circle of people involved in nuclear decision-making
  - Implement confidence-building measures between nuclear states
  - Increase transparency regarding launch policies
  - Educate military personnel on humanitarian impacts of nuclear use

## Methodology

Historical case study analysis. Draws on declassified documents, published accounts, and expert analysis to reconstruct 13+ near-miss incidents. Combines historical narrative with policy analysis. Not a quantitative study but a systematic documentation of incidents that were previously scattered across different sources and time periods.

## Relevance to This Work

**Directly relevant to Section II (governance gap / high-stakes human judgment failures).** This report is the primary evidence base for our argument that human cognitive failures in high-stakes governance contexts are not hypothetical but historically documented. The pattern Lewis et al. identify -- that individual human judgment, often by mid-level officers acting against protocol, was the last line of defense against catastrophe -- perfectly illustrates our thesis: governance systems that depend on human cognitive capacity must invest in that capacity. The time-pressure problem (5-15 minute decision windows) is a concrete example of the cognitive demands that exceed normal human capacity and that our manuscript argues should be addressed through cognitive enhancement. The leadership vulnerability findings (impaired decision-makers) support our argument that cognitive fitness of decision-makers is a governance variable, not a personal matter. As AI systems are integrated into nuclear command-and-control, the interaction between human cognitive limitations and AI decision-support becomes our manuscript's central concern.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- Have there been documented near-misses since 2002 that update this analysis? The report's coverage ends two decades ago
- How has AI integration into early warning systems changed the risk profile? Does AI decision-support help (more accurate detection) or hurt (faster escalation, less human deliberation time)?
- The report argues for expanding decision-making circles -- how does this interact with cognitive enhancement? Could enhanced decision-makers partially substitute for expanded circles?
- What is the current status of the policy recommendations? Have any been implemented since 2014?
- The Petrov and Brzezinski cases illustrate "wise restraint" -- a cognitive capacity (resisting protocol under pressure) that seems resistant to standard training. Is this a candidate for enhancement?

## Key Quote

The 1979 incident exemplifies the report's core finding: National Security Advisor Brzezinski received notification of 220 incoming Soviet missiles and had minutes to decide whether to inform the President for a retaliatory launch. His decision to delay -- to wait one more minute for confirmation -- proved decisive when the alert was revealed to be caused by a faulty computer chip. This single case illustrates the report's central argument: nuclear governance currently depends on the cognitive composure and independent judgment of individual humans operating under extreme time pressure and existential uncertainty.
