## Argument

This section confronts the possibility that the tools proposed in this paper could become instruments of domination rather than agency. It covers mental privacy and cognitive surveillance, soft coercion, market-driven misaligned optimization, cognitive monoculture, enhancement arms races, and therapeutic dependence/paternalism. These dangers do not invalidate the capacity-building project, but they set strict terms for how it must proceed.

## Evidence Bridge

1. Neural data is uniquely intimate; the transition from behavioral to cognitive surveillance marks a qualitative moral threshold.
2. Neurorights recognition is emerging (Chile constitutional amendment, Bublitz 2023) but recognition is not protection.
3. Soft coercion is more likely than overt override — enhancement may become practically mandatory through competitive pressure.
4. Market incentives favor pseudo-enhancement (connecting back to Section V) — productivity and compliance over wisdom and judgment.
5. Cognitive monoculture risks reducing the diversity and adaptive capacity that governance requires.
6. Enhancement arms races could reproduce at the human level the very race dynamics the paper criticizes in AI development.
7. Combined interventions may blur helping vs compliance — the evaluative criteria from Section V apply with particular force here.

## Draft (~800 words, 4 paragraphs)

Any serious argument for human capacity-building must confront the possibility that the tools themselves become instruments of domination. The same pharmacological, neurotechnological, and therapeutic interventions this paper has reviewed as potential supports for human agency could, under different institutional conditions, become mechanisms of control. The risk begins with mental privacy. Neural data is uniquely intimate: it may reveal patterns of attention, emotion, cognition, and vulnerability that no other dataset captures as directly. The transition from behavioral surveillance (tracking what people do) to cognitive surveillance (inferring aspects of what people think, feel, attend to, or are vulnerable to) would mark a qualitative moral threshold, not merely a quantitative extension of existing monitoring. Some jurisdictions have begun to recognize this: Chile has amended its constitution to protect neurorights, and scholarly work on the foundations of cognitive liberty has accelerated [@bublitz2023]. But recognition is not protection. As neurotechnological tools become more precise and more widely deployed, the infrastructure for cognitive monitoring may develop more quickly than the legal frameworks needed to constrain it. And the same tools that modulate mood, attention, or cognition therapeutically can in principle be used to override rather than support individual judgment — turning instruments of self-regulation into instruments of external control.

The more likely danger, however, is not overt override but soft coercion. Enhancement may become practically mandatory without formal mandates if refusal carries significant social or economic costs. An employer who offers cognitive enhancement tools as a workplace benefit need not require their use; the competitive disadvantage of declining may be sufficient. Military applications present similar dynamics: soldiers who refuse available cognitive enhancement may be deemed unfit for roles that enhanced peers can perform more reliably. Educational contexts could also drift in this direction if enhancement tools become associated with academic performance. Unequal access introduces a further risk: governance institutions may become stratified between those able to afford cognitive support and those expected to compete without it. In each case, the coercion is structural rather than explicit — and therefore harder to identify, regulate, or resist.

Market incentives compound this risk. As Section V argued, commercial pressures tend to favor interventions that maximize productivity, compliance, engagement, and measurable performance rather than the harder-to-quantify capacities this paper cares about — wisdom, reflective judgment, moral restraint, cooperative disposition. The result could be a form of optimization that produces smarter but less trustworthy actors: individuals with greater cognitive throughput but diminished empathy, weaker ethical reasoning, or reduced resistance to manipulation. This is the intelligence-without-wisdom problem, and it is one of the most important failure modes for the capacity-building agenda this paper proposes.

Two additional risks deserve attention. The first is cognitive monoculture. Governance under uncertainty often benefits from heterogeneity in perception, reasoning style, risk sensitivity, and social cognition. If capacity-building programs converge on a standardized profile of ideal cognition — optimized for a particular set of performance metrics, cultural values, or institutional needs — the result could be a reduction in cognitive diversity that weakens collective resilience and adaptive capacity. Forms of cognition that do not fit standard optimization profiles may contribute to the disagreement, perspective-shifting, and adaptive variation that complex governance problems require. The second risk is an enhancement arms race. If nations, firms, or individuals treat cognitive enhancement as a competitive domain, pressure to escalate may outstrip the caution that safety and ethics demand — reproducing at the level of human cognition the very race dynamics this paper has criticized in AI development itself. The parallel is uncomfortable but instructive: the same competitive logic that makes AI governance difficult could make enhancement governance difficult for structurally identical reasons.

These dangers do not refute the case for capacity-building, but they do define the conditions under which it can be pursued responsibly. Combined interventions — the very model this paper has endorsed as most promising — may become powerful enough to blur the distinction between helping people govern themselves and training them into compliance, especially if capacity-building is justified in the language of public safety or AI-governance necessity. The evaluative criteria proposed in Section V — net effect on autonomous functioning, effect on metacognition and self-regulation, effect on underlying competence and resilience — apply here with particular force. Any intervention that fails these tests is not capacity-building in the sense this paper intends, regardless of what it is called. The dark side of this agenda is real, and any paper that proposes it bears the burden of naming it clearly.
