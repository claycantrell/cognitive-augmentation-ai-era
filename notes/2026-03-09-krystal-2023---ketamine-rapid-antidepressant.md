## Summary

Comprehensive review synthesizing clinical and preclinical evidence on ketamine's rapid antidepressant mechanisms. The authors (Krystal, Kavalali, and Monteggia) integrate three converging mechanistic models: (1) the Duman laboratory's disinhibition hypothesis (NMDA blockade on interneurons releases glutamate, activating mTOR and dendritic spine formation), (2) Monteggia/Kavalali's homeostatic synaptic plasticity model (NMDA blockade triggers rapid AMPA receptor upscaling), and (3) BDNF-TrkB signaling via eEF2K inhibition. The review establishes that ketamine produces antidepressant effects within hours at 0.5 mg/kg IV, working through a novel form of rapid synaptic plasticity that restores synaptic deficits in depressed patients.

Note: The user's bibliography labels this "Duman 2023" but the paper is authored by Krystal, Kavalali & Monteggia. It discusses Duman's laboratory work extensively.

## Key Findings

- **Clinical need:** Only ~one-third of depressed patients remitted on initial antidepressant in the NIMH STAR*D study, establishing the urgency for novel treatments
- **Dosing:** Standard protocol is 0.5 mg/kg IV infusion (targeting plasma levels 130-200 ng/ml); reduced efficacy at lower doses (<=0.2 mg/kg), no additional benefit at doubled doses
- **Broad efficacy:** Ketamine shows effects across treatment-resistant depression, bipolar disorder, OCD, and PTSD, with onset within hours rather than weeks
- **Duman disinhibition hypothesis:** Ketamine blocks NMDA receptors on inhibitory interneurons, reducing GABAergic tone, disinhibiting glutamatergic neurons, activating mTOR, and promoting dendritic spine formation
- **Homeostatic synaptic plasticity (Monteggia/Kavalali):** Ketamine induces a "novel form of rapid homeostatic plasticity" distinct from Hebbian mechanisms -- NMDA receptor blockade triggers upscaling of AMPA receptor-mediated transmission within hours, outlasting ketamine's systemic presence
- **BDNF-TrkB pathway:** Ketamine blocks tonically active NMDA receptors, suppressing calcium influx and inhibiting eEF2K (eukaryotic elongation factor 2 kinase), permitting rapid dendritic BDNF synthesis, which activates postsynaptic TrkB receptors to insert AMPA receptors
- **MeCP2 sustained effects:** Initial translation-dependent rapid effects transition to sustained responses through MeCP2 transcriptional regulation; MeCP2 phosphorylation at Serine 421 is necessary for prolonged (but not immediate) antidepressant effects
- **Synaptic density evidence:** Imaging with SV2A radioligands reveals synaptic density reductions in moderate-to-severe MDD; pilot study found only depressed patients with baseline synaptic deficits showed increased SV2A binding post-ketamine, suggesting "restoration of synaptic efficacy"
- **Glutamate dysfunction in depression:** Proton MRS and 13C-MRS with isotopic tracers document reduced cortical metabolic activity and altered glutamate-glutamine cycling in depressed patients

## Methodology

Narrative review integrating clinical pharmacology, neuroimaging (fMRI, 1H-MRS, 13C-MRS, SV2A PET), molecular biology, and electrophysiology. Synthesizes decades of work from multiple laboratories, particularly the Duman lab (Yale), Monteggia/Kavalali lab (Vanderbilt), and Krystal's clinical group (Yale). Not a systematic review but a comprehensive synthesis of mechanistic models.

## Relevance to This Work

**Key evidence for Section VIII (restorative psychopharmacology).** This paper provides the strongest mechanistic case for ketamine as a tool that genuinely restores synaptic function rather than merely masking symptoms. The convergence of three independent mechanistic models -- all pointing to rapid synaptic plasticity and restoration of lost dendritic spines -- supports the manuscript's argument that pharmacological interventions can rebuild cognitive and emotional capacity at the neural level.

The "restoration of synaptic efficacy" framing is directly relevant to the paper's thesis on human capacity-building: ketamine does not just alter mood, it physically rebuilds neural connectivity that depression has eroded. This positions it as a cognitive augmentation tool, not just a treatment -- depression-damaged brains get their synaptic architecture restored, potentially enabling higher-order functions like decision-making, emotional regulation, and long-term planning that are critical for effective governance participation.

The mTOR/BDNF/spine formation cascade also connects to the broader neuroplasticity theme across the manuscript (psychedelics, meditation, education all converging on similar plasticity mechanisms).
