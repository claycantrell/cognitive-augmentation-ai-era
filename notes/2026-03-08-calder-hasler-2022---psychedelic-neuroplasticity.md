## Summary

Comprehensive review of the evidence that classic psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, DMT, DOI) promote neuroplasticity. Examines cellular and molecular mechanisms (dendritogenesis, synaptogenesis, neurogenesis, gene expression), brain regions affected (primarily neocortex/PFC, to lesser extent hippocampus), dose-response relationships, temporal dynamics of the plasticity window, and downstream consequences for cognition, emotion, and behavior. Published in Neuropsychopharmacology (272 citations).

## Key Findings

### Mechanisms
- Psychedelics enhance neuroplasticity via **5-HT2A receptor** agonism → post-synaptic stimulation of layer 5/6 pyramidal neurons → increased extracellular glutamate → AMPA receptor stimulation → BDNF secretion → TrkB and mTOR activation (positive feedback loop)
- Evidence for **dendritogenesis** (dendritic growth), **synaptogenesis** (new synapse formation), **spinogenesis** (dendritic spine formation), and some evidence for **neurogenesis** (new neurons, mainly via DMT/5-MeO-DMT acting on sigma-1 receptors)
- Term "**psychoplastogens**" used for substances that rapidly stimulate neuroplasticity

### Brain Regions
- **Neocortex, especially PFC:** Strongest neuroplastic effects — psychedelics upregulate plasticity-related genes, promote dendritic growth, increase presynaptic density in PFC
- **Hippocampus:** More modest effects — psilocybin upregulated fewer plasticity-related transcripts than in cortex; LSD failed to upregulate immediate early genes
- Pattern explained by higher density of **5-HT2A** (pro-neuroplastic) vs **5-HT1A** (inhibitory) receptors in cortex compared to hippocampus

### Temporal Dynamics (Figure 1 — key visualization)
- **Within 1-2 hours:** Upregulation of immediate early genes (IEGs) and neuroplasticity-related transcripts
- **1.5-6 hours:** BDNF mRNA upregulated; changes in gene expression peak
- **6+ hours:** Cellular morphology changes begin — dendritic growth, synaptogenesis, spinogenesis
- **24-72 hours:** Significant neuronal growth phase; increased synaptic and dendritic density observed
- **5+ days:** Dendritic spine formation rate returns to baseline in psilocybin-treated mice
- **1+ month:** New dendrites formed during growth period can survive; functional connectivity changes persist in humans
- Peripheral BDNF levels elevated 2 days after ayahuasca in humans; 4 hours after LSD

### Dose and Duration
- Both hallucinogenic and sub-hallucinogenic ("microdoses") can promote neuroplasticity
- Dose-dependent: 4mg/kg psilocybin required for gene expression changes (rats)
- Chronic microdosing effects may differ from single large doses — more research needed

### Consequences
- **Emotional regulation:** Enhanced dendritic and synaptic growth in PFC neurons — PFC essential for emotional regulation via connections with amygdala; depression characterized by reduced cortical neuroplasticity and PFC-amygdala disconnection
- **Cognitive flexibility:** Chronic LSD improved frontal-dependent memory retrieval; ayahuasca users show better behavioral inhibition, working memory, executive functioning
- **Fear extinction:** Improvements in fear extinction learning and reduced anxiety behaviors alongside increased dendritic density
- **Experience-dependent:** Neuroplastic changes are activity-dependent — the psychedelic experience during the "window of plasticity" may have greater psychological impact because the brain is more malleable

## Methodology

- Narrative review synthesizing animal studies (in vitro and in vivo) and human studies
- Covers LSD, psilocybin/psilocin, DMT, 5-MeO-DMT, DOI
- Published in Neuropsychopharmacology, 9 pages + references

## Relevance to This Work

**Central evidence for Section V — the biological mechanism behind psychedelic enhancement.** This paper provides the mechanistic backbone for why psychedelics can serve as cognitive enhancement tools. The "psychoplastogen" concept — substances that rapidly open a window of neuroplasticity — is exactly the kind of "seed and signal" our paper is looking for.

Key takeaways for our argument:
1. Psychedelics don't just alter consciousness temporarily — they physically remodel brain circuits
2. The effects are concentrated in the PFC, the brain region most important for the cognitive capacities we argue are needed (emotional regulation, decision-making, cognitive flexibility)
3. The plasticity window concept explains why a few doses can produce lasting effects
4. The experience-dependent nature of the plasticity means the *content* of the psychedelic experience matters — pointing toward psychedelic-assisted therapy as the delivery mechanism

## Questions & Follow-ups

- Most molecular evidence is from animal studies — direct human evidence for dendritogenesis is still limited
- The claim that microdoses promote neuroplasticity needs more human evidence
- How does psychedelic neuroplasticity compare to meditation-induced neuroplasticity? (bridges to Section VI)

## Key Quote

> "The window of neuroplasticity appears to open within a few hours and may last a few days, although neuroplastic changes occurring during this time may survive for at least a month. Because neuroplastic changes occur in an experience-dependent manner, experiences people have during this time may have a greater psychological impact than they otherwise would." (p. 108)

> "Classic psychedelics are thought to catalyze a period of accelerated neuronal growth, enhancing the brain's capacity for neuroplastic changes." (p. 104)
