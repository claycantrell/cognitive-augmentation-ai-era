## Summary

Open-label pilot study showing that a single high dose of psilocybin (25mg/70kg) produces sustained changes in affect, brain response to emotional stimuli, and resting-state functional connectivity lasting up to one month in healthy volunteers. Key finding: positive affect increased while negative affect and amygdala reactivity decreased at 1 week, with positive affect remaining elevated at 1 month. Global increases in functional connectivity persisted at both timepoints, suggesting a neuroplastic process.

## Key Findings

- **N=12** healthy volunteers (7F/5M, mean age 32.1), open-label within-subjects design
- **Negative affect reduced at 1 week:** PANAS negative affect F[2,20]=9.28, p=0.0014; DASS stress p=0.009; POMS depression p=0.004; state anxiety p=0.016 — returned to baseline at 1 month
- **Positive affect increased at both 1 week AND 1 month:** DPES joy p=0.007, content p=0.026, pride p=0.007, compassion p=0.003, amusement p=0.004 (Table 1)
- **Trait anxiety reduced at 1 month:** STAI trait F[2,20]=3.96, p=0.036; post-hoc: 1 month vs baseline p=0.024
- **Amygdala response to facial stimuli reduced at 1 week:** left amygdala t[118]=4.303, p<0.00005, d=0.79; right amygdala t[118]=4.199, p=0.00005, d=0.83 — returned to baseline at 1 month
- **Increased DLPFC and MOFC response** to emotionally conflicting stimuli at 1 week and 1 month — suggests enhanced top-down emotional regulation
- **Global increase in resting-state functional connectivity:** 695 edges significantly different from zero; 38 edges increased at 1 week, 29 at 1 month (Bonferroni corrected)
- **Personality change:** strongest effect on conscientiousness (d=0.738) and absorption (d=1.122) at 1 month
- Effects persisted well beyond drug elimination (psilocybin half-life ~3 hours, >50 half-lives by 1 week), arguing for a **neuroplastic mechanism** rather than residual pharmacological effects

## Methodology

- Open-label, within-subjects, longitudinal pilot study
- Single psilocybin session (25mg/70kg capsule), 7-hour monitored session
- Assessments at baseline (1 day before), 1 week after, and 1 month after
- fMRI during 3 emotion tasks: emotion discrimination, emotion recognition, emotional conflict Stroop
- 8-minute eyes-open resting-state scans; Shen 268-node atlas for connectivity analysis
- Limitations: small sample, no placebo control, open-label design

## Relevance to This Work

**Key evidence for Section V — psychedelics as enhancement technology.** This paper shows a single dose of psilocybin produces lasting (1 month) improvements in emotional regulation and brain connectivity in *healthy* people, not just clinical populations. The sustained increase in positive affect, reduced trait anxiety, and enhanced top-down emotional control (DLPFC/MOFC) are precisely the kinds of cognitive-emotional enhancements our paper argues are needed.

The neuroplastic mechanism is critical: the effects outlast the drug by orders of magnitude (drug gone in hours, effects last weeks), pointing to genuine brain remodeling — "psilocybin may have initiated a dynamic and neuroplastic process" (p. 8).

Global increase in functional connectivity supports the "psychoplastogen" hypothesis — psilocybin doesn't just temporarily alter consciousness, it may permanently (or at least durably) rewire brain connectivity.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- Small sample (N=12) and no placebo control — need to weight this accordingly
- The amygdala effects reversed at 1 month but positive affect persisted — different mechanisms at play?
- Would be good to pair with the Calder & Hasler 2022 neuroplasticity review for mechanistic explanation

## Key Quote

> "Rather than receptor trafficking or other residual pharmacological effects, the reported findings might better be explained by a neuroplastic period during which the neural processing of affective stimuli is altered." (p. 5)

> "The observed increase in functional connectivity strength indiscriminately across networks may reflect a domain-general cortical plasticity process supporting the observed changes in affective processing, consistent with preclinical evidence for psychoplastogenic properties of psychedelic drugs." (p. 8)
