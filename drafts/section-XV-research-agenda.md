## Argument

This section translates the paper's analysis into a concrete research and policy agenda. It identifies research priorities (which capacities matter, standalone vs combined, durability), educational priorities (governance-relevant outcomes, not just academic ones), and governance safeguards (cognitive liberty, informed consent, equitable access). The key principle: capacity-building should be treated as a public-interest issue, not a luxury market.

## Draft (~600 words, 3 paragraphs)

If human capacity-building matters for AI governance, then a responsible path forward requires not enthusiasm but a structured research and policy agenda. The research priorities follow directly from the analysis in this paper: which capacities matter most for AI stewardship, how those capacities should be measured, whether combined interventions outperform standalone ones, and how durable any gains prove to be over time. The table in Section VII offers a starting framework, but rigorous empirical work is needed to identify which capacities most strongly predict governance-relevant outcomes — sound judgment under uncertainty, resistance to manipulation, sustained cooperative behavior — as opposed to narrow performance metrics. The evidence reviewed in Section VIII suggests that combined models outperform standalone ones in several therapeutic domains, but this pattern needs systematic testing across the intervention categories this paper has identified. Durability testing — with follow-up periods measured in years, not weeks — is essential for any intervention proposed as governance-relevant. Research should also prioritize measures that distinguish genuine capacity-building from pseudo-enhancement: instruments that assess metacognitive monitoring, resistance to manipulation, cooperative disposition, and reflective judgment, not merely cognitive speed or symptom reduction. A realistic agenda also requires sequencing: lower-risk, scalable, and noncoercive interventions should generally be studied and deployed before more invasive or easily weaponized ones.

Educational priorities are equally important but operate on different timescales. Research on childhood mindfulness, executive function development, social and emotional learning, and civic reasoning education should focus not only on academic outcomes but on the specific capacities this paper has identified as governance-relevant. The key methodological challenge is distinguishing programs that genuinely strengthen metacognition, emotional regulation, and reflective reasoning from those that merely teach compliance or produce short-term behavioral effects that fade. The ideological sensitivity of educational intervention also demands care: programs aimed at cultivating civic reasoning or critical thinking must be clearly distinguishable from ideological programming, and research designs should address that concern directly.

A responsible policy framework must address safeguards and justice at the same time. Cognitive liberty — the right to mental self-determination — should be recognized as a foundational principle, alongside robust protections for informed consent, neural and psychological privacy, and freedom from coercive or manipulative applications. Independent oversight of high-risk interventions, particularly those involving neurotechnology or pharmacological modification, is essential. And the justice dimension cannot be an afterthought: if capacity-building tools prove effective, access must not be limited to elites, corporations, or militaries. The most promising interventions from a governance perspective may be precisely those that are scalable, low-cost, and noncoercive — educational programs, therapeutic frameworks, community-based approaches — rather than expensive technological augmentations available only to the privileged. Treating capacity-building as a public-interest issue rather than a luxury market is not merely an ethical preference; it is a strategic necessity. A governance system in which only some actors have access to enhanced judgment is not more stable — it is more fragile.
