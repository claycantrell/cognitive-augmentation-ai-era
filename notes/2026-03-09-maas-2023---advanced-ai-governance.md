## Summary

Comprehensive literature review mapping the emerging field of advanced AI governance research, organized into three major streams: (I) problem-clarifying research analyzing the challenges advanced AI poses for governance, (II) option-identifying work mapping the actors, levers, and mechanisms available for governing AI, and (III) prescriptive work articulating concrete policy proposals and priorities. Published as AI Foundations Report 4 through the Institute for Law & AI and SSRN, this review provides the most systematic taxonomy of the advanced AI governance landscape available, aiming to bring "greater analytical and strategic clarity" to a field that the author argues lacks internal and external coherence.

## Key Findings

- **Three-part taxonomy of AI governance research:**
  - **Problem clarification:** Technical parameters (capabilities, timelines, alignment challenges), deployment parameters (developer landscape, supply chains), governance parameters (stakeholder perceptions, regulatory landscape, institutional barriers), and cross-cutting lenses (theory, historical analogies, ethics)
  - **Option identification:** Maps key actors (AI labs, governments, international organizations, civil society) and their available governance levers, including direct regulation, compute governance, supply chain controls, industry self-governance, and international coordination
  - **Proposals:** Concrete policy frameworks including licensing regimes, international agreements, safety requirements, and institutional design
- **The field lacks internal clarity** — different research programs within AI governance often talk past each other because they operate from different assumptions about technical parameters and strategic priorities
- **"Different views of strategic parameters constitute underlying cruxes"** for policy effectiveness — disagreements about AI timelines, capability trajectories, and risk profiles drive divergent policy recommendations
- **Governance must address both existing and anticipated AI capabilities** — frameworks designed only for current systems risk being overtaken by rapid capability advances
- **Multiple governance mechanisms identified:** Direct regulation, new institutional creation (licensing bodies, safety standards), supply chain controls (compute hardware, training data), international coordination, and research community norms

## Methodology

Systematic literature review of the advanced AI governance field. Organized taxonomically by research function (problem clarification, option identification, prescription) rather than by topic area. Draws on academic publications, policy papers, and institutional reports. Published through SSRN and the Institute for Law & AI. Author affiliated with the Centre for the Study of Existential Risk (Cambridge), Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence, Institute for Law & AI, and Legal Priorities Project.

## Relevance to This Work

**Directly supports Section XII (Capacity-Building Within the Broader AI Governance Portfolio).** The manuscript draft positions human capacity-building as complementary to existing governance approaches, arguing it is "a background condition for the effectiveness of existing proposals." The Section XII gaps table lists "[NEEDED] AI governance framework overview — would anchor the list of governance strategies in the existing literature." Maas 2023 fills this gap precisely. It provides the comprehensive taxonomy needed to show what the existing governance portfolio looks like — alignment research, regulation, compute governance, international coordination — so that Section XII can argue that capacity-building is the neglected prerequisite running beneath all of them. Maas's observation that the field "lacks internal and external clarity" also supports the paper's broader point: if governance researchers themselves struggle to coordinate across research programs, this is itself evidence of the human-capacity bottleneck this paper identifies.

## Key Quote

> "As the capabilities of AI systems have continued to improve, the technology's global stakes have become increasingly clear. In response, an 'advanced AI governance' community has come into its own, drawing on diverse bodies of research to analyze the potential problems that this technology poses; map the options available for its governance; and articulate and advance concrete policy proposals."
