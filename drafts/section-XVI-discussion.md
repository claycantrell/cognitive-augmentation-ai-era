## Draft (~500 words, 2 paragraphs)

Much of the evidence relevant to this paper remains preliminary, uneven, or indirect. The paper does not claim that any single intervention will solve AI risk, nor that humanity must choose among education, therapy, enhancement, and regulation. Some interventions reviewed here may fail, prove difficult to generalize, or introduce tradeoffs that complicate their use. The chicken-and-egg problem identified in Section XIII — that better judgment may be needed to develop better tools for judgment — is real and may not admit any clean solution. These limitations are important to state plainly, because the case for human capacity-building does not depend on optimism about any particular tool. It depends on the narrower and more defensible claim that the human-capacity bottleneck is real and currently underexamined in AI governance discourse. Its contribution is therefore best understood not as a settled policy blueprint, but as a conceptual and strategic framework for research, governance, and institutional design.

What this paper does claim is that three distinctions should structure future work on this problem. The first is conceptual: the distinction between *agency-building* and *dependency-building* interventions, developed in Section V and applied throughout the paper. Not all enhancement is stewardship enhancement; the evaluative question is whether an intervention expands autonomous functioning or erodes it. The second is strategic: the distinction between *near-term adult intervention* and *longer-term developmental reform*. Both are needed; neither alone is sufficient; and the timeline pressures identified in Section III make the layering of these approaches a matter of some urgency. The third is ethical: the distinction between *support for human flourishing* and *systems of optimization aimed at control*. Section XIV made clear that the same tools proposed here could become instruments of domination, and the paper that proposes capacity-building bears the burden of insisting on this boundary. These three distinctions — conceptual, strategic, ethical — frame a research agenda worth pursuing even if many specific interventions prove ineffective. The question is not whether we know enough to act with certainty. It is whether we know enough to stop treating human capacity as a background assumption in AI governance, and to recognize continued inattention as a risk in its own right.
