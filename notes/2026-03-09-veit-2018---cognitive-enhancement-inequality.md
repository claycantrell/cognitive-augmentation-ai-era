## Summary

Philosophical analysis of whether the "inequality objection" (IO) -- the claim that cognitive enhancements will inevitably accentuate existing inequality -- justifies prohibiting enhancement technologies. Veit argues cognitive enhancements can be embraced for three reasons: (1) empirical evidence suggests enhancements may decrease rather than increase inequality (e.g., modafinil has diminishing returns for high-ability individuals), (2) even if inequality increases, benefits may outweigh costs, and (3) prohibition is unlikely to be the optimal policy response compared to regulation, subsidies, or universal access. The paper concludes that the IO raises legitimate policy design concerns but does not support outright prohibition.

## Key Findings

- **Formal structure of the IO:** P1: Enhancement accentuates inequality. P2: Inequality has negative social impact. P3: Technologies with negative social impact should be prohibited. C: Enhancement should be prohibited. Veit attacks all three premises.
- **Modafinil as equalizer:** Citing Randall et al. (2005) and Bostrom & Roache (2007), Veit argues modafinil has diminishing effects on those already possessing higher cognitive capacities, and can even lead to opposite effects in the highly intelligent -- so universal access would produce more rather than less equality
- **Genetic enhancement eliminates natural lottery:** Citing Bostrom (2003), Veit argues genetic enhancements could eliminate a major source of inequality -- the natural genetic lottery -- providing genuinely more equal opportunities
- **Enhancement as education parallel:** Enhancement technologies should be treated like education: children from better socioeconomic backgrounds already receive better education (a form of cognitive enhancement), and affirmative action addresses this -- similar solutions are available for enhancement technologies
- **Benefits outweigh costs:** Even a single cognitively enhanced scientist discovering a new disease cure produces substantial wellbeing gains; minor productivity increases (e.g., 10%) compound over time; competition drives innovation
- **Prohibition fails:** Draws analogy to US alcohol Prohibition and the war on drugs; black markets make enhancements more expensive and more dangerous; prohibition requires distinguishing treatment from enhancement (an arguably arbitrary line); must be globally enforced; creates punishment dilemmas (force DNA reversal? sterilization?)
- **Better alternatives:** Subsidies, free access for poor families (Bostrom 2003), regulated markets -- the "middle ground between open market access and prohibition" (citing Lamont & Favor 2014)
- **Welfarist definition of enhancement:** Following Savulescu, Sandberg & Kahane (2011), defines enhancement as "any change in the biology or psychology of a person which increases the chances of leading a good life" -- making medical treatment a subset of enhancement, shifting burden of proof to prohibition advocates
- **Free cognitive enhancements already exist:** Meditation research (Malinowski & Shalamanova 2017) shows many cognitive capacities can be enhanced through freely available practices

## Methodology

Philosophical argument and policy analysis. Not an empirical study. Engages with bioethics literature (Bostrom, Savulescu, Parens, Mehlman & Botkin) and some empirical pharmacology literature (modafinil studies). Structured as a formal attack on the three premises of the inequality objection, followed by policy analysis.

## Relevance to This Work

**Key evidence for Section XIV (dark side / equity).** This paper directly addresses the strongest objection to the manuscript's proposal: that cognitive augmentation will benefit the privileged and widen existing gaps. Veit provides the philosophical framework for responding to this objection.

Three specific contributions to the manuscript:

1. **The equalizer argument:** Some enhancements (modafinil, and by extension educational interventions, meditation programs) have larger effects on those starting from lower baselines, potentially reducing rather than increasing inequality. This supports the manuscript's argument that capacity-building programs targeted at underserved populations could be equity-enhancing.

2. **The prohibition trap:** Veit shows that banning enhancement technologies is likely worse than regulated access -- the same logic applies to the manuscript's argument that we should embrace cognitive augmentation under appropriate governance rather than resist it.

3. **Education-enhancement continuity:** By framing education as already a form of cognitive enhancement, Veit dissolves the artificial boundary between "natural" and "artificial" means of capacity-building, supporting the manuscript's integrated approach spanning education, meditation, pharmacology, and neurotechnology.

The main limitation for our purposes: Veit's analysis is somewhat optimistic about market mechanisms and does not deeply engage with structural racism, disability justice, or Global South perspectives on enhancement access.

## Key Quote

> "There is no empirical evidence that cognitive enhancement will lead to more inequality, rather I argue it has the potential to decrease it."

> "Even if the effects on well-being in the society through inequality are substantial, prohibition is very unlikely to be the best policy for maximization of well-being."

> "When one considers education a form of cognitive enhancement similar concerns arise. Children from a better socio-economic background do receive better education, benefits which can accrue over time. Affirmative action is widely accepted as an intervention to prevent these inequalities from becoming larger and larger. This gives us reason to believe that similar solutions are available in the case of enhancement technologies."
