## Summary

Johann Laux argues that human oversight of AI -- a central governance mechanism in emerging regulation like the EU AI Act -- faces a fundamental competence and motivation problem. Human overseers are often unable or unwilling to effectively supervise AI systems, due to automation bias, cognitive incompatibility with the systems they oversee, fatigue, and misaligned incentives. Rather than treating this as a reason to abandon human oversight, Laux proposes institutionalising *distrust* in human overseers -- borrowing from democratic theory (particularly Sztompka's work), where term limits, separation of powers, and accountability mechanisms institutionalise distrust in elected officials, paradoxically enabling justified trust in the system. The paper makes three contributions: (1) a survey of the EU AI Act's oversight provisions revealing significant gaps, (2) a novel taxonomy distinguishing first-degree (constitutive) from second-degree (corrective) oversight, and (3) six normative principles for institutional design that anticipate overseer fallibility. This is a normative legal and theoretical analysis, not empirical research.

## Key Findings

- **Taxonomy of human oversight roles:**
  - **First-degree (constitutive) oversight:** Human has counterfactual influence on the decision outcome. Involvement is integral to the decision itself. Examples: doctors using diagnostic AI, judges reviewing algorithmic risk assessments. Occurs *during* active decision-making.
  - **Second-degree (corrective) oversight:** Human intervenes *after* AI output is generated. No counterfactual influence on the original decision. Examples: audits, appeals processes, Meta's Oversight Board. Operates retrospectively.
  - Key distinction: First-degree overseers could theoretically prevent harm; second-degree overseers can only correct existing decisions.

- **Two-by-two challenge matrix:**
  - Both oversight types face (A/B) lack of competence and (C/D) wrong incentives, requiring different interventions for each quadrant.

- **Six normative principles of institutionalised distrust:**
  1. **Justification:** Institutions deploying AI must provide empirical evidence their oversight mechanisms function reliably, not merely assert human involvement.
  2. **Periodical mandates:** Rotating auditor assignments to prevent regulatory capture, though at the cost of competence continuity.
  3. **Collective decisions:** Distributing oversight authority among multiple decision-makers to mitigate individual corruption or bias; diverse groups improve epistemic quality.
  4. **Limited competence of institutions:** Preventing oversight bodies from expanding authority beyond their designated scope; enabling competing oversight providers.
  5. **Justiciability and accountability:** Complaint procedures, appeals mechanisms, and liability regimes allowing affected individuals to challenge failed oversight.
  6. **Transparency:** Disclosing whether human involvement is genuine or performative; revealing AI-assisted oversight tools; publicizing oversight procedures and performance data.

- **The oversight paradox:** AI typically enters deployment only *after* outperforming humans at specific tasks, creating a logical contradiction: the humans asked to oversee the system are, by definition, less competent at the task than the system they oversee. "Humans are mostly unable to perform their assigned oversight functions" due to this inherent performance gap.

- **Automation bias and algorithm aversion:** Untrained personnel over-rely on algorithmic predictions (automation bias) even when demonstrated as inaccurate. Conversely, domain experts may exhibit algorithm aversion -- preferring their own judgment despite algorithmic superiority. Both failure modes undermine oversight.

- **"Ironies of automation":** Keeping humans "in the loop" for safety often backfires -- humans become disengaged from systems they nominally supervise. Aviation research showed this led to transitioning toward "hard automation" that limits human override capability.

- **EU AI Act gaps:**
  - Article 14 mandates effective human oversight for high-risk AI but provides no certification standards, specific qualifications, or guidance on practical implementation.
  - The law demands overseers be "competent" and possess "AI literacy" without defining these terms.
  - A European Parliament amendment adds that human interference should be avoided if it "increases risks or negatively impacts performance" -- directly undermining override authority.
  - No technical standards (ISO/IEC SC 42) specify effective oversight implementation despite 20 published AI standards.

## Methodology

Normative legal and theoretical analysis. Laux: (1) surveys existing legal frameworks (EU AI Act, GDPR, Council/Parliament amendments), (2) synthesizes empirical research on human cognitive biases and oversight failures (automation bias, algorithm aversion, fatigue), (3) constructs a novel analytical taxonomy based on counterfactual influence, (4) applies democratic theory (particularly Sztompka's concept of institutionalised distrust and the "paradox of democracy") to AI governance, and (5) formulates design principles bridging political theory and AI regulation. No original data collection.

## Relevance to This Work

**This paper provides the strongest direct support for our human-capacity bottleneck argument.**

1. **The oversight paradox is our core problem:** Laux articulates precisely the challenge our paper addresses -- humans are asked to oversee AI systems that, by design, exceed their capabilities. Our paper's thesis is that cognitive enhancement is the missing piece: rather than only redesigning institutions (Laux's approach), we should also *upgrade the humans* performing oversight.

2. **Laux identifies the problem; we propose the complementary solution:** His six principles address *institutional* design to work around human limitations. Our paper argues this is necessary but insufficient -- institutional design alone cannot overcome fundamental cognitive limitations. The two approaches are complementary: better institutions (Laux) *plus* better-equipped humans (our paper).

3. **Automation bias and deskilling support our urgency argument:** Laux's evidence that humans are poor at oversight -- due to automation bias, disengagement, and the "ironies of automation" -- strengthens our case that the status quo is untenable. Without cognitive enhancement, human oversight will increasingly become performative rather than genuine.

4. **The EU AI Act analysis shows regulatory gaps we can cite:** The fact that the EU AI Act mandates human oversight without providing any mechanism for ensuring human *capacity* to oversee is a concrete policy gap our paper can address. We can argue that a cognitive enhancement strategy is the missing complement to regulatory frameworks.

5. **The democratic theory parallel enriches our framing:** Laux's application of Sztompka's "paradox of democracy" to AI governance provides a conceptual framework we can adopt. Just as democracies institutionalise distrust in officials, AI governance should institutionalise *investment* in human cognitive capacity -- treating enhancement as infrastructure for democratic oversight.

6. **Collective decisions principle aligns with our group-level enhancement argument:** Laux's third principle (distributing oversight among multiple decision-makers to improve epistemic quality) connects to our argument for enhancing collective intelligence, not just individual cognition.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- Laux's framework assumes institutional redesign alone can address the competence gap. What happens when even well-designed institutions cannot compensate for the fundamental cognitive limitations of all available overseers? This is where our enhancement argument enters.
- The "periodical mandates" principle (rotating auditors) trades competence for independence. Could cognitive enhancement programs reduce this trade-off by expanding the pool of qualified overseers?
- How do Laux's six principles interact with each other? Could transparency requirements (Principle 6) create information overload that exacerbates competence problems (Principles 1-2)?
- The Parliament's amendment undermining human override authority is a striking real-world example of the logic our paper warns against -- the temptation to remove humans from the loop when they underperform AI.
- Follow up on Laux's 2024 paper on "trustworthy AI" and the conflation of trustworthiness with acceptability of risk.
