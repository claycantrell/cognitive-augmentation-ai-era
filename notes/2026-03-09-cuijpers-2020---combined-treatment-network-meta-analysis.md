## Summary
Cuijpers, Noma, Karyotaki, Vinkers, Cipriani, and Furukawa conducted the first network meta-analysis comparing the relative effects of psychotherapies, pharmacotherapies, and their combination for adult depression. Analyzing 101 randomized controlled trials with 11,910 patients, they found that combined treatment (psychotherapy + pharmacotherapy) was significantly more effective than either treatment alone for achieving response, while psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy alone showed no significant difference from each other.

## Key Findings
- **Combined treatment vs. psychotherapy alone (response):** RR = 1.27 (95% CI: 1.14-1.39) -- combined treatment significantly superior.
- **Combined treatment vs. pharmacotherapy alone (response):** RR = 1.25 (95% CI: 1.14-1.37) -- combined treatment significantly superior.
- **Psychotherapy alone vs. pharmacotherapy alone (response):** RR = 0.99 (95% CI: 0.92-1.08) -- no significant difference between the two monotherapies.
- **Remission:** Combined treatment was also superior for remission rates (RR = 1.23; 95% CI: 1.05-1.45).
- **Acceptability (dropout):** Combined treatment (RR = 1.23; 95% CI: 1.05-1.45) and psychotherapy alone (RR = 1.17; 95% CI: 1.02-1.32) were more acceptable (lower dropout) than pharmacotherapy alone.
- **Long-term effects (6-12 months):** Psychotherapy showed slightly superior efficacy compared to medication alone at follow-up.
- The combination is described as "the best choice for patients with moderate depression."
- Pharmacotherapy alone is "probably not the optimal option" given the alternatives.

## Methodology
Network meta-analysis of 101 RCTs involving 11,910 patients with adult depression. Systematic searches of bibliographical databases for trials directly comparing psychotherapy vs. pharmacotherapy, or comparing combination treatment vs. either monotherapy. Main outcome: treatment response (50% improvement from baseline to endpoint). Secondary outcomes: remission and acceptability (study dropout). Used network meta-analysis methodology to enable indirect comparisons across treatment modalities. Published in World Psychiatry. Authors from VU Amsterdam, Institute of Statistical Mathematics (Tokyo), Amsterdam UMC, University of Oxford, and Kyoto University.

## Relevance to This Work
This paper is a critical citation for Section VIII (combined treatment superiority). It provides the most comprehensive evidence to date that combining psychological and biological interventions for depression yields outcomes superior to either alone. This directly supports the manuscript's core argument about synergy between capacity-building approaches: just as psychotherapy + pharmacotherapy outperforms either monotherapy, the paper suggests that integrating multiple enhancement modalities (contemplative practice + neurotechnology + psychotherapy) may produce synergistic effects. The finding that psychotherapy alone matches pharmacotherapy for efficacy, while adding psychotherapy to pharmacotherapy yields a 25-27% improvement in response rates, underscores the value of capacity-building (psychological skill development) as a complement to biological interventions.

The combination of psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy seems to be the best choice for patients with moderate depression."
