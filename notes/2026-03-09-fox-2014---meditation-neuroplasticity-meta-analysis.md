## Summary
Fox et al. conducted the first systematic review and meta-analysis of morphometric neuroimaging studies examining brain structure in meditation practitioners. Reviewing 21 studies (~300 meditators) and 123 brain morphology differences, they used anatomical likelihood estimation (ALE) to identify eight brain regions consistently altered in meditators, and calculated 132 effect sizes from 16 studies yielding a global medium effect size (adjusted Cohen's d = 0.46). While the findings are promising, the authors emphasize significant concerns about publication bias and methodological limitations.

## Key Findings
- **Eight brain regions consistently altered in meditators:**
  1. Frontopolar cortex (BA 10) -- meta-awareness and introspection
  2. Sensory cortices -- exteroceptive body awareness
  3. Insular cortex -- interoceptive body awareness
  4. Hippocampus -- memory consolidation and reconsolidation
  5. Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) -- self and emotion regulation
  6. Mid-cingulate cortex (MCC) -- self and emotion regulation
  7. Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) -- emotion regulation
  8. Superior longitudinal fasciculus and corpus callosum -- intra- and interhemispheric communication
- **Global effect size:** Raw d = 0.77; adjusted (conservative) d = 0.46 (r = .19), falling between "small" and "medium."
- **Gray matter vs. white matter:** Effect sizes for gray matter differences (d = 0.81) were significantly larger than for white matter (d = 0.73), though both are moderate.
- **Short-term training parallels long-term practice:** Brain changes after short-term meditation training in novices closely paralleled cross-sectional differences in long-term practitioners, supporting a causal role for meditation.
- Consistent differences found almost exclusively in higher-order executive and association cortices, not primary sensory or motor areas.
- **Publication bias** is a strong concern: funnel plot showed considerable deviation from ideal, with very large effects found exclusively in small-sample studies.
- **Negative correlation** between meditation experience and effect size (r = -.56), likely confounded by age-related brain atrophy in more experienced (older) practitioners.

## Methodology
Systematic review + two meta-analytic approaches: (1) Anatomical likelihood estimation (ALE) meta-analysis of peak coordinates from 21 neuroimaging studies to identify spatially consistent brain regions; (2) Effect size meta-analysis calculating 132 Cohen's d values from 16 studies. Studies used voxel-based morphometry (VBM), cortical thickness, diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), and volumetry. Conservative adjustment applied to correct for inflated peak-voxel effect sizes (~57% of raw values).

## Relevance to This Work
This paper is essential for Section XI (contemplative practice) of the manuscript. It provides quantitative meta-analytic evidence that meditation produces measurable structural brain changes in regions directly relevant to self-regulation, meta-awareness, and emotional processing -- precisely the capacities that the manuscript argues are targets for "capacity-building as governance." The medium effect size (d = 0.46) compares favorably with many behavioral, educational, and therapeutic interventions (mean d ~ 0.50 per Lipsey & Wilson, 1993), supporting the claim that contemplative practice constitutes a legitimate form of neuroplasticity-driven cognitive enhancement. The identified regions (ACC, insula, PFC, hippocampus) overlap substantially with networks implicated in executive function and emotional regulation, reinforcing the connection between meditation and governance-relevant capacities.

## Key Quote
"Meditation is consistently associated with changes in brain morphology... including regions key to meta-awareness (frontopolar cortex/BA 10), exteroceptive and interoceptive body awareness (sensory cortices and insula), memory consolidation and reconsolidation (hippocampus), self and emotion regulation (anterior and mid cingulate; orbitofrontal cortex), and intra- and interhemispheric communication (superior longitudinal fasciculus; corpus callosum)."
