## Summary
Cipriano et al. conducted the largest and most comprehensive meta-analysis to date of universal school-based social-emotional learning (SEL) interventions, synthesizing 424 studies from 53 countries involving over 575,000 K-12 students and 252 distinct SEL programs (2008-2020). The study found that students participating in SEL programs demonstrated significantly improved outcomes across multiple domains compared to controls, confirming and extending the landmark Durlak et al. (2011) findings with a much larger evidence base.

## Key Findings
- Students in SEL interventions showed significant improvements in: SEL skills, positive attitudes, prosocial behaviors, school climate and safety, peer relationships, school functioning, and academic achievement.
- Students also showed significant reductions in emotional distress and externalizing behaviors.
- Academic achievement effect size: Hedges' g = 0.082 (95% CI [0.009, 0.155], p = .029).
- School functioning: Hedges' g = 0.112 (95% CI [0.038, 0.185], p = .004).
- Programs that instructed all five CASEL competency domains (self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, responsible decision-making) demonstrated significantly better peer relationship outcomes (g = 0.225) compared to single-domain programs.
- Significant heterogeneity in program content, intervention features, context, and implementation quality moderated student outcomes.
- This was recognized as the Top Cited Article in Child Development for 2022-2023.

## Methodology
Systematic review and meta-analysis. Searched for experimental studies (RCTs and quasi-experimental designs) of universal (not targeted) school-based SEL interventions for K-12 students published 2008-2020. Included 424 studies across 53 countries. Calculated Hedges' g effect sizes. Conducted moderator analyses examining program characteristics, implementation quality, and contextual factors. Interdisciplinary team of 10+ researchers from Yale, Rochester, Maryland, and Loyola.

## Relevance to This Work
This paper is a key citation for Section X (education) of the manuscript. It provides the strongest available evidence that systematic training of social-emotional capacities in educational settings produces measurable benefits across cognitive, behavioral, and academic domains. This supports the manuscript's argument that human capacity-building is not speculative but is already being implemented at scale through SEL programming, and that such "low-tech" cognitive enhancement through structured training has a robust evidence base. The breadth (53 countries, 575K+ students) strengthens claims about generalizability and scalability of capacity-building approaches.
