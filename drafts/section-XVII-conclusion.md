## Argument

The conclusion restates the paper's core claim (human capacity is a governance variable), summarizes the three pathways, and closes on the agency-vs-dependency distinction as the paper's signature idea.

## Evidence Bridge

- This section synthesizes; it does not introduce new evidence.

## Draft (~400 words, 2 paragraphs)

Advanced AI creates not only a problem of machine capability but a problem of human adequacy. The governance challenges posed by increasingly powerful AI systems — from alignment and regulation to coordination and democratic oversight — depend for their resolution on human capacities that are not guaranteed and may be actively eroding. This paper has argued that human capacity-building deserves serious attention as a complementary dimension of AI governance: not a replacement for technical alignment or institutional design, but a recognition that the quality of governance depends partly on the quality of the minds producing it. Three pathways merit sustained investigation: assisted adult therapeutic intervention, which may strengthen judgment and self-regulation in populations already making governance decisions; adult enhancement, whose AI-accelerated trajectories require governance attention before mature evidence arrives; and early educational and developmental intervention, which offers the most durable, equitable, and institutionally legitimate route to cultivating stewardship capacities at scale.

The prudent response to these possibilities is neither techno-utopianism nor blanket refusal. It is serious, interdisciplinary work on which forms of capacity-building genuinely support agency, wisdom, cooperation, and democratic self-governance — and which merely deepen the dependency they claim to cure. The distinction between interventions that build agency and those that produce dependency is not a secondary concern; it is the central evaluative question for any capacity-building program proposed in the name of governance. At minimum, this paper has argued that human capacity should no longer be treated as a background assumption in AI governance: the bottleneck is real, the relevant capacities are at least partly trainable, and the consequences of neglecting them are too serious to ignore.
