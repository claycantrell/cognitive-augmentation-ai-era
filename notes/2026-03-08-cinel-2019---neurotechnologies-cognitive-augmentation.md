## Summary

Comprehensive review of neuroscience technologies for human cognitive augmentation, covering both brain-activity recording technologies (EEG, fMRI, fNIRS, MEG, ECoG, implanted microelectrodes) and brain stimulation technologies (tDCS/tACS, TMS, focused ultrasound, deep brain stimulation). The paper surveys current applications across communication, cognitive enhancement, memory, attention, decision-making, situation awareness, social interactions, and complex problem solving. Crucially, it provides a 20-year roadmap (to ~2040) forecasting which technologies will move from lab to field to routine use.

## Key Findings

- **No single neurotechnology is optimal** — each involves trade-offs between spatial resolution, temporal resolution, invasiveness, portability, and cost (Table 1, Figure 1)
- **Non-invasive recording:** EEG and fNIRS offer best current compromise for augmentation (portable, cheap, non-invasive). fMRI and MEG too bulky for real-world use.
- **Non-invasive stimulation:** tES (tDCS/tACS) most practical — portable, cheap, non-invasive. But significant variability between "responders" and "non-responders" (López-Alonso et al., 2014). TMS has better resolution but is bulky. **Focused ultrasound (FUS)** is novel and promising — spatial resolution as small as 1 × 1.5 mm, no effect on tissues traversed by beams (Section 2.2.1).
- **Invasive technologies** (ECoG, implanted electrodes, DBS) offer superior signal quality but require surgery — currently limited to clinical populations. Intracortical microelectrode arrays used to improve memory (Hampson et al., 2018) and even transfer information between brains in rats (Deadwyler et al., 2013).
- **tDCS cognitive enhancement results:** Accelerated threat detection learning (McKinley et al., 2013), improved verbal problem-solving (Cerruti & Schlaug, 2009), enabled 40% of participants to solve an otherwise-unsolvable puzzle (Chi & Snyder, 2012), improved planning task speed (Dockery et al., 2009).
- **Collaborative BCIs:** Groups using BCI-assisted decision-making outperform individuals — neural signals from multiple brains can be combined to improve accuracy in target detection tasks (Poli et al., 2014; Valeriani et al., 2017).
- **20-year roadmap (Figure 2):** Predicts BCIs for communication will become routine; neurostimulation-based enhancement will move from lab to field testing; attention monitoring and situation awareness will see routine deployment in security/defense; memory enhancement and complex problem solving will advance from lab to field.

## Methodology

Narrative review covering both observing and influencing brain activity technologies, organized by the cognitive function they augment. Includes a taxonomy figure comparing technologies on 4 dimensions (spatial/temporal resolution, invasiveness, portability) and a forecast roadmap based on: (a) 20-year development trends, (b) publication volume per technology/application, (c) predictions from existing roadmaps (BNCI Horizon 2020, Brunner et al. 2015, Wiseman 2016).

## Relevance to This Work

**Critical source for Section V (The Case for Cognitive Enhancement)** — provides the most comprehensive overview of what neurotechnologies exist for cognitive augmentation and where they're heading. Key uses:

1. **Survey of what works:** Concrete evidence that tDCS, TMS, BCIs, and neurofeedback produce measurable cognitive improvements in healthy humans — not just clinical populations.
2. **The FUS mention** connects directly to Sanguinetti's SEMA Lab work (Section VI) — Cinel notes FUS is "novel and still experimental" but has excellent spatial resolution. Sanguinetti's subsequent work (2020-2024) confirms FUS's promise.
3. **The roadmap** supports our "AI as accelerant" argument — if Cinel predicted these developments over 20 years using linear extrapolation, AI-accelerated R&D could compress this timeline dramatically.
4. **Ethical issues section** feeds Section VII (Roadblocks) — covers mind reading/privacy, agency/responsibility, safety of brain stimulation, societal dependence concerns.
5. **Collaborative BCIs** are a particularly striking example for our thesis — technology literally combining multiple human brains to exceed individual cognitive limitations.

## Questions & Follow-ups

- Hampson et al. 2018 (memory enhancement with implanted electrodes in humans) — should we track this down for Section V?

- How has the field actually progressed since 2019? A 2024-2025 follow-up review would strengthen our case
- The "responder vs non-responder" problem with tDCS is a genuine limitation worth acknowledging in Section VII
