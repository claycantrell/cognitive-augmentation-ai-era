## Argument

Contemplative practice provides the clearest existing evidence that stewardship capacities — attentional stability, emotional regulation, metacognitive awareness, compassion — are trainable. But traditional cultivation is slow, effortful, and not scalable. This makes contemplative practice valuable as a proof of concept: it reveals the targets and demonstrates trainability, while also motivating the search for scaffolding that can broaden access.

## Evidence Bridge

1. Long-term meditators show measurable differences in attentional control, emotional reactivity, and default-mode network activity — at least partly reflecting training effects.
2. Sanguinetti 2020 demonstrates that targeted neuromodulation (TUS) can alter mood and functional connectivity in healthy participants, showing that meditation-relevant neural dynamics are measurable and modulable.
3. Emerging ultrasound work (Lord et al. 2024, Ehmann et al. 2025 preprint) supports the possibility of transiently facilitating meditation-relevant states or phenomenology, but it remains unclear whether such interventions can produce the durable trait-level changes associated with long-term contemplative development.
4. Traditional contemplative cultivation requires years of disciplined practice within institutional and cultural frameworks that are neither scalable nor widely accessible.
5. This creates a paradox: contemplative practice demonstrates trainability but cannot address the scale of the problem.
6. The paper's intervention pathways (VIII–X) represent attempts to broaden access to capacities contemplative traditions have shown are achievable.

## Draft (~500 words, 2 paragraphs)

Contemplative practice occupies a distinctive role in this paper's argument. It provides some of the clearest existing evidence that capacities often treated as fixed personality traits — attentional stability, emotional regulation, metacognitive awareness, compassion — can in fact be systematically cultivated. A growing body of research reports measurable differences among long-term meditators in attentional control, emotional reactivity, and default-mode network activity compared to non-practitioners, and longitudinal studies suggest that at least some of these differences reflect training effects rather than self-selection alone. Research on transcranial focused ultrasound has shown that targeted neuromodulation can alter mood and functional connectivity in networks associated with self-referential processing, suggesting that contemplative-state-relevant neural dynamics are measurable and, at least to some extent, modulable [@sanguinetti2020]. More recent work in this line explicitly explores whether such neuromodulatory scaffolds can facilitate meditative depth and related phenomenology, including in designs aimed at reducing the barrier to entry for less experienced practitioners. Early studies and preprints suggest that focused ultrasound may deepen meditative phenomenology and may eventually help reduce the barrier to meditative development, but they do not yet establish that novice practitioners can achieve the trait-level capacities associated with advanced practice. The challenge is that traditional contemplative cultivation is demanding. The capacities it develops — sustained voluntary attention, equanimity under discomfort, metacognitive monitoring of one's own cognitive and emotional states — typically require years of disciplined practice, often within institutional and cultural frameworks that are neither scalable nor widely accessible. This places contemplative practice in a paradoxical position: it demonstrates that the capacities most relevant to stewardship are trainable, but it does so through a pathway that cannot, by itself, address the scale of the problem this paper identifies.

That paradox defines the section's contribution to the paper's larger argument. Contemplative traditions are valuable here not as a proposed solution but as a proof of concept. They reveal the targets — the specific capacities that matter and the fact that those capacities respond to sustained cultivation — while also demonstrating the practical limits of relying on individual discipline alone. The question this raises is whether therapeutic, pharmacological, or neurotechnological scaffolding can broaden access to capacities that contemplative practitioners have demonstrated are achievable. This is emphatically not an argument that technology can replace wisdom traditions or that a pill can substitute for years of disciplined practice. It is the more modest claim that if meditation research has identified trainable dimensions of human cognition and affect — as a growing body of evidence suggests — then interventions that support or accelerate access to those dimensions deserve serious attention, provided they meet the evaluative criteria this paper has proposed: net gains in autonomous functioning, metacognition, and resilience, rather than mere symptom relief or performance enhancement. The key unresolved question is whether interventions that modulate networks associated with contemplative states can produce durable trait-level changes in judgment and self-regulation, rather than only transient state effects. The contemplative evidence base, in short, tells us what is possible. The intervention pathways discussed in the preceding sections can be understood as attempts to make those capacities more widely accessible.
